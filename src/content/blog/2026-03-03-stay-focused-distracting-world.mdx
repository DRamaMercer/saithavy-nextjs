## Introduction

The average person checks their phone 96 times per day.

That is once every 10 minutes.

Each check takes an average of 5 minutes (checking, scrolling, switching tasks).

That is **8 hours per day** of phone time.

And each interruption costs 23 minutes to fully recover focus.

**You are not weak-willed. You are not broken.**

**You are fighting supercomputers engineered to hack your brain is dopamine system.**

The attention economy is designed to be addictive—social media, notifications, infinite scroll, variable rewards. You are up against companies with thousands of engineers and billions of dollars dedicated to one goal: **Keeping your attention.**

This is not a fair fight. But you can fight back.

This post introduces the **Focus Framework**—evidence-based strategies for reclaiming your attention, resisting distraction, and achieving deep work in an age of chaos.

## The Attention Crisis

### How Bad Is Phone Addiction Really?

**The data is terrifying:**

**Usage statistics:**
- Average phone checks: 96 times per day (once every 10 minutes)
- Average screen time: 3-4 hours/day (some people: 7+ hours)
- Time spent on social media: 2.5 hours/day average

**Cognitive cost:**
- Each interruption costs 23 minutes to fully recover focus (UC Irvine study)
- Phone notifications reduce IQ by 10 points (more than marijuana impact, King is College London)
- Heavy phone users show reduced gray matter density in brain regions for focus and impulse control (Institute of Psychiatry)

**Productivity impact:**
- Even checking phone 20 times/day = 460 minutes of focus recovery time lost = **7.6 hours** of mental energy
- Task-switching (multitasking) = 40% productivity loss, 50% more errors, 2x time to complete tasks
- Workers are interrupted every 11 minutes on average, take 25 minutes to refocus

### Element 2: Time Boxing

**Work in focused blocks, not open-ended stretches.**

<Callout type="tip" title="Parkinson is Law">
"Work expands to fill the time available for its completion." Without time boundaries, tasks take forever. With time boundaries, you focus and finish.
</Callout>

**Why time boxing works:**
- Creates urgency (Parkinson is Law: work expands to fill time available)
- Reduces perfectionism (limited time forces prioritization)
- Increases energy (sprinting vs marathon)
- Provides natural breaks (recovery is essential)

### Element 3: The 50/10 Protocol

**The optimal work-break rhythm for most people.**

<Callout type="info" title="Why 50/10 Works Best">
Body operates in 90-120 minute ultradian cycles. 50 minutes is substantial but not depleting. 10 minutes is enough for meaningful recovery (movement, hydration, rest eyes) but not so long you lose momentum. 4 hours of deep work in 50-min blocks = more output than 8 hours of scattered work.
</Callout>

**The protocol:**
- **50 minutes:** Focused work on single task
- **10 minutes:** Complete break (not "light work")
- **Repeat:** 3-4 cycles per day

<Callout type="warning" title="Break Activities - What To Do">
Movement (stretch, walk, jumping jacks), hydration (drink water), bathroom break, rest eyes (look out window, close eyes), fresh air (step outside), light snack (if hungry).
</Callout>

<Callout type="error" title="Break Activities - What NOT To Do">
Check phone (defeats purpose), email/slack (still work), news (adds stress), heavy thinking (brain needs rest).
</Callout>
