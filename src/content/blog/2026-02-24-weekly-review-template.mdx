## Introduction

Most people drift through weeks.

Monday arrives with good intentions. Friday arrives with "where did the week go?" Another week passes without:
- Celebrating what went well
- Learning from mistakes
- Course-correcting when off-track
- Consciously choosing next week's focus

Weeks blend into months, months into years. Life happens **to** you, not **by** you.

**The antidote:** Weekly review.

A weekly review is 60 minutes of intentional reflection that transforms weeks of drifting into weeks of growth.

This post provides the **complete weekly review template**—a framework for celebrating wins, harvesting learning, and consistently aligning your actions with your values.

## Why Weekly Review Works

### The Weekly Sweet Spot

**Why weekly, not daily or monthly?**

**Daily review is too frequent:**
- Most days don't have enough meaningful change for deep reflection
- Can become rote or skipped ("nothing different from yesterday")
- Decision fatigue from constant reflection

**Monthly review is too infrequent:**
- You lose 4 weeks of potential course correction
- Waiting a month to adjust means drifting far off course
- Problems accumulate before addressing them

**Weekly review is the sweet spot:**
- 52 course correction points per year (vs 12 monthly)
- Frequent feedback loops without becoming tedious
- Enough time per week for meaningful progress and learning
- Prevents problems from accumulating
- Aligns with how humans experience time (week is natural unit)

### What Weekly Review Provides

**1. Course correction:**
- Catch misalignment early
- Adjust before going too far off-track
- 52 opportunities per year to realign

**2. Evidence collection:**
- Proof that you're growing (even when it doesn't feel like it)
- Track progress over time
- See patterns you'd otherwise miss

**3. Intentionality:**
- Choose your week instead of reacting to it
- Set priorities before week begins
- Live by design, not default

**4. Emotional processing:**
- Integrate experiences through reflection
- Process emotions, not suppress them
- Extract wisdom from wins and losses

**5. Self-trust building:**
- Keep promises to yourself (weekly review appointment)
- Build consistency and discipline
- Prove you're someone who reflects and grows

## The Complete Weekly Review Template

Total time: 45-60 minutes

### Part 1: Celebration (10 minutes)

**Goal:** End negative bias by explicitly acknowledging what went well.

**Humans have a negativity bias**—we naturally focus on what went wrong, ignore what went right. This creates skewed reality: "Nothing good happened this week."

Celebration corrects this bias.

<StepGuide
  steps={[
    { title: 'Review Your Calendar', content: 'Look through your calendar for the past week. Notice what you actually did, not what you meant to do.' },
    { title: 'List Accomplishments', content: 'Write down everything you accomplished, big and small. Work projects, personal wins, habits maintained.' },
    { title: 'Identify Pride Points', content: 'What are you proud of? Not just outcomes—effort counts. Character moments, boundaries set, progress made.' },
    { title: 'Notice Surprises', content: 'What went better than expected? What pleasant surprises occurred? What unexpected gifts or opportunities appeared?' },
    { title: 'Celebrate Progress', content: 'What progress are you celebrating, even if incomplete? Partial progress counts. Movement in right direction.' }
  ]}
/>

**Questions:**

**1. What did I accomplish this week?**
- Big wins and small wins
- Work and personal
- Notice everything

**2. What am I proud of from this week?**
- Not just outcomes—effort counts
- Character moments (hard conversation, boundary set)
- Progress toward goals

**3. What went better than expected?**
- Pleasant surprises
- Things you worried about that went fine
- Unexpected gifts or opportunities

**4. What progress am I celebrating, even if incomplete?**
- Partial progress counts
- Movement in right direction
- Habits maintained

**Write it down.** Don't just think—writing makes it real.

**Example:**
- Accomplishments: Finished project proposal, exercised 4x, had great conversation with mom
- Proud of: Setting boundary with coworker, sticking to meditation habit even when busy
- Better than expected: Client meeting I was nervous about went great
- Progress: Book outline progressing (not done, but moved forward)

### Part 2: Learning (10 minutes)

**Goal:** Harvest wisdom from experience so you don't repeat mistakes.

<StepGuide
  steps={[
    { title: 'Extract Lessons', content: 'What were your 3 biggest lessons this week? From successes AND failures. What do you know now that you did not know Monday?' },
    { title: 'Analyze What Did not Go Well', content: 'What did not go well and what did you learn? Without self-judgment. Focus on learning, not blame. What would you do differently?' },
    { title: 'Identify Patterns', content: 'What patterns are you noticing? Recurring challenges, strengths showing up repeatedly, areas needing attention.' },
    { title: 'Note Surprises', content: 'What surprised you this week? About yourself, about others, about situations.' }
  ]}
/>

**Questions:**

**1. What were my 3 biggest lessons this week?**
- From successes AND failures
- About yourself, others, life
- What do you know now that you didn't know Monday?

**2. What didn't go well and what did I learn?**
- Without self-judgment
- Focus on learning, not blame
- What would you do differently?

**3. What patterns am I noticing?**
- Recurring challenges
- Strengths showing up repeatedly
- Areas needing attention

**4. What surprised me this week?**
- About yourself
- About others
- About situations

**Example:**
- Lessons: I work better in mornings, I need to say no more often, exercise boosts my mood
- Didn't go well: Procrastinated on big project until deadline pressure. Learned: Break big tasks into smaller chunks.
- Patterns: I consistently overcommit on Thursdays, need to protect that day
- Surprised: How much energy I had when I cut social media to 30 min/day

### Part 3: Feeling Check (10 minutes)

**Goal:** Connect with emotions and energy, not just logistics.

**Most planning ignores feelings.** Big mistake—feelings are data.

**Questions:**

**1. How do I feel right now about this week?**
- One word or sentence
- Notice without judgment
- Proud? Relieved? Disappointed? Neutral?

**2. What was my dominant emotion this week?**
- Anxiety? Excitement? Overwhelm? Peace?
- When did it show up? What triggered it?

**3. What drained my energy this week?**
- Activities, people, situations
- What do I need less of?

**4. What energized me this week?**
- Activities, people, situations
- What do I need more of?

**5. How's my stress level (1-10)?**
- Be honest
- If 7+, what needs to change?

**Example:**
- Feel: Relieved and proud—tough week but navigated it
- Dominant emotion: Stress (work project) mixed with excitement (new opportunity)
- Drained: Back-to-back meetings, news consumption
- Energized: Morning walks, deep work sessions, time with close friends
- Stress: 7/10—need to protect more downtime next week

### Part 4: Alignment Check (10 minutes)

**Goal:** Ensure actions match values.

**Your top 3 values** (identified in [Values-Based Decision Making](/blog/2026-02-10-values-based-decision-making) post):
1. [Value 1]
2. [Value 2]
3. [Value 3]

**Questions:**

**1. How did I honor each value this week?**
- Specific examples
- Even small ways count

**2. Where did I violate my values this week?**
- Without shame—just awareness
- What can you learn?

**3. What's one area where my actions and values are misaligned?**
- Need attention
- What adjustment needed?

**4. What values need more attention next week?**
- Which felt neglected?
- How will you honor them?

**Example (Values: Growth, Authenticity, Connection):**
- Honored Growth: Read challenging book, had coaching call
- Violated Growth: Procrastinated on learning new skill
- Honored Authenticity: Spoke truth in meeting, set boundary
- Violated Authenticity: Said yes when meant no (people-pleasing)
- Honored Connection: Quality time with partner, called friend
- Misaligned: Connection—didn't call mom, need to prioritize
- More attention: Authenticity—practice saying no more often

### Part 5: Forward Focus (10 minutes)

**Goal:** Choose next week's focus so you're proactive, not reactive.

**Questions:**

**1. What are my top 3 priorities for next week?**
- Not 10 things—3 things
- What must happen for week to feel successful?
- If nothing else gets done, what matters most?

**2. What do I need to let go of to make space for priorities?**
- Drop, delegate, defer
- What's on your plate that shouldn't be?

**3. What one habit will I maintain next week?**
- Non-negotiable
- What keeps you grounded?

**4. What's one thing I'll do differently next week?**
- Based on this week's learning
- Experiment or adjustment

**5. What support do I need next week?**
- Ask for help
- Resources, accountability, rest

**Example:**
- Priorities: 1) Finish client project, 2) Exercise 4x, 3) Complete book chapter 2
- Let go: Social media time, one non-essential meeting, perfection on lower-stakes tasks
- Maintain habit: Morning meditation (non-negotiable)
- Do differently: No meetings Thursdays (protect deep work day)
- Support: Ask spouse to handle dinner Tuesdays so I can write

### Part 6: Gratitude & Closing (5 minutes)

**Goal:** End on positive note, reinforce growth mindset.

**Questions:**

**1. What are 3 things I'm grateful for from this week?**
- Big and small
- People, opportunities, moments
- Specific is better than general

**2. What am I looking forward to next week?**
- Anticipation creates positive energy
- Even small things count

**3. What's my intention for next week?**
- One word or phrase
- Theme or focus
- Your north star for coming week

**Example:**
- Grateful for: Partner's support during stressful week, sunny Saturday morning walk, finishing proposal on time
- Looking forward to: Dinner with friends Friday, making progress on book, weekend downtime
- Intention: **Balance**—intense work + intentional rest

## Making Weekly Review a Habit

### When to Do Your Weekly Review

**Most common options:**
- **Sunday evening:** Prepare for week ahead (most popular)
- **Monday morning:** Start week with clarity
- **Friday afternoon:** Close loops before weekend

**Choose based on:**
- When you have 60 uninterrupted minutes
- When you're most reflective (not rushed, not exhausted)
- When review feels most useful for planning ahead

**Schedule it as recurring** in your calendar. Treat it as meeting with yourself that can't be double-booked.

### How Long Should It Take?

**45-60 minutes is the sweet spot.**

Time breakdown:
- Celebration: 10 min
- Learning: 10 min
- Feeling Check: 10 min
- Alignment Check: 10 min
- Forward Focus: 10 min
- Gratitude & Closing: 5 min
- **Total: 55 minutes**

As you practice, you'll find your optimal timing. Some do thorough reviews in 30 minutes (concise). Some prefer 90 minutes (go deep).

Start with 60 minutes and adjust. Quality of attention matters more than duration—focused 45 minutes beats distracted 90 minutes.

### Documentation: Keep It or Reflect Only?

**Both reflection and documentation are valuable.**

**Reflection in the moment** provides immediate clarity and learning.

**Documentation (writing your answers)** provides:
- Pattern tracking over time (see themes, progress, recurring challenges)
- Evidence of growth (when you feel like "nothing ever changes")
- Accountability (seeing your weekly answers creates commitment)
- Emotional processing (writing helps integrate more than thinking)

You don't need elaborate documentation—brief bullet points work. But writing down your answers, especially celebrations and learnings, compounds the benefit.

**Format options:**
- Handwritten journal
- Google Doc
- Notion template
- Audio journal (if you prefer speaking)

## Adapting the Template for Your Life

### Customize Based on What Works

**The template is comprehensive starting point, not rigid test.**

**Customization guidelines:**
- Keep what's useful
- Modify or discard what's not
- Add sections specific to your goals and life
- Remove questions that don't resonate or feel redundant
- Adjust depth (spend more time on sections that matter, less on others)
- Change questions to match your language and concerns

**Examples of custom additions:**
- Business metrics (entrepreneurs)
- Parenting reflection (parents)
- Training metrics (athletes)
- Financial review (anyone)
- Creative progress (artists, writers)
- Spiritual practice (if applicable)

**Your weekly review should feel like a helpful tool, not rigid test.**

Start with full template for 4-6 weeks, then customize based on what you actually use and find valuable.

If you never reference a section after 2 months, drop it. If you wish you had a section on specific topic, add it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q1: Why weekly review instead of daily or monthly?

**A:** Weekly is the sweet spot between frequency and depth.

**Daily review is too frequent:** Most days don't have enough meaningful change for deep reflection. Daily review can become rote or skipped ("nothing different from yesterday"). It also creates decision fatigue from constant reflection.

**Monthly review is too infrequent:** You lose 4 weeks of potential course correction, waiting a month to adjust means you can drift far off course before realigning. Problems accumulate for weeks before addressing them.

**Weekly review provides:**
- 52 course correction points per year (vs 12 monthly)
- Frequent feedback loops without becoming tedious
- Enough time per week for meaningful progress and learning
- Prevents problems from accumulating for weeks

The weekly cadence aligns with how humans experience time—week is a natural unit (work week, weekend, weekly rhythms). You get the compound effect of consistent reflection without the burnout of daily or the drift of monthly.

### Q2: How long should a weekly review take?

**A:** 45-60 minutes is the sweet spot for most people.

Less than 30 minutes tends to feel rushed and superficial—you don't go deep enough to extract real learning.

More than 90 minutes tends to become procrastination—reviewing your life instead of living it.

**Time breakdown:**
- Celebration: 10 min
- Learning: 10 min
- Feeling Check: 10 min
- Alignment Check: 10 min
- Forward Focus: 10 min
- Gratitude & Closing: 5 min
- **Total: 55 minutes**

As you practice, you'll find your optimal timing. Some people do thorough reviews in 30 minutes because they're concise. Some prefer 90 minutes to go deep.

Start with 60 minutes and adjust based on what feels useful. Quality of attention matters more than duration—focused 45 minutes is better than distracted 90 minutes.

### Q3: What day and time is best for weekly review?

**A:** Most common choices: Sunday evening (prepare for week ahead), Monday morning (start week with clarity), or Friday afternoon (wrap up week before weekend).

The best time is when you'll actually do it consistently.

**Consider:**
- When do you have 60 uninterrupted minutes?
- When are you most reflective (not rushed, not exhausted)?
- When does review feel most useful for planning ahead?

Sunday evening works well for many because you have weekend space and you're naturally thinking about the week ahead.

Monday morning works well to set tone for the week.

Friday afternoon works well to close loops before weekend.

Choose based on your schedule and rhythm, then block the time in your calendar as recurring (same time every week). Treat it as a meeting with yourself that can't be double-booked.

### Q4: Do I need to keep my weekly review answers or is reflecting enough?

**A:** Both reflection and documentation are valuable for different reasons.

**Reflection in the moment** (thinking through the questions) provides immediate clarity and learning.

**Documentation (writing your answers)** provides:

1) **Pattern tracking over time**—you can look back months later and see themes, progress, recurring challenges

2) **Evidence of growth**—when you feel like "nothing ever changes," your written reviews prove otherwise

3) **Accountability**—seeing your weekly answers creates self-trust and commitment

4) **Emotional processing**—writing helps integrate experiences more than thinking alone

You don't need elaborate documentation—brief bullet points work. But writing down your answers, especially celebrations and learnings, compounds the benefit.

**Format options:**
- Handwritten journal
- Google Doc
- Notion template
- Audio journal (if you prefer speaking)

### Q5: What if I skip a week or multiple weeks?

**A:** Life happens—you'll skip weeks sometimes due to illness, travel, crisis, or just being overwhelmed.

**The trap:** Making missed weeks mean you've "fallen off the wagon" and abandoning the practice.

**Instead:**

1) **When you return, do a "catch-up review"** covering the missed weeks. Don't try to remember everything—just cover:
   - What were the main themes of these weeks?
   - What did I accomplish (big picture)?
   - What do I need to carry forward?

2) **Recommit to regular practice.**

3) **Adjust if needed:** If you're consistently skipping, maybe weekly is too frequent—try bi-weekly. If you're consistently doing it, great—stick with weekly.

Missing a few weeks per year is normal and fine. Missing most weeks means the practice isn't working for you—adjust the format, timing, or frequency.

The goal is consistent reflection that serves you, not another item on your to-do list that you feel guilty about.

### Q6: Can I customize the weekly review template?

**A:** Absolutely—customize based on what works for you.

The template in this post is a comprehensive starting point, but your version may need different or fewer sections.

**Customization guidelines:**
- Keep what's useful
- Modify or discard what's not
- Add sections specific to your goals and life:
  - Business metrics for entrepreneurs
  - Parenting reflection for parents
  - Training metrics for athletes
- Remove questions that don't resonate or feel redundant
- Adjust depth: Spend more time on sections that matter to you, less on others
- Change questions to match your language and concerns

**Your weekly review should feel like a helpful tool, not a rigid test.**

Start with the full template for 4-6 weeks, then customize based on what you actually use and find valuable.

If you never reference a section after 2 months, drop it. If you wish you had a section on a specific topic, add it.

### Q7: How is weekly review different from quarterly review or annual review?

**A:** Weekly, quarterly, and annual reviews serve different functions at different time scales.

**Weekly review:**
- Short-term focus
- Immediate adjustments
- Tracks patterns week-to-week
- Maintains momentum
- Prevents small problems from accumulating

Questions like: What did I accomplish this week? What's my focus next week?

**Quarterly review:**
- Medium-term focus
- Assesses progress toward bigger goals
- Realigns priorities based on reality
- Measures seasonal energy changes

Questions like: What were my 3 priorities this quarter? What did I learn about my capacity? What are my priorities for next quarter?

**Annual review:**
- Long-term focus
- Celebrates year's achievements
- Harvests learning from entire year
- Sets vision and direction for next year

Questions like: What was my year about? What am I most proud of? What do I want next year to be about?

**You need all three** for different scopes. Weekly keeps you on track. Quarterly keeps you aligned. Annual keeps you connected to your bigger vision.

## Conclusion

Most people drift through weeks.

Monday intentions, Friday regrets. Repeat for decades.

Life happens **to** them, not **by** them.

**Weekly review changes this.**

In just 60 minutes, you:
- **Celebrate** what went well (correct negativity bias)
- **Learn** from experience (harvest wisdom)
- **Connect** with feelings (emotional awareness)
- **Align** with values (live intentionally)
- **Focus** next week (proactive, not reactive)
- **Practice gratitude** (growth mindset)

52 weeks × 60 minutes = 52 hours per year.

That's one full day of reflection that transforms the other 364 days.

**Your challenge:** Choose your weekly review time. Schedule it recurring. Do your first review this week.

Stop drifting. Start growing.

**Because weeks build lives. Make them count.**
