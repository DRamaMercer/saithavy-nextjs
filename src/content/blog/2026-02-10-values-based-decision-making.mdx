# Values-Based Decision Making: Live with Intention Every Day

<Callout type="tip" title="Quick Start">
Already know your values? Jump straight to the Values-Based Decision Framework. Need help identifying what matters most? Start with the Values Audit Framework below.
</Callout>

Most people live by default, not by design.

They react to opportunities, obligations, and expectations without asking: **Does this align with what matters most to me?**

The result:
- Jobs that drain them
- Relationships that don't fulfill
- Life paths that feel "off"
- Persistent sense of "is this it?"

They're successful on paper but misaligned in reality.

Here's the truth: **Every decision is either moving you toward your values or away from them.**

The problem? Most people have never explicitly identified their values. They're living on inherited values—from family, society, culture—without examining if those values actually resonate.

This post introduces the **Values-Based Decision Making Framework**—a practical system for identifying your core values and using them to guide daily decisions.

Stop living by default. Start living with intention.

## What Are Values, Really?

<Callout type="info" title="The Core Definition">
<strong>Values are who you want to be and how you want to live your life.</strong>

They're your north star—your internal compass for what matters most. Values are the direction you're headed, while goals are destinations along that path.
</Callout>

### Values vs Goals: The Critical Distinction

<Tabs>
  <div label="Values Are Direction">
    <p><strong>Values are ongoing directions:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Growth (not "achieve growth"—live in alignment with growth)</li>
      <li>Connection (not "get connected"—build meaningful relationships)</li>
      <li>Creativity (not "create something"—express your creative nature)</li>
      <li>Freedom (not "become free"—live autonomously)</li>
    </ul>
    <p>You never "achieve" a value—you live in alignment with it daily.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="Goals Are Destinations">
    <p><strong>Goals are specific endpoints:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Launch a business (achievement)</li>
      <li>Deepen relationships (milestone)</li>
      <li>Write a book (completion)</li>
      <li>Work remotely (arrangement)</li>
    </ul>
    <p>Goals are measurable achievements with clear completion points.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="The Relationship">
    <p><strong>Values should guide goal-setting:</strong></p>
    <p>When your goals align with your values:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Achieving feels meaningful</li>
      <li>Obstacles feel manageable</li>
      <li>Persistence feels natural</li>
      <li>Life feels coherent</li>
    </ul>
    <p>When your goals conflict with your values, achieving feels hollow—even if successful.</p>
  </div>
</Tabs>

<Callout type="warning" title="Check Your Goals">
Does this goal honor my top values? If yes → pursue. If no → reconsider. This simple check prevents years of striving toward achievements that won't fulfill you.
</Callout>

### The Inherited Values Problem

Most people have never explicitly chosen their values.

They're living with:
- **Parents' values:** "Make money, be stable, don't take risks"
- **Society's values:** "Achieve, accumulate, impress"
- **Culture's values:** "Marry, have kids, buy house"

**None of these are wrong.** But they're not necessarily **yours.**

Values you've inherited but don't actually embody create:
- Internal conflict ("I should want this but I don't")
- Self-betrayal ("I'm living someone else's life")
- Chronic dissatisfaction ("Why don't I feel successful?")

**The solution:** Explicitly identify your values. Choose what's yours, release what's inherited.

## The Values Audit Framework

### Step 1: Values Discovery Exercise

Discover your values through self-reflection, not logical analysis.

<StepGuide
  steps={[
    {
      title: "Peak Experiences Audit",
      description: "Reflect on times when you felt most alive, authentic, proud, fulfilled. Ask: What was I doing? Who was I with? What values was I honoring? Example: 'When I mentored that junior employee, I felt fulfilled' → Values honored: Growth, Teaching, Contribution."
    },
    {
      title: "Nadir Experiences Audit",
      description: "Reflect on times when you felt most drained, resentful, misaligned. Ask: What was happening? What values were being violated? Example: 'Working 80-hour weeks made me feel dead inside' → Values violated: Freedom, Balance, Health."
    },
    {
      title: "Anger Audit",
      description: "Anger often points to violated values. Ask: What makes you angry repeatedly? What value is being violated? Examples: Angry at injustice → Fairness, Justice. Angry at dishonesty → Truth, Integrity. Angry at wasting time → Purpose, Growth."
    },
    {
      title: "People Admiration",
      description: "Who do you admire and why? Ask: What qualities do they embody? What values do they live by? Example: 'I admire my friend who left corporate to start a nonprofit' → Values: Purpose, Impact, Courage."
    }
  ]}
/>

### Step 2: Values Prioritization

From discovery, you'll have 10-15 possible values. Now narrow.

<Tabs>
  <div label="Round 1: Top 5">
    <p>From your list, choose your top 5 most important values.</p>
    <p>Don't overthink this. Go with your gut. Which values feel non-negotiable? Which ones, if violated, would make you feel you'd betrayed yourself?</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> 15 values identified → Narrow to top 5: Growth, Authenticity, Connection, Freedom, Creativity.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="Round 2: Top 3">
    <p>From those 5, choose your top 3 non-negotiable values.</p>
    <p><strong>Why top 3?</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>You can't prioritize 10 things equally</li>
      <li>Top 3 clarify decision-making</li>
      <li>When values conflict, you know what wins</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> 5 narrowed to top 3: Growth, Authenticity, Connection.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="Testing Your Values">
    <p>Ask yourself:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Would I sacrifice for this value?</li>
      <li>Does living this value feel energizing or depleting?</li>
      <li>Would people who know me well guess these values?</li>
      <li>Does this feel like ME (not who I should be)?</li>
    </ul>
    <p>If the answer is "no," refine until it resonates.</p>
  </div>
</Tabs>

### Step 3: Values Definition

Define what each value means to **you**, not the dictionary definition.

<Callout type="info" title="Definition Template">
"[Value] means ______ to me. I honor this value when ______."

<strong>Example:</strong>
"Growth means continually learning, evolving, and expanding my capacity to me. I honor this value when I challenge myself, seek new experiences, and reflect on lessons learned."
</Callout>

<Tabs>
  <div label="Growth Example">
    <p><strong>Growth</strong> means "continually learning, evolving, and expanding my capacity" to me.</p>
    <p>I honor this value when I:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Challenge myself</li>
      <li>Seek new experiences</li>
      <li>Reflect on lessons learned</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div label="Authenticity Example">
    <p><strong>Authenticity</strong> means "living in alignment with my true self, not others' expectations" to me.</p>
    <p>I honor this value when I:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Speak my truth</li>
      <li>Set boundaries</li>
      <li>Make choices for myself, not others</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div label="Connection Example">
    <p><strong>Connection</strong> means "deep, meaningful relationships built on trust and vulnerability" to me.</p>
    <p>I honor this value when I:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Invest time in relationships</li>
      <li>Show up fully</li>
      <li>Share authentically</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</Tabs>

## The Values-Based Decision Framework

Once you have your top 3 values defined, use them to guide decisions.

### Step 1: Pause and Check

Before making decisions (especially significant ones), pause.

<Tabs>
  <div label="Ask These Questions">
    <p><strong>Ask yourself:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>"What decision am I facing?"</li>
      <li>"What are my options?"</li>
      <li>"Does this require values-alignment check?"</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div label="Not Every Decision">
    <p><strong>Not every decision needs this framework.</strong></p>
    <p><strong>Skip for:</strong> Routine daily choices, low-stakes decisions, emergency situations (act first, reflect later).</p>
    <p><strong>Use for:</strong> Major life decisions, commitments of time/energy, relationships, career/business choices, financial commitments.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="Decision Examples">
    <p><strong>Values Check Needed:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Accepting a job offer → Absolutely</li>
      <li>Starting a relationship → Yes</li>
      <li>Moving cities → Definitely</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>No Values Check Needed:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Choosing lunch → Probably not</li>
      <li>What to wear → Skip it</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</Tabs>

### Step 2: Run Through Values Filter

Rate each option against your top 3 values (scale of 1-5):

<Callout type="info" title="Scoring System">
<strong>5</strong> = Fully honors this value
<strong>4</strong> = Mostly honors
<strong>3</strong> = Somewhat honors
<strong>2</strong> = Minimally honors
<strong>1</strong> = Does not honor or violates
</Callout>

<StepGuide
  steps={[
    {
      title: "List Your Options",
      description: "Write down all viable options for the decision you're facing. Be specific. Don't censor options that seem unrealistic—include everything that's genuinely possible."
    },
    {
      title: "Score Each Option",
      description: "For each option, rate how well it honors each of your top 3 values (1-5 scale). Be honest. Don't inflate scores for options you want to choose. This only works if you're rigorously honest."
    },
    {
      title: "Calculate Totals",
      description: "Add up the scores for each option. The highest total score is your values-aligned choice. If there's a tie, look at which individual values score highest—prioritize your most important value."
    },
    {
      title: "Check for Deal-Breakers",
      description: "Is any value completely violated (score of 1)? If so, that's likely a deal-breaker, even if the total score is high. Don't violate your core values, period."
    }
  ]}
/>

### Step 3: Decide and Own

**Make the decision** based on your values filter.

<Tabs>
  <div label="Communicate Your Decision">
    <p>Communicate it without over-explaining:</p>
    <p>"I'm choosing this option because it aligns with my values of [value] and [value]."</p>
    <p>You don't need to justify your values. They're your north star.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="Own It Completely">
    <p><strong>No second-guessing:</strong> You made the decision. Trust it.</p>
    <p><strong>No seeking validation:</strong> You don't need others to approve.</p>
    <p><strong>No apologizing:</strong> Your values are your compass. You don't apologize for using them.</p>
  </div>

  <div label="The Principle">
    <p><strong>Values-based decisions don't require justification.</strong></p>
    <p>The people who matter will respect your values-based choices. The people who don't... why do their opinions matter more than your values?</p>
  </div>
</Tabs>

## Living Your Values When It's Hard

<Callout type="warning" title="Values-Based Living Isn&apos;t Easy">
Living your values will disappoint others who don't share them. It will require difficult conversations. It will mean saying no to good opportunities that don't align. But the alternative—betraying yourself—is far harder.
</Callout>

### When Values Conflict

Values conflict is normal and common.

<Tabs>
  <div label="Example: Freedom vs Security">
    <p>Both matter, but they pull in opposite directions.</p>
    <p><strong>Resolution strategies:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Seasonal prioritization:</strong> "This year: Freedom (entrepreneurial risk). Next year: Security (stabilize income)."</li>
      <li><strong>Integration:</strong> "I'll take calculated risk with safety net (honors both freedom and security)."</li>
      <li><strong>Hierarchy:</strong> "Security is foundation—I won't pursue opportunities that threaten basic stability. Within stability, I pursue freedom."</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div label="The Principle">
    <p><strong>Values aren't about perfect alignment</strong>—they're about conscious choice about when and how you honor each value.</p>
    <p>Sometimes Freedom wins. Sometimes Security wins. What matters is that you choose consciously, not by default.</p>
  </div>
</Tabs>

### When Others Disappoint

Living your values will disappoint others who don't share them.

</Callout>

### When Values Evolve

**Values can change** as you grow and your life circumstances change.

<Tabs>
  <div label="Annual Re-Audit">
    <p>Once per year, revisit your top 3 values and check:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Do these still resonate?</li>
      <li>Have my priorities shifted?</li>
      <li>Am I living these values or just professing them?</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div label="Life Transitions">
    <p><strong>Life transitions often trigger values evolution:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Becoming a parent → May shift from Achievement to Family</li>
      <li>Career change → May shift from Security to Growth</li>
      <li>Burnout/health crisis → May shift from Ambition to Wellbeing</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div label="This Isn&apos;t Fickleness">
    <p>This isn't being fickle—it's growing.</p>
    <p>Annual re-audits ensure your stated values match your lived values. If you've genuinely outgrown a value, release it and embrace the new one.</p>
    <p>You're not abandoning your values—you're evolving them.</p>
  </div>
</Tabs>

## Frequently Asked Questions

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Q1: What&apos;s the difference between values and goals?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> <strong>Values are who you want to be and how you want to live your life. Goals are what you want to achieve or have.</strong></p>
    <p>Values are the direction; goals are destinations along that path.</p>
    <p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Values:</strong> Growth, connection, creativity, freedom</li>
      <li><strong>Goals:</strong> Launch a business, deepen relationships, write a book, work remotely</li>
    </ul>
    <p>Values are ongoing (you never "achieve" growth—you live in alignment with growth). Goals are endpoints (you achieve the launch, the book, the remote work).</p>
    <p><strong>The relationship:</strong> Values should guide goal-setting. When your goals align with your values, achieving them feels meaningful. When your goals conflict with your values (pursuing money at expense of relationships), achieving them feels hollow even if successful.</p>
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Q2: How do I identify my core values if I don&apos;t know what they are?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> Many people haven't explicitly identified their values—they're living on inherited values (from family, society, culture) without examining if they actually resonate.</p>
    <p><strong>Discovery process:</strong></p>
    <ol>
      <li><strong>Peak experiences audit:</strong> When have you felt most alive, authentic, proud? What values were you honoring?</li>
      <li><strong>Nadir experiences audit:</strong> When have you felt most drained, resentful, misaligned? What values were being violated?</li>
      <li><strong>Anger audit:</strong> What makes you angry often points to violated values.</li>
      <li><strong>People admiration:</strong> Who do you admire and why? What values do they embody that you resonate with?</li>
      <li><strong>Values lists:</strong> Review common values lists (online resources) and note which resonate emotionally.</li>
    </ol>
    <p>From this exploration, identify 10-15 possible values, then narrow to top 5, then top 3. Test them: Does this feel like ME? Does living this value feel energizing or depleting?</p>
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Q3: What if my values conflict with each other?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> Values conflict is common and normal.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Freedom vs Security. You value both autonomy and stability—how do you choose when they conflict?</p>
    <p><strong>Resolution strategies:</strong></p>
    <ol>
      <li><strong>Seasonal prioritization:</strong> One value takes precedence this season, the other next season. "I'm choosing freedom this year (entrepreneurial risk), security next year (stabilize income)."</li>
      <li><strong>Integration:</strong> Find ways to honor both values partially. "I'll take a calculated risk that has a safety net (honors both freedom and security)."</li>
      <li><strong>Hierarchy:</strong> Rank values so when they conflict, you know which wins. "Security is my foundation value, so I won't pursue opportunities that threaten basic stability. Within stability, I pursue freedom."</li>
    </ol>
    <p>Values aren't about perfect alignment—they're about conscious choice about when and how you honor each value.</p>
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Q4: How do I make values-based decisions when other people pressure me?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> Values-based decisions often disappoint others who don't share your values.</p>
    <p><strong>Communication framework:</strong></p>
    <ol>
      <li><strong>Acknowledge their request:</strong> "I appreciate you thinking of me."</li>
      <li><strong>State your values-based decision:</strong> "This opportunity doesn't align with my current priority on [value]."</li>
      <li><strong>No justification needed:</strong> Your values are your north star, you don't need to defend them.</li>
      <li><strong>Hold boundary:</strong> If they push, repeat your decision without over-explaining. "I understand this isn't the answer you were hoping for, and I'm clear this isn't right for me right now."</li>
    </ol>
    <p>Some people won't understand or will judge your choice. <strong>That's okay.</strong> Their values ≠ your values. Living your values means disappointing others sometimes. The people who matter will respect your values-based choices even if they don't agree.</p>
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Q5: How often should I revisit my values?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> Values can evolve as you grow and your life circumstances change.</p>
    <p><strong>Annual values re-audit is ideal:</strong></p>
    <p>Once per year, revisit your top 3 values and check:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Do these still resonate?</li>
      <li>Have my priorities shifted?</li>
      <li>Am I living these values or just professing them?</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Life transitions often trigger values evolution:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Becoming a parent may shift you from Achievement to Family</li>
      <li>Career change may shift you from Security to Growth</li>
      <li>Burnout or health crisis may shift you from Ambition to Wellbeing</li>
    </ul>
    <p>Beyond annual formal review, pay attention to your daily life: Where am I consistently compromising or feeling resentful? What values am I violating? That's data that your values may need updating.</p>
    <p>You're not abandoning your values—you're evolving them. Annual re-audits ensure your stated values match your lived values.</p>
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Q6: Can my values change over time?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> Absolutely—values are not static.</p>
    <p>Your 20s values may differ from your 30s or 40s values.</p>
    <p><strong>Evolution examples:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Early career:</strong> Achievement, growth, recognition—building</li>
      <li><strong>Mid-career:</strong> Mastery, mentorship, legacy—integrating</li>
      <li><strong>Later career:</strong> Wisdom, teaching, generosity—giving back</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Personal crises often trigger values reassessment:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Divorce may shift you from pleasing others to Authenticity</li>
      <li>Health scare may shift you from Achievement to Wellbeing</li>
      <li>Burnout may shift you from Ambition to Balance</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>This isn't being fickle—it's growing.</strong></p>
    <p>The key is being intentional about values evolution rather than drifting. Annual re-audits ensure your stated values match your lived values. If you've genuinely outgrown a value, release it and embrace the new one.</p>
    <p>You're not failing at your old values—you're graduating into new ones.</p>
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Q7: How do I handle situations where no option perfectly aligns with my values?">
    <p><strong>A:</strong> Values alignment is rarely 100%—most decisions involve some compromise.</p>
    <p><strong>The values filter framework</strong> (with scoring system) helps you navigate gray areas.</p>
    <p><strong>Process:</strong></p>
    <ol>
      <li>Rate each relevant value 1-5 for how well the choice honors it</li>
      <li>Calculate total score</li>
      <li>Consider: Is any value completely violated? (deal-breaker)</li>
    </ol>
    <p><strong>If all values are partially honored but none fully:</strong> Ask: Can I live with this compromise?</p>
    <p><strong>Sometimes the answer is yes:</strong> "I'll accept some freedom limitation for this security opportunity."</p>
    <p><strong>Other times: No.</strong> "This violates my core value of Family too much."</p>
    <p>There's no perfect choice, only values-informed choice. Make the decision that honors your most important values most fully, accept the compromise, and move forward without second-guessing.</p>
    <p>You made the best values-aligned choice available.</p>
  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>

## Conclusion

Most people live by default, reacting to opportunities and expectations.

They make choices based on:
- What they "should" do
- What others expect
- What's easiest or most lucrative

Then wonder why life feels misaligned.

**Every decision is a vote for the life you want.**

The **Values-Based Decision Making Framework** helps you:

1. **Discover your values** through reflection, not logic
2. **Define what they mean** to you personally
3. **Filter decisions** through your top 3 values
4. **Choose with intention**, not by default
5. **Own your choices** without needing validation

When you live aligned with your values:
- Decisions feel clearer
- Life feels more coherent
- Success feels more meaningful
- You feel more like yourself

**Your challenge:** Complete the Values Audit. Identify your top 3. Define what they mean to you. Use them to guide your next significant decision.

Stop living by default. Start living with intention.

**Your values are your compass—use them.**
