# Async Communication Playbook

## Executive Summary

The #1 mistake remote teams make: treating async communication like sync communication. They expect immediate responses, run too many meetings, and create always-on cultures that lead to burnout.

**The Async-First Approach:**
- Default to written, asynchronous communication
- Meet only when necessary
- Document everything
- Respect everyone's time and focus

**Result:** Higher productivity, better documentation, more inclusive teams, and sustainable work.

---

## The Philosophy: Why Async Matters

### The Problem with "Always On"

**Traditional remote work traps:**
- Slack is always open, creating distraction
- Meetings fill calendar because "we need to connect"
- Immediate response expectations kill focus time
- People work odd hours to be "available"
- Burnout from constant context switching

**The Cost:**
- 23 minutes to recover focus after each interruption
- 50%+ of meetings are unnecessary
- Best work happens in deep focus, not constant availability
- People quit due to burnout, not workload

### The Async-First Solution

**Async-first means:**
1. **Default to async:** Written communication, your own pace
2. **Sync when necessary:** Real-time only for complex/urgent matters
3. **Document everything:** Create searchable knowledge base
4. **Respect focus:** Protect deep work time

**The Benefits:**
- Include all time zones (no one excluded)
- Better thinking time (not forced to respond immediately)
- Stronger documentation (decisions are written down)
- More inclusive (introverts thrive in async)
- Sustainable (no burnout from always being "on")

**The Trade-off:**
- Slower for quick decisions (but better quality)
- Requires discipline to document
- Takes practice to write clearly
- Feels "weird" at first (until you see the benefits)

## 📊 Response Time Guidelines

### Establish Clear Expectations

**Share these with your team. Post in a visible place.**

**Communication Channels and Expected Response Times:**

**Slack/Teams (Instant Messaging)**
- **Urgent:** Within 1 hour during work hours
- **Non-urgent:** Within 4 hours during work hours
- **Questions without deadlines:** Within 24 hours
- **Outside work hours:** Respond next work day

**When to mark as urgent:**
- Production is down or critical issue
- Security breach or legal matter
- Client crisis (if applicable)
- Decision needed today to unblock work

**When NOT to mark as urgent:**
- You're curious about something
- You want feedback "when convenient"
- You forgot to ask earlier (plan better next time)

---

**Email**
- **Standard:** Within 24 hours
- **Complex requests:** Within 48 hours
- **Weekends:** Not expected (unless truly urgent)
- **After 6pm:** Respond next work day

**Best Practices:**
- Use subject lines effectively (describe what's needed + deadline)
- One topic per email (easier to organize and respond)
- Include clear call-to-action (what do you need from me?)
- Set expectations if you need longer (e.g., "I'll review by Wednesday")

---

**Document Review**
- **Standard:** 2-3 business days
- **Rush:** Request explicitly (and explain why)
- **Large documents:** Request deadline upfront (e.g., "Need feedback by Friday")

**Pro Tip:** When requesting review, specify:
- What to focus on (not "review everything")
- Level of detail (typos only, or conceptual feedback?)
- Deadline (be realistic)

---

**Project Updates**
- **Daily:** End of day (or beginning of next day)
- **Weekly:** Specified day (e.g., Friday EOD)
- **Milestone:** When milestone is complete

**Format matters:** Use templates (see below) so updates are scannable and consistent.

---

### Time Zone Considerations

**The Golden Rule:**
Response times are based on the recipient's work hours, not yours.

**Example:**
- You're in SF (PT)
- Recipient is in London (GMT)
- You send message at 2pm PT (10pm GMT)
- Recipient sees message next morning (their time)
- Recipient responds by end of their work day
- **Total elapsed time:** ~20 hours
- **Conclusion:** Normal and acceptable

**Avoid:**
- Expecting immediate response when it's their evening/night
- Scheduling meetings during their non-work hours
- Marking messages as urgent just because you're working late

**Instead:**
- Include time zones in deadlines ("by Friday 5pm your time")
- Ask about their work hours if you're unsure
- Use tools like everytimezone.com to check overlap

---

### Availability Signaling

**Slack Status Best Practices:**

**Set status to manage expectations:**

"Deep work until 2pm PT - urgent? Call me"
- Tells team: I'm focusing, don't interrupt
- Tells team: Call if truly urgent

"In client meeting 1-3pm PT"
- Tells team: I'm unavailable
- Tells team: When I'll be back

"Offline until tomorrow - emergency? Call [phone number]"
- Tells team: I'm done for the day
- Tells team: How to reach me if crisis

"Parenting hours - back online at 1pm PT"
- Tells team: Why I'm away
- Tells team: When I'll return

**Benefits:**
- Reduces "did you see my message?" follow-ups
- Sets clear expectations
- Protects your focus time
- Respects your boundaries

## 📝 Update Templates

### Daily Stand-in Template

**Purpose:** Replace daily standup meetings with written updates

**When:** Post by 10am your time, read others' by end of day

**Where:** Team Slack channel or shared document

```
**Daily Stand-in - [Your Name] - [Date]**

**Yesterday I:**
- ✅ Completed [specific task - link to work if relevant]
- ✅ Made progress on [project - brief status update]
- ✅ [Another win]

**Today I will:**
- Focus on [specific task - priority #1]
- Work on [specific task - priority #2]
- Address [blocker, if any]

**Blockers:**
- None / [describe blocker + who can help + urgency]

**Team visibility:** Anyone working on [related project]? I have questions.

**ETA on current task:** [if relevant]

---
*Responses appreciated by: [time your time]*
```

**Team Protocol:**
- Everyone posts by agreed time (e.g., 10am)
- Read all updates by end of day
- React with 👀 or 👍 when you've read
- Comment if you can help with a blocker
- No meetings needed unless there's a blocker

**Time Saved:** 30-60 minutes/day compared to standup meeting

---

**Purpose:** Replace weekly status meetings with written updates

**When:** Post by specified time (e.g., Friday 3pm)

**Where:** Project Slack channel or shared document

```

**Status:** 🟢 On Track / 🟡 At Risk / 🔴 Blocked

**Completed This Week:**
- ✅ [Major win 1 - link to work if relevant]
- ✅ [Major win 2 - metrics/results if applicable]
- ✅ [Another completion]

**In Progress:**
- [Task name] - [Owner] - [Status] - [ETA]
- [Task name] - [Owner] - [Status] - [ETA]

**Upcoming Next Week:**
- [Priority 1]
- [Priority 2]
- [Priority 3]

**Blockers:**
- [Describe blocker + who needs to help + deadline + urgency]

**Risks/Concerns:**
- [Describe risk + potential impact + proposed mitigation]

**Wins to Celebrate:**
- [Share something great that happened this week]

**Need Input On:**
- [Decision needed + who + deadline]

**Next Update:** [Date/time]

---
*Comments/additions? Add below.*
```

**Team Protocol:**
- Everyone posts by agreed time
- Read all updates before Monday
- Comment if you can help or need clarification
- Manager/team lead consolidates into team summary
- No meeting unless there's a blocker that needs real-time discussion

**Time Saved:** 1 hour/week compared to status meeting

### Decision Request Template

**Purpose:** Make decisions async without meetings

**When:** Use for any decision that doesn't require real-time brainstorming

**Where:** Project channel or email

```
**Decision Needed: [Clear, specific title]**

**Context:** [2-3 sentences of background - why this matters, what problem we're solving]

**Constraints:** [Budget, timeline, or other limitations]

**Options:**

**Option A: [Name]**
- Description: [What this option entails]
- Pros: [2-3 bullet points]
- Cons: [2-3 bullet points]
- Estimated effort: [time/complexity]
- Cost: [if applicable]

**Option B: [Name]**
- Description: [What this option entails]
- Pros: [2-3 bullet points]
- Cons: [2-3 bullet points]
- Estimated effort: [time/complexity]
- Cost: [if applicable]

**Option C: [Name]**
- Description: [What this option entails]
- Pros: [2-3 bullet points]
- Cons: [2-3 bullet points]
- Estimated effort: [time/complexity]
- Cost: [if applicable]

**My Recommendation:** [Option X]

**Reasoning:** [Why this option - 2-3 sentences]

**Input needed by:** [Date/time + timezone]

**How to provide input:**
- Reply with your preferred option
- Add brief reason if you disagree with recommendation
- New options welcome if you see something we missed

**Decision maker:** [Person who will make final call if no consensus]

**Decision will be announced:** [Date/time]

---
*Discussion thread below*
```

**Team Protocol:**
- Everyone responds by deadline
- Decision maker reviews input
- Decision announced by specified time
- Decision documented in project docs
- No meeting needed

**Time Saved:** 30-60 minutes compared to decision meeting

### Feedback Request Template

**Purpose:** Get feedback on work without scheduling review meetings

**When:** Use for design reviews, document reviews, code reviews

**Where:** Relevant tool (Google Docs, Figma, GitHub) + notification in channel

```
**Feedback Requested: [Project/Document/Design name]**

**Context:**
- What this is: [brief description]
- Who it's for: [target audience/user]
- Why it matters: [impact/goal]

**Link:** [Document/design/file]

**Focus areas for feedback:**
1. [Specific thing you want input on - e.g., "Is the CTA clear?"]
2. [Another specific thing - e.g., "Does this flow make sense?"]

**Nice-to-have feedback:** [Optional areas if people have time]

**Please respond by:** [Date/time + timezone]

**How to give feedback:**
- Comment directly in [tool: Google Docs/Figma/GitHub/etc]
- Use [suggestion mode/comment feature]
- For major changes, start a new thread (don't rewrite everything)
- For typos/small fixes, feel free to edit directly

**Level of feedback needed:**
- [ ] Typos and grammar only
- [ ] Structure and flow
- [ ] Conceptual and strategic (this requires more thought)
- [ ] Rewrite/redesign (start from scratch)

**Questions for reviewers:**
- [Specific question 1]
- [Specific question 2]

**Next steps after feedback:**
- I'll incorporate feedback by [date]
- Updated version shared by [date]
- Final approval needed by [date]

**Questions? Ask below.**

---
*Thank you for your time and input!*
```

**Team Protocol:**
- Reviewers add feedback by deadline
- Discussion happens in comments (not meetings)
- Creator addresses feedback
- If major disagreement emerges, THEN schedule a meeting

**Time Saved:** 30-45 minutes per review cycle

## 📢 Communication Norms & Etiquette

### Core Principles

**1. Default to Public, Written Communication**

**What this means:**
- Slack channels instead of DMs (when appropriate)
- Written decisions instead of verbal
- Shared documents instead of presentations

**Why:**
- Creates searchable knowledge base
- Includes people who weren't involved originally
- Prevents "I don't remember saying that"
- More inclusive of different time zones

**When to DM instead:**
- Sensitive information (performance, personal issues)
- One-on-one conversations
- Brief coordination that doesn't need to be public

---

**2. Over-Communicate Context**

**Always include:**
- What it is
- Why it matters
- What you need (feedback? approval? just FYI?)
- When you need it by

**Example of too little context:**
"Here's the design: [link]"

**Example with good context:**
"Here's the new landing page design (link). We're testing whether adding social proof near the CTA increases conversions. Need your feedback on the headline and button placement by EOD Thursday. Design note: The blue matches our brand guidelines."

**Why context matters:**
- Reduces back-and-forth
- People can prioritize better
- Better quality feedback
- Shows respect for their time

---

**3. Be Explicit About Urgency**

**Urgency hierarchy:**
- **Truly urgent:** Mark as urgent, explain why, include deadline
- **Normal urgency:** No special marking, standard response time applies
- **Low urgency:** "No rush," "When you have time," "By [distant date]"

**Example:**

"Need approval on this by 2pm PT today so we can launch before the weekend."
- Clear deadline
- Clear reason for urgency

"Feedback needed by end of week - no rush, just whenever you have time."
- Clear deadline
- Clear that it's not urgent

**Don't cry wolf:**
- If everything is urgent, nothing is urgent
- Reserve urgent marking for true urgencies
- Your team will learn when to take you seriously

---

**4. Respect Time Zones**

**Best practices:**
- Never assume someone's "now" is their "work hours"
- Include time zones in all meeting invites
- Include time zones in deadlines ("by Friday 5pm your time")
- Use scheduling tools that show local times

**Example:**

Bad: "Let's meet at 2pm"
- Assumes 2pm what timezone? Yours? Mine?

Good: "Let's meet at 2pm PT (5pm ET / 10pm GMT)"
- Clear for everyone

Good: "Let's find a time that works - what's your availability like [dates]?"
- Even better, respects everyone

---

**5. Record Important Information**

**Always document:**
- Meeting summaries (even if recorded)
- Decisions (with reasoning)
- Agreements (what was decided, who does what, by when)
- Important links and resources

**Where to document:**
- Project documentation (Notion, Confluence, Google Docs)
- Team knowledge base (wiki, shared drive)
- Decision log (dedicated document for major decisions)

**Why:**
- People can reference later (didn't attend meeting, joined later, etc.)
- Creates institutional memory
- Prevents re-hashing old decisions
- Onboards new people faster

### Channel Etiquette

**When to use what:**

**Slack/Teams (Instant Messaging):**
- Quick questions that don't need documentation
- Non-urgent coordination
- Social conversation
- Time-sensitive updates (but not urgent)

**Email:**
- External communication
- Formal communication
- Communication that needs a paper trail
- Larger attachments or documents

**Project Management Tools (Asana, Jira, etc.):**
- Task assignments and tracking
- Project updates
- Sprint planning
- Bug tracking

**Documentation Tools (Notion, Confluence, etc.):**
- Permanent knowledge
- Process documentation
- Decision logs
- Team guidelines

**Video Calls:**
- Complex problem-solving
- Relationship building (1:1s, team social)
- Sensitive topics
- Brainstorming (small groups only)

---

### Thread Management

**Best practices:**

**Use threads in Slack/Teams:**
- Keeps main channel uncluttered
- People can follow conversations they care about
- Easier to find later

**When NOT to use threads:**
- Urgent announcements (use main channel)
- Time-sensitive information
- When everyone MUST see it

**Thread etiquette:**
- Start thread with clear subject
- Keep discussion focused
- Move to separate channel if thread gets long/complex
- Summarize conclusion in main channel when thread resolves

---

### Meeting Hygiene (For When You DO Meet)

**Before the meeting:**
- Send agenda and pre-work 24+ hours in advance
- Include goal: "By the end of this meeting, we will have [decision/plan/etc]"
- Keep attendee list to minimum (who is ESSENTIAL?)

**During the meeting:**
- Start on time, end on time
- Take notes (document decisions, action items, owners)
- Record if people couldn't attend (time zones, etc.)
- Stay focused on agenda (parking lot for off-topic)

**After the meeting:**
- Send summary within 1 hour (or by end of day)
- Include: decisions made, action items, owners, deadlines
- Post in relevant channel for visibility
- Update project documentation

**Meeting audit:**
Could this have been:
- An email? → Send email instead
- A document? → Create document instead
- A recorded video? → Record loom and share instead
- A threaded discussion? → Use async instead

**If yes to any of these, cancel the meeting.**

## 🚀 Implementation Guide

### Phase 1: Set Expectations (Week 1)

**Do this week:**
1. Share response time guidelines with your team
2. Post in visible place (Slack channel, team wiki)
3. Discuss and get agreement
4. Set up your Slack status with work hours
5. Try one async template (start with daily stand-in)

**Sample message to team:**

```
Hey team,

To help us all work better remotely, I'd like to try some async communication practices.

**The goal:** Fewer meetings, more focus time, better documentation.

**What this means:**
- Default to written, async communication
- Response time expectations: [link to guidelines]
- Daily stand-in posts instead of standup meetings (trial)
- Decisions via documents, not meetings (when possible)

**What I need from you:**
- Try the daily stand-in template this week
- Feedback on what works/what doesn't
- Patience as we adjust

**Let's try it for one week and reassess.**

Thoughts?
```

---

### Phase 2: Build Habits (Weeks 2-4)

**Focus on:**
1. Using templates consistently
2. Providing great context in all communications
3. Being explicit about urgency and deadlines
4. Documenting decisions and agreements
5. Setting and respecting boundaries

**Common challenges and solutions:**

**Challenge: Team isn't participating in async updates**
- Solution: Lead by example. Post your updates consistently. Gently remind others.
- Escalation: Manager reinforces expectation

**Challenge: People still scheduling too many meetings**
- Solution: Ask "Could this be async?" when invited
- Solution: Propose async alternative (document + deadline)
- Escalation: Team audit of recurring meetings

**Challenge: Urgency is overused**
- Solution: Ask "Is this truly urgent or just important to you?"
- Solution: Define what "urgent" means for your team
- Escalation: Manager calls out excessive urgency

---

### Phase 3: Optimize and Expand (Month 2+)

**Once basics are habitual:**
1. Audit which meetings can become async
2. Create team-specific templates
3. Refine response time guidelines based on what works
4. Share successes and learnings with other teams
5. Iterate and improve

**Measure success:**
- Fewer meetings (track hours in meetings)
- Faster decision-making (track time from request to decision)
- Better documentation (easier to find information)
- Higher satisfaction (survey team about work quality)

## 📋 Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Someone DMs you constantly

**Problem:** Colleague sends multiple DMs per day, disrupting your focus

**Solution:**
1. Respond: "I'm heads-down on [project]. DMs are breaking my focus. Can we batch questions and check in once/day instead?"
2. Set Slack status: "Deep work, checking messages at 2pm and 5pm"
3. If they continue: Direct conversation about boundaries and communication norms

---

### Scenario 2: Team expects instant responses

**Problem:** Slack messages get angry replies if you don't respond in 5 minutes

**Solution:**
1. Share response time guidelines with team
2. Set boundaries: "I check Slack at 10am, 2pm, 5pm. Urgent? Call me."
3. Be consistent: Stick to your stated times
4. Enforce: Don't respond instantly (reinforces expectation)
5. Escalate if needed: Manager reinforces boundaries

---

### Scenario 3: Decisions keep getting revisited

**Problem:** Team rehashes decisions that were already made

**Solution:**
1. Create decision log: Document all decisions with reasoning
2. When decision is revisited: Link to decision log
3. "We decided X on [date] because [reasoning]. What's changed that makes us reconsider?"
4. If change is warranted: Update decision log with new decision

---

### Scenario 4: Information is scattered everywhere

**Problem:** Can't find information, decisions, resources

**Solution:**
1. Centralize documentation: One source of truth (Notion, Confluence, etc.)
2. Create team knowledge base: Organized by topic
3. Link to docs from Slack channels (pinned messages)
4. Make documentation part of process (document after every meeting/decision)
5. Regular cleanup: Archive or delete outdated info

---

### Scenario 5: Remote team feels disconnected

**Problem:** Async communication feels impersonal, team lacks cohesion

**Solution:**
1. Keep some meetings: 1:1s, team social, complex collaboration
2. Async social: Share photos, pets, wins in dedicated channel
3. Virtual office hours: Optional time to connect live
4. Team retreats: Quarterly or annual in-person meetups
5. Balance: Async for work, sync for relationship-building

## Troubleshooting

### Problem: Async communication feels slow

**Possible causes:**
- Waiting too long for responses (people aren't checking async channels)
- Unclear deadlines (people don't know when to respond)
- Poor context (people need clarification)

**Solutions:**
- Set clear deadlines with time zones
- Be explicit about what you need and when
- Provide thorough context upfront
- If truly urgent, pick up the phone

---

### Problem: People aren't participating in async updates

**Possible causes:**
- Too much friction (templates are too complex)
- No accountability (no consequences for not participating)
- Unclear value (people don't see the point)

**Solutions:**
- Simplify templates (make them easy to complete)
- Manager reinforces expectation (makes it mandatory)
- Show value (share success stories, time saved)
- Lead by example (you do it consistently)

---

### Problem: Too much documentation, can't keep up

**Possible causes:**
- Documenting everything, not just important things
- Poor organization (can't find what you need)
- No filtering (everything is "must read")

**Solutions:**
- Prioritize: Document decisions, agreements, important info (not everything)
- Organize: Clear structure, good search, tagging
- Filter: Channel for announcements, channel for discussion, etc.
- Summarize: Weekly digest of important updates

---

### Problem: Async works for some topics, not others

**Diagnosis:**
- Works well: Updates, decisions, feedback, announcements
- Doesn't work: Complex collaboration, brainstorming, sensitive topics

**Solution:**
- Don't force async for everything
- Use async for 70-80% of communication
- Keep sync for the 20-30% that truly require it
- The goal is fewer/better meetings, not zero meetings

## Conclusion

Async communication is a skill. It takes practice.

**Start small:**
- Use one template consistently
- Over-communicate context
- Set and respect boundaries
- Document decisions

**Build momentum:**
- Replace a few meetings with async alternatives
- Refine what works for your team
- Share successes and learnings

**The result:**
- More focus time
- Better documentation
- Inclusive of all time zones
- Sustainable remote work

**The key insight:**
Async-first isn't about NEVER meeting.
It's about meeting when necessary, not by default.

Use these templates and guidelines as a starting point.
Adapt them to your team's culture and needs.
Experiment, learn, iterate.

Remote work is better when it's async-first.
