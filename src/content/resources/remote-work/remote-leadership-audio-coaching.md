## Session 1: The Remote Leadership Mindset Shift

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Welcome to Remote Leadership Coaching. I'm glad you're here. Leading a distributed team is different—not harder, not easier, just different. And most leaders are trying to lead remotely the same way they led in person. That doesn't work.

Today, I want to talk about the fundamental mindset shift you need to make as a remote leader.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what I want you to consider: In an office, leadership often looks like visibility—walking around, being present, popping into meetings. When you lead remotely, visibility doesn't work the same way. You can't be everywhere. You shouldn't try.

The shift is this: From presence-based leadership to outcome-based leadership.

**[PAUSE]**

Presence-based leadership asks: "Is everyone working? Are they at their desks? Are they responsive?"

Outcome-based leadership asks: "What are we achieving? Is the work getting done? Are people growing?"

**[PAUSE]**

Here's your first reflection question: Are you currently leading for presence or outcomes? Be honest with yourself. If someone stopped working for two hours but delivered excellent work, would you be okay with that? If your answer is "it depends" or "no," you might still be rooted in presence-based thinking.

**[LONGER PAUSE]**

Here's what I want you to try this week: Pick one outcome focus for your team. State it clearly. "This week, our success metric is X." Then let people figure out their own path to that outcome. Notice how it feels to release control of the "how" and focus only on the "what."

**[PAUSE]**

Before we end, I want you to write down one thing you'll do differently this week to shift from presence to outcomes. Ready? Go ahead and pause this recording and write it down.

**[LONG PAUSE FOR WRITING]**

Good. That's your commitment for the week. We'll build on this in our next session.

**[OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN]**

## Session 2: Building Trust Across Distance

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Welcome back. Today we're tackling the biggest challenge in remote leadership: trust.

When you can't see people working, trust becomes the foundation of everything. The problem is, most leaders destroy trust accidentally when they go remote.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's the trust equation for remote leaders:

Trust equals visibility of your work plus transparency of your decisions plus consistency of your communication, divided by how much you check up on people.

**[PAUSE]**

Let me say that differently: When you micromanage, you signal mistrust. When you're transparent about decisions and consistent in communication, you build trust.

**[PAUSE]**

Here are three trust-destroyers I see remote leaders make all the time:

Number one: Tracking activity instead of outcomes. Monitoring when people are online, counting their messages, watching their status indicators. This screams "I don't trust you."

Number two: Holding information close. Making decisions behind closed doors and announcing them. This creates uncertainty and speculation.

Number three: Inconsistent communication. Being super responsive one day, ghosting the next. This creates anxiety.

**[PAUSE]**

Instead, try these three trust-builders:

First, default to transparency. Share the thinking behind decisions. Document where information lives. Make your work visible.

Second, communicate predictably. Have regular touchpoints. Show up when you say you will. If you can't, communicate that.

Third, focus on outcomes, not activity. Celebrate results, not hours worked. Make it clear that output matters more than input.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's your challenge this week: Identify one trust-destroying behavior you might be doing. Maybe it's checking Slack status too much. Maybe it's holding back information. Maybe it's unpredictable availability. Pick one and replace it with the trust-building alternative.

**[PAUSE]**

Before we continue, I want you to think about: On a scale of 1-10, how much does your team trust you? Be honest. Then ask yourself: What would make it a 10?

**[LONG PAUSE]**

That second question—that's your roadmap. Write down one action you'll take this week to move closer to a 10.

**[OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN]**

## Session 3: Communication That Actually Works Remotely

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Today we're talking about communication, but not in the way you might think. We're not talking about being a better communicator. We're talking about communication architecture.

Most remote leaders try to improve communication by doing MORE of it. More meetings, more messages, more updates. But more communication isn't better communication. Better architecture is.

**[PAUSE]**

Let me give you a framework. Every communication in your team should answer three questions:

One: What channel is this for?

Two: What response time is expected?

Three: What happens with this information?

If you can't answer those three questions, your communication architecture is broken.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what this looks like in practice:

For urgent issues, you have a direct channel. Expected response: immediately. What happens: problem gets solved.

For normal work, you have async channels. Expected response: within a few hours. What happens: work moves forward.

For updates, you have a broadcast channel. Expected response: none, just read. What happens: everyone stays informed.

**[PAUSE]**

The problem most teams have: Everything goes into the same channel. Urgent and non-urgent mixed together. No clear expectations. People don't know how to prioritize.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's your action item this week: Map your communication. List every channel your team uses—Slack channels, email, meetings, whatever. For each one, answer the three questions: What's this channel for? What response time is expected? What happens with this information?

If you can't answer clearly for a channel, that's a problem. Fix it or kill it.

**[PAUSE]**

I also want you to have a conversation with your team about this. Share your communication architecture. Get their input. Make sure everyone understands the expectations.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's a question to reflect on: When your team wakes up and opens their communication tools, do they feel overwhelmed or organized? If it's overwhelmed, you have an architecture problem, not a communication volume problem.

**[OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN]**

## Session 4: Managing Performance Without Micromanaging

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Today we're addressing the fear that keeps remote leaders up at night: "How do I know people are working if I can't see them?"

Here's the uncomfortable truth: You've never actually known if people were working when you were in the same office. You knew they were present. You knew they were at their desk. But you didn't know if they were productive. Remote work just makes this visible.

**[PAUSE]**

The shift you need to make is from monitoring behavior to measuring outcomes.

Behavior monitoring asks: Are they online? Are they responsive? Are they at their computer?

Outcome measurement asks: What did they deliver? What impact did it have? Did they meet their commitments?

**[PAUSE]**

Here's a framework I call the Output Agreement. When you assign work, you should agree on four things:

First: What does "done" look like? Be specific.

Second: What's the impact we're creating? Not just the task, but the value.

Third: When will it be delivered? A real deadline, not "soon."

Fourth: How will we know it's good quality? What are the criteria?

If you can't answer these four questions, you don't have an assignment—you have a hope.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what this sounds like in practice:

Instead of: "Can you work on that project proposal?"

Try: "I need a project proposal that addresses X problem for Y stakeholder. It needs to include budget, timeline, and risks. I need it by Wednesday at noon. I'll know it's good when Y stakeholder says it answers their questions."

**[PAUSE]**

See the difference? The first is a task assignment. The second is an outcome agreement.

**[PAUSE]**

Your challenge this week: Take your next three assignments and rewrite them using this framework. Be specific about done, impact, deadline, and quality criteria. Notice how much clearer both you and your team become.

**[PAUSE]**

I also want you to reflect on: Are you holding people accountable for outputs, or are you judging them for their process? Some people work at night. Some people work in bursts. Some people need to think before they act. As long as the output is there, does the process matter?

**[OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN]**

## Session 5: Leading Across Time Zones

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Today we're talking about time zones. Leading a distributed team across time zones is a skill. It requires you to think differently about work, communication, and fairness.

The biggest mistake leaders make: They design their team's rhythms around their own time zone. This creates a two-tier system—some people get normal hours, others get inconvenient ones. That's not sustainable.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's the principle: Share the inconvenience.

If you need a meeting that's early for some and late for others, rotate it. If this week's meeting is at 7 AM for you and 7 PM for them, next week make it 7 PM for you and 7 AM for them.

**[PAUSE]**

I worked with a leader who had team members in London, New York, and Los Angeles. The London team had 6 AM meetings for years. The leader lived in New York. When I pointed out the unfairness, she was genuinely shocked. She'd never thought about it from their perspective.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's your framework for time zone fairness:

First, audit your meeting times. Who's inconvenienced? How often?

Second, create a rotation schedule. Spread the inconvenience.

Third, default to async when possible. If you can solve it without a meeting, don't have one.

Fourth, be explicit about time zones. Always say "9 AM Eastern, which is 6 AM Pacific" or whatever the conversion is. Don't make people do the math.

**[PAUSE]**

Your action this week: Map your team's time zones. Visualize where everyone is. Look at your meeting schedule through their eyes. What's it like to be them?

**[PAUSE]**

Then make one change to share the inconvenience. Rotate a meeting. Move something to async. Make someone's life a little easier.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's something I want you to reflect on: Does everyone on your team have equal access to you? Or do some people have to work harder to reach you? If there's an imbalance, that's on you to fix.

## Session 6: Remote One-on-Ones That Matter

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** One-on-ones are more important when you're remote, not less. They're your primary connection to each person's experience, challenges, and growth. But most leaders treat remote 1:1s as status updates. That's a waste.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's the principle: One-on-ones are for the direct report, not for you.

If you're spending most of the time getting project updates, you're doing it wrong. Project updates can happen async. One-on-ones are for connection, development, and the things that don't fit anywhere else.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's my framework for remote 1:1s that matter:

Start with a check-in: Not "how's the project?" but "how are you?" genuinely. Notice tone, energy, stress levels.

Then ask: What's on your mind? And let them lead. Your job is to listen, not to steer.

Then ask: What support do you need? This is where you remove blockers.

Then ask: How are you growing? This is where you coach.

End with: What's one thing you'll do before we meet again?

**[PAUSE]**

The whole thing should be 30 minutes, max. But the quality of those 30 minutes matters more than any other meeting you'll have.

**[PAUSE]**

For remote 1:1s specifically, here are some tips:

Video on, always. You need to see faces, not just hear voices.

Have a standing agenda, but be flexible to what's actually needed.

Use a shared document for notes. Both of you can see it, both contribute.

Schedule them at a frequency that works—weekly for new reports, bi-weekly for established ones.

**[PAUSE]**

Your challenge this week: Audit your 1:1s. Are they serving your direct reports or serving you? If you're doing most of the talking, that's backwards. If it's all project updates, that's missed opportunity.

**[PAUSE]**

Try this format for your next three 1:1s and notice the difference.

## Session 7: Building Remote Team Culture

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Culture doesn't happen by accident in an office, but it feels like it does. In remote work, culture absolutely will not happen without intention. You have to design it.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what remote culture actually is: It's the worst behavior you tolerate as a leader.

In offices, social pressure regulates behavior. People see how others act, they adjust. In remote teams, that social pressure is gone. The only regulator is what you allow.

**[PAUSE]**

So the question becomes: What are you tolerating?

If someone's consistently rude in async channels and you don't address it, that's your culture now.

If someone misses deadlines and there are no consequences, that's your culture.

If some people are always available and others aren't, and you say nothing, that's your culture.

**[PAUSE]**

Culture is built in three ways: What you celebrate, what you tolerate, and what you ignore.

What you celebrate becomes valued.

What you tolerate becomes accepted.

What you ignore becomes invisible.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's your framework for building remote culture:

First, define your cultural norms explicitly. Don't assume people know. Write them down.

Second, celebrate behaviors that align with your culture. Be specific about what you're celebrating and why.

Third, address misalignments quickly. Have the awkward conversations. If you don't, you're sending a message that this behavior is okay.

Fourth, create rituals that reinforce culture. Regular practices that remind people who we are and how we work.

**[PAUSE]**

Your action this week: Identify one cultural norm you want your team to have. Then explicitly communicate it. "On this team, we value X. Here's what that looks like in practice. Here's an example."

Then look for opportunities to celebrate it.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's a question to reflect on: If someone joined your team tomorrow and observed for a week, what would they say your culture is? Not what you want it to be—what it actually is.

That gap is your work.

**[OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN]**

## Session 8: Managing Remote Team Conflict

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Conflict in remote teams is harder to spot and harder to resolve. You don't see body language. You don't hear tone in Slack. By the time you realize there's conflict, it's often festered.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's the truth: Every team has conflict. Healthy teams have healthy conflict. Unhealthy teams have unhealthy conflict. Your goal isn't to eliminate conflict—it's to make it productive.

**[PAUSE]**

The challenge with remote conflict: It happens in the shadows. Someone feels dismissed in a meeting, says nothing. Someone reads a message as aggressive, doesn't clarify. Over time, resentment builds.

**[PAUSE]**

Here are the signs of remote conflict to watch for:

People stop engaging in channels where they used to be active.

Messages become shorter, more formal.

Collaboration decreases—people work in silos.

Passive-aggressive language increases.

**[PAUSE]**

If you see these signs, don't ignore them. Here's a framework for addressing remote conflict:

First, make it visible. Name what you're observing. "I've noticed less collaboration between you two lately. Everything okay?"

Second, move to voice. Conflict shouldn't be resolved in text. Schedule a call.

Third, create psychological safety. Make it safe to speak honestly. "I want to understand what's happening. No judgment, just clarity."

Fourth, focus on impact, not intent. "When you said X, here's how it landed. That may not have been your intention, but that was the impact."

**[PAUSE]**

Your challenge this week: Check for conflict signs in your team. If you see something, say something. Don't let it fester.

**[PAUSE]**

Also, reflect on this: Are you creating conditions for healthy conflict? Do people feel safe to disagree? Do you reward honesty even when it's uncomfortable? If not, that's where you start.

**[OUTRO MUSIC FADES IN]**

## Session 9: Avoiding Remote Leader Burnout

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** Let's talk about you. Remote leadership is exhausting. The boundaries are gone. The work is always there. You feel like you need to be always on, always available, always responsive.

That's not sustainable.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's the pattern I see: Remote leaders work more hours than office leaders. They start earlier, end later, check in on weekends. Because there's no physical separation between work and home, work seeps into everything.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's the principle that will save you: Boundaries are not weakness. They're essential for sustainable leadership.

You cannot lead well if you're burned out. You cannot serve your team if you're not taking care of yourself.

**[PAUSE]**

Here are four boundaries every remote leader needs:

First: Work hours. Define them. Communicate them. Honor them. When you work at all hours, you signal that your team should too.

Second: Response time expectations. Be clear about when you'll respond. Then actually stick to it.

Third: Deep work time. Block time on your calendar when you're not available. Your team needs to see you modeling focus, not constant availability.

Fourth: True disconnect time. Time when you're completely offline. Not checking email, not glancing at Slack. Gone.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what I want you to try this week: Block one hour of deep work time every day. Make yourself unavailable. See what happens. I promise you: The world won't fall apart. Your team will step up. You'll be more productive.

**[PAUSE]**

Also, define your work hours explicitly. Communicate them to your team. Then honor them for one week. Notice how it feels.

**[PAUSE]**

Reflect on this: What message are you sending about work-life balance? If your team imitated your boundaries, would that be healthy or unhealthy?

## Session 10: Your 30-Day Remote Leadership Plan

**[INTRO MUSIC FADES]**

**Coach:** We've covered a lot. Mindset, trust, communication, performance, time zones, one-on-ones, culture, conflict, and sustainability. Now let's turn this into action.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's your 30-day plan to become a better remote leader:

**Week 1: Foundation**
- Define your team's communication architecture
- Clarify expected response times
- Set your own work hours and communicate them
- Have one outcome-focused conversation with each direct report

**Week 2: Trust & Connection**
- Identify and stop one trust-destroying behavior
- Start one trust-building practice
- Audit meeting fairness across time zones
- Have one development-focused 1:1 with each direct report

**Week 3: Culture & Performance**
- Define one cultural norm explicitly
- Celebrate one behavior that aligns with your culture
- Rewrite three assignments using the Output Agreement framework
- Check for and address any team conflict signs

**Week 4: Sustainability & Review**
- Hold one deep work block daily
- Take one full day completely offline
- Review what's working and what isn't
- Ask your team for feedback on your leadership

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what I want you to do right now: Pause this recording. Write down the one action from each week that feels most important to you. Just four things. That's your commitment.

**[LONG PAUSE]**

Good. Now I want you to tell someone. Share your commitment with a peer, a mentor, or even your team. Accountability increases follow-through.

**[PAUSE]**

Remember: Remote leadership isn't about being perfect. It's about being intentional. Small, consistent improvements add up to transformation.

You've got this.

## Quick Reference: Remote Leadership Scripts

### Script: Setting Clear Expectations

"I want to be clear about how we work together. Here's what I expect: [Specific outcomes]. Here's when I expect updates: [Cadence]. Here's how to reach me if it's urgent: [Method]. What questions do you have?"

### Script: Addressing Performance Issues

"I've noticed [specific observation]. The impact is [specific impact]. I want to understand what's happening. Can you walk me through your perspective? How can I support you in getting back on track?"

### Script: Giving Remote Feedback

"When you [specific behavior], here's the impact I see: [specific impact]. My expectation is [clear standard]. Can you commit to [specific change]? How can I support you?"

### Script: Checking on Team Wellbeing

"On a scale of 1-10, how are you feeling about work right now? What would make it a 10? Is there anything I should know about your capacity or stress level?"

### Script: Declaring Your Boundaries

"Going forward, I'll be offline [specific hours]. For emergencies, [contact method]. I'll respond to non-urgent messages within [timeframe]. This helps me be more present and effective when I am working."
