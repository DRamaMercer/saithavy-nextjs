# Executive Presence Video Mini-Course

**Voice, Posture, and Communication Presence for Leaders**

---

## Course Overview

**Executive presence** is the ability to project confidence, clarity, and authority while remaining authentic and approachable. It's not about being the loudest or most charismatic person in the room—it's about commanding attention through your very way of being.

This mini-course covers the three pillars of executive presence:

1. **Physical Presence** — Posture, movement, and body language
2. **Vocal Presence** — Voice, tone, pacing, and strategic silence
3. **Communication Presence** — What you say and how you say it

- It's not being the loudest voice
- It's not having all the answers

In this course, we're breaking it down into three components:

**Vocal presence** — How you use your voice
**Communication presence** — How you structure and deliver your message

## Module 2: Physical Presence

### Video 1: Posture and Stance

**[Visual: Side-by-side comparison of poor vs. excellent posture. HOST demonstrates.]**

**HOST:**

"Your body is always broadcasting. Before you speak, your body has already sent a message. The question is: what message?

Let's start with your foundation: how you stand or sit.

**The Executive Presence Posture:**

**1. Grounded Feet**
Feet flat on the floor, about shoulder-width apart. Feel the contact. This communicates stability and confidence.

**2. Relaxed Shoulders**
Shoulders away from ears, dropped naturally. Tense shoulders communicate stress and defensiveness.

**3. Open Chest**
Not puffed out, but not collapsed. Open. This communicates openness and confidence.

**4. Aligned Spine**
Head directly over shoulders, not jutting forward. This communicates dignity and self-respect.

**[Visual: Animation showing the 'grounding' effect]**

**HOST (voiceover):**

"When you adopt this posture, two things happen:

First, others perceive you as more present and confident. They literally see your leadership before you speak.

Second—and this is fascinating—your own psychology shifts. When you take up space with open posture, your brain receives feedback that you are safe, capable, and in charge.

**[Visual: Before/After photos of posture transformation]**

**HOST:**

"Notice the difference. Same person. Same clothes. Different posture. Completely different presence.

**Your exercise this week:** Practice the executive presence posture until it becomes your default. Feet grounded, shoulders relaxed, chest open, spine aligned."

### Video 2: Movement and Use of Space

**[Visual: Examples of purposeful vs. aimless movement.]**

**HOST:**

"How should you move when you're leading? The answer: with purpose.

**Purposeful movement** is movement that means something. It emphasizes. It transitions. It engages.

**Aimless movement** is fidgeting, pacing, shifting. It communicates nervousness and uncertainty.

**The Rules of Movement:**

**1. Move for emphasis**
When you're making an important point, step toward your audience. That movement says: this matters.

**2. Move for transition**
When you're moving to a new topic, physically move to a new spot. That movement says: something new is coming.

**3. Stand still for impact**
When you deliver your most important message, be completely still. That stillness says: this is important.

**4. Use the full space**
Don't hide behind furniture or stay glued to one spot. Own the space you're in.

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates each type of movement]**

**HOST:**

"Watch what happens when I move with purpose versus when I stand still.

[HOST demonstrates movement while speaking, then stillness while making a key point]

Did you feel the difference? Movement maintains energy. Stillness creates impact.

**Your exercise:** Practice ONE type of purposeful movement this week. Either movement for emphasis OR standing still for impact. Master one before adding the other."

### Video 3: Facial Expressions and Eye Contact

**[Visual: Close-ups of various facial expressions. Eye contact examples.]**

**HOST:**

"Your face is your most expressive presence tool. Let's use it intentionally.

**The Eyes:**

Eye contact is the single most important element of facial presence. But there's an art to it.

**Duration:** Hold eye contact for 3-5 seconds per person. Long enough to connect, not so long it becomes intense.

**Distribution:** In groups, make eye contact with multiple people, not just the decision-maker.

**Quality:** Soft eye contact, not a stare. A soft gaze communicates warmth; an intense stare can feel aggressive.

**[Visual: Examples of effective eye contact distribution]**

**HOST (voiceover):**

"When you enter a room, scan it and make eye contact with several people. When you speak, rotate your eye contact around the room. When someone asks a question, look at them when they're speaking, and at the group when you're answering."

**The Mouth:**

- **Genuine smiles:** When real, smiles build connection and trust
- **Relaxed jaw:** Tension in your jaw communicates stress
- **No forced smiling:** Authenticity beats fake positivity

**The Brows:**

- **Raised eyebrows:** Can signal surprise or inquiry
- **Furrowed brows:** Can communicate concern or deep thought

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates 'resting face' vs. 'engaged face']**

**HOST:**

"Your resting face matters. A pleasant, engaged resting face communicates approachability. A tense or closed resting face keeps people at arm's length.

**Your exercise:** Record yourself speaking for 2 minutes. Watch with the sound off, focusing only on your face. What is your face communicating? Adjust until your facial expression matches your intended presence."

## Module 3: Vocal Presence

### Video 1: Finding Your Executive Voice

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates vocal exercises. Sound wave animation.]**

**HOST:**

"Your voice is your primary instrument of leadership. Let's make sure you're using it to maximum effect.

**The Elements of Vocal Presence:**

**1. Pitch**
Your optimal pitch is slightly lower than your casual speaking voice. Not artificially low—just grounded.

**2. Volume**
You need to project enough that everyone can hear you easily. Straining to hear you erodes your presence.

**3. Pace**
Most people speak too fast when they're nervous. Slow down. A deliberate pace signals confidence and authority.

**4. Tone**
Your tone should match your message. Warm but firm. Confident but approachable.

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates pitch exercise]**

**HOST:**

"Let's find your optimal pitch. Count from 1 to 5. [Counts normally]

Now count one note lower. [Counts lower]

Again, one note lower. [Counts lower]

Find the lowest pitch that still feels natural and resonant. That's your executive speaking pitch—grounded, authoritative, but not forced.

**Your exercise:** Practice counting from 1 to 10 at your optimal pitch every day until it becomes your default."

### Video 2: The Power of Pausing

**[Visual: Clock animation showing seconds ticking. Examples of effective pauses.]**

**HOST:**

"The pause is the most underused tool in executive presence. A well-placed pause:

- Gives people time to absorb what you've said
- Creates emphasis around important points
- Shows confidence—you're not rushing to fill silence
- Allows YOU to breathe and think

**[Visual: Famous examples of powerful pauses]**

**HOST (voiceover):**

"Martin Luther King Jr. used pauses. Winston Churchill used pauses. Every great speaker uses pauses. They understand that silence speaks louder than words."

**Where to pause strategically:**

**BEFORE a key point** — This signals importance and creates anticipation

**AFTER a key point** — This gives the listener time to absorb

**BEFORE answering** — This shows thoughtfulness

**AFTER someone asks** — This honors their question

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates pausing]**

**HOST:**

"Watch what happens when I pause. [PAUSE] Did you feel that? That pause created space. It gave my words weight.

**Your exercise:** In your next conversation or meeting, consciously pause three times—once before a key point, once after, and once before answering. Notice what changes."

---

### Video 3: Vocal Variety and Emphasis

**[Visual: Examples of monotone vs. varied delivery.]**

**HOST:**

"Nothing undermines executive presence faster than a monotone voice. Vocal variety keeps people engaged and communicates enthusiasm, conviction, and emotional intelligence.

**Three Types of Vocal Variety:**

**1. Pitch variation**
Use higher pitch for questions or excitement, lower pitch for authority and emphasis.

**2. Pace variation**
Speed up for energy and enthusiasm. Slow down for importance and gravity.

**3. Volume variation**
Softer volume draws people in. Louder volume commands attention.

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates the same sentence with different emphasis]**

**HOST:**

"Listen to how emphasis changes meaning.

'THE project is due on Friday' — Emphasizing what

'The PROJECT is due on Friday' — Emphasizing which thing

'The project is DUE on Friday' — Emphasizing deadline

'The project is due ON Friday' — Emphasizing timing

Same words. Different emphasis. Different meaning.

**Your exercise:** Take one paragraph of a speech or presentation. Mark where you'll vary pitch, pace, and volume. Practice until the variation feels natural."

## Module 4: Communication Presence

### Video 1: Structuring Your Message

**[Visual: Framework diagrams. Message structure examples.]**

**HOST:**

"Executive presence isn't just HOW you say it—it's WHAT you say and how you STRUCTURE it.

Leaders with executive presence communicate clearly, concisely, and with purpose.

**The PREP Framework:**

**P - POINT**
Start with your main point. Don't bury the lead.

**R - REASON**
Give the reason or context for your point.

**E - EXAMPLE**
Provide an example or evidence to support your point.

**P - POINT**
Restate your point with a call to action.

**[Visual: Example of PREP in action]**

**HOST:**

"Here's PREP in action:

**POINT:** We need to launch the product in Q2.

**REASON:** Market conditions are favorable now and may not be later.

**EXAMPLE:** Competitors are launching similar products in Q3.

**POINT:** So I recommend we approve the Q2 launch.

Clear, structured, actionable. That's executive presence.

**Your exercise:** Use the PREP framework for your next three important communications."

---

### Video 2: Handling Questions and Challenges

**[Visual: Q&A scenarios. Challenging questions being handled gracefully.]**

**HOST:**

"How you handle questions and challenges reveals your executive presence. Leaders with presence remain calm under pressure and respond thoughtfully, not reactively.

**The PAUSE Framework for Questions:**

**P - PAUSE**
Take a breath before responding. Don't rush.

**A - ACKNOWLEDGE**
Acknowledge the question or concern. "That's an important question."

**U - UNDERSTAND**
Make sure you understand. "Let me make sure I understand what you're asking."

**S - SPEAK**
Respond clearly and concisely.

**E - END**
End by checking for satisfaction. "Does that answer your question?"

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates PAUSE with a challenging question]**

**HOST (roleplay):**

[Asks a challenging question]

"That's a fair question, and I'm glad you raised it. Let me make sure I understand: you're concerned about timeline, right? [Waits for confirmation] Yes, the timeline is tight. Here's how we're approaching it... [Clear response]. Does that address your concern?

Notice: no defensiveness. No rushing. Clear, composed response.

**Your exercise:** Prepare PAUSE responses for the three most challenging questions you're likely to face."

### Video 3: Presence in Different Settings

**[Visual: Three settings: Boardroom, Presentation, One-on-One]**

**HOST:**

"Executive presence looks different in different settings. Let's adapt."

**Setting 1: The Boardroom / Senior Meetings**

- Speak less frequently, but with more impact
- Listen more than you talk
- When you do speak, be concise and direct
- Ask powerful questions
- Your presence = thoughtful contribution, not domination

**Setting 2: Presentations**

- Maximum variety in voice and body
- Strategic use of stillness for key points
- Eye contact distributed across the room
- Your presence = engagement and energy

**Setting 3: One-on-Ones**

- Softer volume, more intimacy
- More listening, less talking
- Eye contact and full attention
- Your presence = connection and care

**[Visual: Comparison table]**

**HOST:**

"The mistake is using the same presence approach everywhere. Adapting to the setting is itself a sign of executive presence—you read the room and respond appropriately.

**Your exercise:** Before your next interaction in each setting, identify which presence approach you'll use."

## Module 5: Presence Under Pressure

### Video Script: Maintaining Presence When It Counts

**[Visual: High-pressure scenarios. Leader navigating challenges.]**

**HOST:**

"Executive presence is easy when things are calm. The real test is when the pressure is on.

Crisis. Conflict. High-stakes decisions. Tough questions.

In these moments, your body wants to pull you into reactivity. Your instinct is to fight, freeze, or flee.

**Presence under pressure is what separates good leaders from great ones.**

**The 90-Second Reset:**

When you're activated, your emotional charge lasts only 90 seconds in your body—UNLESS you keep re-triggering it with your thoughts.

Use this reset:

**1. NOTICE** — "I'm activated right now."
**2. BREATHE** — 5 slow breaths (inhale 4, exhale 6)
**3. GROUND** — Feel your feet, look around, name 3 things you see
**4. CHOOSE** — Respond intentionally

**[Visual: HOST demonstrates the reset quickly]**

**HOST:**

"You can do this reset in 90 seconds, right in the middle of a high-pressure situation. No one even needs to know you're doing it. But it will completely change your capacity for presence.

**Presence in Conflict:**

- Lower your voice (slower, quieter = more presence)
- Get curious rather than defensive
- Acknowledge emotions: "This clearly matters."
- Pause before responding

**Presence in Crisis:**

- Be the calmest person in the room
- Communicate clearly and concisely
- Acknowledge what you know AND what you don't know
- Focus on next steps, not blame

**[Visual: Examples of leaders maintaining presence under pressure]**

**HOST (voiceover):**

"Notice the pattern: they're not rushing. They're not escalating. They're calm, clear, and present. That's executive presence under pressure.

**Your exercise:** Identify one high-pressure situation coming up. Plan how you'll use the 90-second reset if you get activated."
