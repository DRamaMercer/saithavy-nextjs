# Conscious Communication Templates

## Overview

These templates are designed to help you communicate with clarity, empathy, and impact. Each template is ready to use as-is or can be customized for your specific situation.

**How to Use These Templates:**
1. Choose the template that matches your situation
2. Fill in the bracketed information [like this]
3. Adjust the tone to fit your style
4. Practice delivering it before the actual conversation
5. Always mean what you say—authenticity matters

---

## Template 1: Giving Difficult Feedback

### The SBI Model (Situation-Behavior-Impact)

**When to Use:** When someone's behavior is negatively impacting the team or work quality.

**Template:**
```
"I want to talk with you about [specific situation].

When you [describe the specific behavior you observed],
the impact was [describe the consequence of the behavior].

I wanted to bring this to your attention because [explain why it matters -
team success, their growth, work quality, etc.].

I'd like to understand your perspective on this. Can you tell me what
was happening from your point of view?"

[Listen to their response]

"Thank you for sharing that context. Going forward, I'd like to see
[describe the desired behavior].

How can I support you in making this change?"
```

**Example:**
```
"I want to talk with you about yesterday's client meeting.

When you interrupted Alex three times while they were presenting,
the impact was that Alex lost their train of thought and couldn't
complete their proposal, which frustrated the client.

I wanted to bring this to your attention because I know you value
collaborative teamwork, and this behavior doesn't align with that.

I'd like to understand your perspective. What was happening for you
in that moment?"

[After listening]

"Thank you for sharing. Going forward, I'd like to see you wait until
people finish speaking before sharing your thoughts. You might even try
writing down your points so you don't lose them.

How can I support you in making this change?"
```

---

### Variations for Different Situations

**For Performance Issues:**
```
"I've noticed [specific behavior pattern] over the past [timeframe].
This is impacting [consequence].

Let's discuss what's getting in the way and how we can address this
together."
```

**For Pattern Behavior:**
```
"This is the [number] time this has happened recently.
The pattern I'm seeing is [describe pattern].

What's your perspective on what's causing this, and how can we
break this pattern?"
```

## Template 2: Requesting Behavior Change

### The Impact-Request-Check Approach

**When to Use:** When you need someone to change how they're working or interacting.

**Template:**
```
"I want to discuss something that would help us work together more effectively.

When [specific situation or behavior occurs], I feel [your emotion]
because [the impact on you or the work].

Going forward, I'd appreciate it if [specific behavior you're requesting].

Would you be open to that?"

[If they agree]

"Great, thank you. Is there anything I can do differently to support
this change?"
```

**Example:**
```
"I want to discuss something that would help us work together more effectively.

When you send me urgent requests after 7 PM, I feel stressed because it
interrupts my personal time and makes it hard to recharge.

Going forward, I'd appreciate it if we could agree that urgent requests
after hours happen only in true emergencies, and we establish what
constitutes an emergency.

Would you be open to that?"

[After they agree]

"Great, thank you. Is there anything I can do differently to make sure
you get what you need during work hours?"
```

## Template 3: Addressing Missed Deadlines

### The Understanding-Prevention-Support Approach

**When to Use:** When someone misses a deadline or commitment.

**Template:**
```
"I noticed that [specific deadline/task] wasn't completed by our agreed date.

I want to understand what happened—can you walk me through what got
in the way?"

[Listen to their explanation]

"Thank you for explaining. Going forward, how can we prevent this from
happening again? What do you need from me to ensure you can meet
your commitments?"

[If pattern exists]

"I've noticed this has happened [number] times recently. I want to
understand what's creating this pattern so we can address it
together."

[If first time]

"This is the first time this has happened, so I'm not concerned yet,
but I do want to make sure we learn from it. What's your plan for
getting back on track?"
```

**Example:**
```
"I noticed that the quarterly report wasn't submitted by Friday as we agreed.

I want to understand what happened—can you walk me through what got
in the way?"

[After they explain that they were pulled into another project]

"Thank you for explaining. I can see how the client emergency created
a conflict. Going forward, if a similar situation arises, please
communicate with me before the deadline so we can prioritize together.

How does that sound to you? And what do you need from me to help you
get this report done by Wednesday?"
```

## Template 4: Resolving Conflict Between Team Members

### The CARE Method

**When to Use:** When facilitating conflict resolution between two or more team members.

**Template:**
```
"I've asked to meet with both of you because I want to address the
tension I'm observing around [specific issue/situation].

My goal is for us to reach a resolution that works for everyone and
strengthens how we work together. I'm not interested in assigning blame—
I'm interested in understanding and moving forward.

[Person A], I'd like to start with your perspective. What's your view
on the situation?"

[Listen and reflect]

"Thank you. [Person B], what's your perspective on what [Person A] shared?"

[Listen and reflect]

"Now, I'd like to hear your view on the original situation."

[Listen and reflect]

"Based on what I've heard, both of you care about [shared interest],
but you have different views on [area of disagreement].

Is that accurate?"

[Both agree]

"Let's brainstorm solutions. What are some options that could address
both of your concerns?"

[Generate options together]

"Of the options we've generated, which one feels like the best path
forward?"

[Reach agreement]

"Great. Let's confirm what we're each committing to."
```

**Example:**
```
"I've asked to meet with both of you because I want to address the
tension I'm observing around project ownership.

My goal is for us to reach a resolution that works for everyone and
strengthens how we work together. I'm not interested in assigning blame—
I'm interested in understanding and moving forward.

Jamie, I'd like to start with your perspective. What's your view on
the situation?"

[After Jamie explains]

"Thank you. What I'm hearing is that you feel unclear about who owns
which deliverables, which leads to duplicate work. Is that right?"

[Jamie confirms]

"Alex, what's your perspective on what Jamie shared?"

[After Alex responds]

"Now, I'd like to hear your view on the project ownership structure."

[After Alex explains]

"Based on what I've heard, both of you care about delivering high-quality
work efficiently, but you have different views on how to divide
responsibilities. Is that accurate?"

[Both agree]

"Let's brainstorm solutions. What are some options that could address
both of your concerns?"

[They generate options: RACI matrix, weekly check-ins, clear ownership document]

"Of the options we've generated, which one feels like the best path forward?"

[They agree on creating a RACI matrix]

"Great. Jamie, you'll own creating the RACI matrix by Friday. Alex, you'll
review it and provide input. Once it's finalized, we'll all reference it
moving forward. Is that accurate?"

[Both agree]
```

## Template 5: Delivering Bad News

### The Transparency-Empathy-Path Forward Approach

**When to Use:** When sharing difficult news about budgets, restructuring, project cancellations, etc.

**Template:**
```
"I want to share some important information with you about [situation].

[State the news clearly and directly - don't bury the lead]

I understand this news is [disappointing/difficult/challenging], and
I want to acknowledge the impact this will have on [what's affected].

Let me share the context and reasoning behind this decision:
[Provide honest, transparent context]

I recognize this brings up [likely emotions - uncertainty, frustration, etc.],
and those feelings are valid.

Here's what happens next: [clear next steps and timeline]

I want to answer your questions as best I can. What questions do you have?"

[Address questions honestly]

"If you have more questions after processing this, please come to me.
I may not have all the answers, but I commit to being transparent with
what I know."
```

**Example:**
```
"I want to share some important information with you about our budget.

We've had to reduce our Q2 budget by 15%, which means we'll be pausing
two planned initiatives.

I understand this news is disappointing, and I want to acknowledge the
impact this will have on the team's goals and your work.

Let me share the context: Due to market conditions, we need to prioritize
core product development over expansion projects. This wasn't an easy
decision, and we explored all options before making it.

I recognize this brings up frustration and perhaps uncertainty about
our priorities, and those feelings are completely valid.

Here's what happens next: We'll finalize the revised budget by Friday.
Each team lead will have a 1:1 with me to discuss what this means for
their specific projects. We'll have a team Q&A on Monday.

What questions do you have right now?"
```

## Template 6: Asking for What You Need

### The Direct-Justification-Collaboration Approach

**When to Use:** When requesting resources, support, or changes from leadership or peers.

**Template:**
```
"I'd like to request [specific request].

The reason I'm asking for this is [clear justification with data/impact].

Without this, I'm concerned that [risk or negative outcome].

With this, I believe we'll achieve [positive outcome/benefit].

I'm open to your thoughts on this and any alternatives you see. Does
this request make sense from your perspective?"
```

**Example:**
```
"I'd like to request hiring a contract designer for 3 months.

The reason I'm asking is that our current design bandwidth is stretched
to capacity. The team is working 50+ hours weekly, and quality is
starting to slip. We've turned down 3 small projects due to capacity.

Without this support, I'm concerned we'll miss Q2 targets and risk
team burnout—we've already had one designer raise stress concerns.

With contract support, we can maintain quality, meet deadlines, and
give the team breathing room to do their best work.

I'm open to your thoughts on this and any alternatives you see. Does
this request make sense from your perspective?"
```

## Template 7: Setting Boundaries

### The Appreciation-Boundary-Alternative Approach

**When to Use:** When you need to say no or set limits on your time, energy, or availability.

**Template:**
```
"I appreciate you thinking of me for [opportunity/request].

I need to decline because [honest reason or boundary].

However, [alternative or suggestion if you have one].

I hope you understand, and I wish you success with [project/initiative]."
```

**Example:**
```
"I appreciate you thinking of me for the keynote at the annual conference.

I need to decline because I'm already committed to two major client
deliverables that month, and adding a keynote would compromise the
quality of that work.

However, my colleague [Name] is an excellent speaker on this topic and
might be available. Would you like me to make an introduction?

I hope you understand, and I wish you success with the conference."
```

## Template 8: Giving Recognition

### The Specific-Impact-Gratitude Approach

**When to Use:** When you want to acknowledge and appreciate someone's contribution.

**Template:**
```
"I want to recognize you for [specific action or behavior].

The impact of this was [describe the positive outcome].

What I particularly appreciated was [quality they demonstrated -
attention to detail, creativity, persistence, etc.].

Thank you for [how it contributed to team/goals]."

[Optional - for public recognition]
"I'd like to share this with the team/leadership to celebrate this win.
Is that okay with you?"
```

**Example:**
```
"I want to recognize you for how you handled the client crisis yesterday.

The impact of your quick thinking and calm communication was that we
retained the client and actually strengthened the relationship. The
client specifically mentioned your responsiveness in their thank you email.

What I particularly appreciated was how you kept everyone informed
while also remaining focused on solutions. You modeled exactly the
kind of leadership we value.

Thank you for representing our team so well."

[For public recognition]

"I'd like to share this story in our next team all-hands to celebrate
your approach. Is that okay with you?"
```

## Template 9: Conducting One-on-One Meetings

### The Reflect-Plan-Support Framework

**When to Use:** For regular check-ins and development conversations with direct reports.

**Opening:**
```
"Thanks for making time. How are you doing, both personally and
professionally?"

[Listen actively - this builds connection]
```

**Reflection:**
```
"I'd like to start by looking back. Since we last met, what are you
most proud of?"

[Let them share]

"What went well? What was challenging?"

[Let them share]

"I want to share my observations as well: [specific feedback -
both positive and constructive]."
```

**Planning:**
```
"Looking forward to the next [week/month/quarter], what are your top
priorities?"

[Discuss priorities]

"How can I support you in achieving these?"

[Discuss support needed]

"What would make this your most successful period yet?"
```

**Development:**
```
"I'd like to talk about your growth and development.

What skills are you looking to build right now?

What kind of work energizes you most?

Where do you see yourself in [timeframe], and how can I help you
get there?"
```

**Closing:**
```
"Before we wrap up:

Is there anything else you need from me?

Any feedback for me on how I can support you better?

What's one thing you'll commit to before we next meet?"

[Summarize commitments]

"Great. Our next meeting is [date/time]. Thanks for a great conversation."
```

## Template 10: Apologizing When You're Wrong

### The Acknowledge-Impact-Prevent Approach

**When to Use:** When you've made a mistake or fallen short as a leader.

**Template:**
```
"I want to apologize for [specific mistake or behavior].

I recognize that this impacted [who was affected and how].

Looking back, I see that [what you should have done differently].

Going forward, I'm committed to [how you'll prevent this from happening again].

I want to make this right. Is there anything I can do to address the
impact this had on you?"

[Listen to their response]

"Thank you for hearing me out. I appreciate your honesty."
```

**Example:**
```
"I want to apologize for how I handled the team meeting yesterday.

I recognize that my reaction to your question was dismissive and likely
made you feel unheard. That's not the standard I want to set for
how we communicate.

Looking back, I should have taken time to understand your concern
instead of shutting it down. I was stressed about the timeline and let
that affect how I showed up.

Going forward, I'm committed to pausing before responding, especially
when I'm feeling pressure. I want all questions and concerns to be
welcomed in our meetings.

I want to make this right. Is there anything I can do to rebuild trust
after this?"

[After they respond]

"Thank you for your honesty. I value your perspective and I'm glad you
brought this to my attention."
```

## Template 11: Delegating Effectively

### The Context-Expectation-Support Approach

**When to Use:** When assigning work or projects to team members.

**Template:**
```
"I'd like to delegate [project/task] to you.

The context for this project is [background information - why it matters,
strategic importance, etc.]

My expectations are:
- [Specific outcome/deliverable]
- [Timeline/deadline]
- [Quality standards]
- [Constraints or boundaries]

The authority you have for this project is [decision-making power,
budget, resources, etc.]

I'll be available to support you by [how and when you'll provide support].

Does this all make sense? What questions do you have?

How can I best support you in this work?"
```

**Example:**
```
"I'd like to delegate the client onboarding process redesign to you.

The context is that our current process is taking 20% longer than
industry standard, which is delaying revenue recognition and creating
frustration for both clients and our team. Improving this is a top
priority for Q2.

My expectations are:
- Reduce onboarding time by 30%
- Maintain or improve client satisfaction scores
- Document the new process completely
- Present proposal by [date], implement by [date]
- Budget of [amount] for any tools or resources needed

The authority you have for this project includes:
- Final decision on the new process design
- Up to $X budget for tools or contractor support
- Access to any stakeholders you need input from

I'll be available to support you by:
- Weekly check-ins to review progress and unblock you
- Connecting you with key stakeholders
- Removing any organizational barriers

Does this all make sense? What questions do you have?

How can I best support you in this work?"
```

## Template 12: Asking for Feedback

### The Specific-Safe-Action Approach

**When to Use:** When you want genuine feedback on your leadership.

**Template:**
```
"I'm committed to growing as a leader, and I'd value your honest feedback.

Specifically, I'm curious about [specific area - communication,
decision-making, team support, etc.].

I want you to know that I'm genuinely asking because I want to improve.
This is a safe space for honesty—I won't react defensively, and I
appreciate whatever you share, even if it's critical.

What's one thing I'm doing well in this area that I should continue?

What's one thing I could do differently to be more effective?

[Listen carefully, take notes, thank them]

"Thank you for this feedback. It's helpful to hear [reflect back key point].

I'm going to [specific action you'll take based on feedback].

I'll circle back with you in [timeframe] to let you know how it's going
and to check in on whether you're seeing improvement."
```

## Tips for Using These Templates Effectively

### Before the Conversation

1. **Prepare:** Fill in the template beforehand so you're clear on your message
2. **Practice:** Say it out loud—it will feel different than reading it
3. **Check your mindset:** Are you calm, clear, and coming from a place of care?
4. **Choose the right setting:** Private, uninterrupted, appropriate time

### During the Conversation

1. **Pause:** After delivering your message, stop and let them respond
2. **Listen:** Actually listen, don't just wait for your turn to speak
3. **Adapt:** If the template doesn't fit the moment, be flexible
4. **Be human:** Authenticity matters more than perfect wording

### After the Conversation

1. **Document:** Send a follow-up email summarizing key points and commitments
2. **Follow through:** Do what you said you'd do
3. **Check in:** Circle back to see how things are going
4. **Reflect:** What went well? What would you do differently?

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Being Too Scripted
**Problem:** Sounding robotic or inauthentic.
**Solution:** Use templates as a guide, not a script. Make it your own.

### Mistake 2: Skipping the Preparation
**Problem:** Rambling, losing clarity, or saying things you regret.
**Solution:** Always fill out the template beforehand.

### Mistake 3: One-Way Communication
**Problem:** Delivering your message without dialogue.
**Solution:** Every template includes asking for their perspective—use it.

### Mistake 4: Avoiding Difficult Conversations
**Problem:** Letting issues fester until they explode.
**Solution:** Use these templates to address issues early and regularly.

### Mistake 5: Not Following Through
**Problem:** Great conversations, no action.
**Solution:** Always document commitments and follow up.

## Your Communication Practice Plan

### Week 1: Foundation
- Use Template 9 (One-on-Ones) with your direct reports
- Practice Template 8 (Recognition) daily with one person

### Week 2: Feedback
- Use Template 1 (SBI Feedback) for at least 2 feedback conversations
- Use Template 12 (Ask for Feedback) with 2-3 people

### Week 3: Difficult Conversations
- Use Template 3 (Missed Deadlines) if needed
- Use Template 7 (Boundaries) for one request you need to decline

### Week 4: Integration
- Use any template as needed
- Reflect on which templates felt most natural and which need more practice

**Remember:** Communication is a skill, not a trait. Every conversation is practice.
