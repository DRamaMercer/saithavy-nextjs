# Intention-Setting Framework: The FOCUS Method

## Overview

The FOCUS framework helps leaders make intentional, values-aligned decisions instead of reacting impulsively. This method is particularly valuable for:

- Strategic decisions with long-term implications
- High-stakes choices where emotions run high
- Resource allocation and prioritization
- Team direction and vision setting
- Career-defining moments

This guide includes the framework, examples, and a ready-to-use worksheet.

---

## The FOCUS Framework

### F - Frame the Decision

**What are we REALLY deciding?**

Many decisions appear to be about one thing but are actually about something deeper. Framing the decision correctly is half the battle.

**Questions to Ask:**
- What problem am I actually trying to solve?
- What's the core issue beneath the surface request?
- What would need to be true for this to be the right decision?
- What am I not seeing that I should be?

**Examples:**
- **Surface:** "Should I take this promotion?"
- **Deeper Frame:** "Does this role align with my long-term career vision and values?"

- **Surface:** "Should we launch this feature?"
- **Deeper Frame:** "Does this feature serve our core customer segment and strategic priorities?"

---

### O - Options Assessment

**What are ALL my possible paths?**

Most leaders consider two options: yes or no. Intentional leaders generate at least three options, including creative alternatives.

**The 3-Option Rule:**
1. Option A: Yes (full commitment)
2. Option B: No (decline)
3. Option C: Creative alternative (modified approach)

**Questions to Ask:**
- What are all the possible approaches?
- What would this look like if I had no constraints?
- What's the middle path I'm not considering?
- Who else has solved this problem and what did they do?

**Example:**
A client asked you to accelerate a project timeline:

- **Option A:** Say yes (but risk team burnout)
- **Option B:** Say no (but potentially damage the relationship)
- **Option C:** Propose a modified scope that meets their core need within realistic timelines

---

### C - Consider Consequences

**What happens 6 months from now with each option?**

Short-term thinking leads to long-term problems. Play the tape forward to see the ripple effects of each option.

**The 6-Month Test:**
Imagine it's 6 months from now. For each option:
- What happened?
- How do I feel about this decision?
- What impact did it have on my team/organization?
- What did I sacrifice or gain?
- What unexpected consequences emerged?

**The 10-10-10 Framework:**
- **10 minutes:** How will I feel in 10 minutes?
- **10 months:** How will I feel in 10 months?
- **10 years:** How will I feel in 10 years?

**Example:**
Taking a high-visibility project:
- **10 minutes:** Excited and validated
- **10 months:** Exhausted but proud of the work
- **10 years:** Barely remember it, but wish I'd spent more time with family

---

### U - Uncover Your Values

**Which option aligns with my core values?**

Values-alignment isn't just about feeling good—it's about sustainable effectiveness. Decisions that conflict with your values will eventually cause problems.

**Your Leadership Values Check:**
- Which option best reflects my stated values?
- Would I be proud to explain this decision to my team/family?
- Does this decision honor what matters most to me?
- If no one was watching, which option would I choose?

**Common Leadership Values:**
- Integrity
- Excellence
- Team Development
- Innovation
- Sustainability
- Courage
- Compassion
- Growth

**Example:**
Your values are **Sustainable Excellence** and **Team Well-being**.

A decision requires working weekends for a month to hit a target:
- Yes option: Hits target but sacrifices team well-being (misaligned)
- No option: Protects team but may miss target (partial alignment)
- Creative option: Adjust timeline or scope to protect team and approach target (aligned)

---

### S - Select and Communicate

**Make the decision and communicate it clearly with reasoning.**

A decision without clear communication is a decision misunderstood. How you communicate is as important as what you decide.

**Communication Components:**
1. **The Decision:** State it clearly (no ambiguity)
2. **The Reasoning:** Share your thought process
3. **The Values Connection:** Explain why this aligns with your values
4. **The Impact:** Acknowledge how this affects others
5. **The Next Steps:** What happens now

**Communication Template:**
```
"After careful consideration, I've decided to [DECISION].

This decision reflects our commitment to [VALUE] and addresses [CORE ISSUE].

I explored multiple options and chose this path because [REASONING].

I understand this means [ACKNOWLEDGE IMPACT].

Moving forward, [NEXT STEPS]."
```

## Real-World FOCUS Examples

### Example 1: Career Decision

**Decision:** Should I accept a promotion to Director level?

**F - Frame:**
- Surface: Promotion opportunity
- Deeper: Does this role allow me to lead in alignment with my values and long-term vision?

**O - Options:**
- Option A: Accept the promotion
- Option B: Decline and stay in current role
- Option C: Accept with modified conditions (e.g., report structure, resources, timeline)

**C - Consequences:**
- Option A (6 months): Higher pay and status, but less direct team contact, more politics
- Option B (6 months): Continued team leadership, but may hit ceiling
- Option C (6 months): Best of both worlds if I negotiate well

**U - Values:**
- My values: Team Development, Authentic Leadership, Impact
- Option A: Low on team development
- Option B: High on all values, but limited growth
- Option C: High alignment if I negotiate team time and decision authority

**S - Select & Communicate:**
Decision: Accept with modified conditions
Communication: "I'm excited to accept this role with the understanding that I maintain 20% direct team coaching time and have decision authority on hiring. This aligns with my commitment to developing leaders while scaling our impact."

---

### Example 2: Strategic Business Decision

**Decision:** Should we expand to a new market?

**F - Frame:**
- Surface: Market expansion
- Deeper: Does this expansion strengthen or dilute our core value proposition?

**O - Options:**
- Option A: Full expansion to new market
- Option B: Decline and focus on existing market
- Option C: Pilot with limited resources before full commitment

**C - Consequences:**
- Option A (6 months): New revenue but diluted focus and stretched team
- Option B (6 months): Stronger market position but missed opportunity
- Option C (6 months): Learn market needs with limited risk

**U - Values:**
- Company values: Excellence, Focus, Team Sustainability
- Option A: Low on focus and sustainability
- Option B: High on all values
- Option C: High on all values with growth potential

**S - Select & Communicate:**
Decision: Pilot program
Communication: "We'll run a 90-day pilot with dedicated resources to validate this opportunity. This honors our commitment to excellence and focus while exploring growth responsibly."

---

### Example 3: Team Resource Allocation

**Decision:** A key client wants us to accelerate their project by 2 weeks.

**F - Frame:**
- Surface: Timeline change request
- Deeper: How do we maintain quality and team well-being while meeting client needs?

**O - Options:**
- Option A: Agree to acceleration (team works overtime)
- Option B: Decline acceleration (maintain timeline)
- Option C: Accelerate with reduced scope or increased budget

**C - Consequences:**
- Option A (6 months): Client happy but team burned out, quality issues emerge
- Option B (6 months): Team healthy but client potentially frustrated
- Option C (6 months): Client gets what they need (speed), team is protected, quality maintained

**U - Values:**
- Values: Sustainable Excellence, Mutual Success, Team Well-being
- Option A: Violates team well-being
- Option B: Misses mutual success opportunity
- Option C: Aligns with all values

**S - Select & Communicate:**
Decision: Accelerate with modified scope
Communication to client: "We can accelerate by 2 weeks. To maintain quality, we'll phase deliverables: [core features] by original date, [secondary features] by accelerated date. This ensures you get the critical functionality faster without compromising quality."

## Common Decision-Making Traps (And How FOCUS Helps)

### Trap 1: Sunk Cost Fallacy
**The Trap:** Continuing down a path because you've already invested time/money.

**How FOCUS Helps:**
- **Frame:** Ask "If I were starting fresh today, would I choose this path?"
- **Consequences:** Focus on future impact, not past investment

---

### Trap 2: Analysis Paralysis
**The Trap:** Overthinking and delaying decisions.

**How FOCUS Helps:**
- **Options:** Limit to 3-5 clear options (not infinite)
- **Communicate:** Set a decision deadline before starting the framework

---

### Trap 3: Confirmation Bias
**The Trap:** Only seeing information that confirms your preferred choice.

**How FOCUS Helps:**
- **Values:** Forces alignment check beyond preferences
- **Consequences:** Requires considering negative outcomes
- **Options:** Generates alternatives you might not see

---

### Trap 4: Emotional Decision-Making
**The Trap:** Deciding based on current emotions (fear, excitement, pressure).

**How FOCUS Helps:**
- **Consequences:** Projects beyond current emotion to long-term reality
- **Values:** Reconnects to principles, not feelings
- **Frame:** Separates surface emotion from deeper need

---

### Trap 5: People-Pleasing
**The Trap:** Deciding based on what others want, not what's right.

**How FOCUS Helps:**
- **Values:** Centers on YOUR values, not others' opinions
- **Frame:** Uncovers what YOU're actually deciding
- **Communicate:** Prepares you to explain your reasoning clearly

## Advanced FOCUS: For Teams and Groups

### Leading Teams Through FOCUS

When making decisions as a team, facilitate FOCUS collectively:

**F - Frame Together (15 minutes)**
- What problem are we solving?
- What are we NOT deciding?
- What boundaries exist?

**O - Generate Options (30 minutes)**
- Brainstorm at least 5 options
- No evaluation during generation
- Welcome wild ideas

**C - Consider Consequences (30 minutes)**
- Split into small groups
- Each group analyzes 2-3 options
- Report back with findings

**U - Check Values (20 minutes)**
- What are our team/organizational values?
- Which options best align?
- Where are the tensions?

**S - Select & Communicate (25 minutes)**
- Decide as a group (consensus or vote)
- Draft communication together
- Identify who needs to know and when

**Total Time:** ~2 hours for significant team decisions

## FOCUS Quick Version (5 Minutes)

For faster decisions, use this streamlined version:

**1. What's the real decision here?** (30 seconds)
**2. What are my 3 options?** (1 minute)
**3. How will I feel about this in 6 months?** (1 minute)
**4. Does this align with my values?** (1 minute)
**5. What's my decision and why?** (1.5 minutes)

## Integrating FOCUS Into Your Leadership

### Daily Practices

**Morning FOCUS (5 minutes):**
- Frame your top 3 priorities for today
- Check: Do these align with your values?
- Decide what you'll say "no" to

**Meeting FOCUS (2 minutes):**
- Before meetings: Frame your intention
- Consider options for the conversation
- Decide how you want to show up

**Weekly FOCUS (15 minutes):**
- Frame your top priority for the week
- Options: What are the possible approaches?
- Consequences: What will this week create?
- Values: Does this align with your quarterly goals?
- Select: What's your #1 commitment?

## Common Questions

### "What if I'm still uncertain after using FOCUS?"

This means you need more information, not more analysis. Identify what data would help you decide and go get it. Or, recognize that some uncertainty is inevitable—make the best decision you can with what you know.

### "How long should FOCUS take?"

For minor decisions: 5-10 minutes (quick version)
For significant decisions: 30-60 minutes (full version)
For major strategic decisions: Multiple sessions over days

### "Can I use FOCUS for personal decisions, not just leadership?"

Absolutely. FOCUS works for career, relationships, health, and major life decisions. The principles are universal.

### "What if my values conflict with what the organization wants?"

This is a critical leadership moment. First, use FOCUS for yourself. Then, have an honest conversation with leadership about the misalignment. Values conflicts rarely resolve themselves—they require direct dialogue.
