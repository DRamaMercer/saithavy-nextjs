# Module 6: Support Network Builder

## We're Not Meant to Do This Alone

**Here's a truth about resilience:** It's not just an individual trait—it's relational.

The most resilient people don't go it alone. They build and maintain strong connections. They know when to ask for help. They both give and receive support.

**In this module, you'll:**
- Assess your current support network
- Identify gaps and areas for growth
- Strengthen existing relationships
- Build new connections
- Learn to ask for help effectively
- Create your personal resource directory

Let's build a network that sustains you.

---

## Part 1: The Support Network Mapping

### Why Map Your Network?

You can't strengthen what you can't see. Mapping your support network helps you:
- Visualize your current connections
- Identify strengths and gaps
- See where you're over-relying on one person
- Recognize connection deserts that need filling
- Appreciate the support you already have

---

### Exercise: Create Your Support Map

**Set aside 30 minutes. You'll need:**
- Large paper or a whiteboard
- Colored markers or pens
- Your honest reflection

**Step 1: Draw Your Circles**

Draw concentric circles like a target:
- **Center circle:** YOU
- **First ring:** Inner circle (most important, can call anytime)
- **Second ring:** Close circle (regular support, good friends)
- **Third ring:** Extended circle (occasional support, community)
- **Fourth ring:** Professional support (therapists, coaches, groups)
- **Fifth ring:** Emergency resources (crisis services)

**Step 2: Place Your People**

For each person in your life, place them in the appropriate ring based on:
- How close you feel to them
- How much you can rely on them
- How often you connect
- What kinds of support they provide

**Use different colors for different types of support:**
- **Emotional support:** People you can be vulnerable with
- **Practical support:** People who help with tasks, advice, resources
- **Inspiration/support:** People who motivate and encourage you
- **Professional support:** Therapists, coaches, mentors

**Step 3: Analyze Your Map**

Answer these questions:

**Quantity:**
- How many people are in your inner circle? _____
- How many in your close circle? _____
- Do you have enough support overall?

**Quality:**
- Which relationships feel one-sided? (giving but not receiving)
- Which feel reciprocal? (both give and receive)
- Which feel draining rather than supportive?
- Which feel nourishing and strengthening?

**Gaps:**
- What type of support is missing? (emotional, practical, professional)
- Are there rings that are empty?
- Are there times when you feel alone?

**Risks:**
- Are you over-relying on one person for everything?
- What would happen if that person wasn't available?
- Do you need to diversify your support?

---

### Example Support Maps

**The Balanced Map:**
- Inner circle: Partner, best friend, sibling (3 people)
- Close circle: 5-8 good friends
- Extended circle: Colleagues, community members, extended family
- Professional: Therapist, coach, or mentor
- Emergency: Crisis hotlines, emergency services

**The At-Risk Map:**
- Inner circle: Only one person (often a partner or best friend)
- Close circle: 1-2 people
- Extended circle: Very few
- Professional: None
- **Risk:** If that one inner circle person becomes unavailable, you have no support

**The Professional-Heavy Map:**
- Inner circle: Empty
- Close circle: 2-3 people
- Extended circle: Many colleagues
- Professional: Therapist, multiple coaches
- **Risk:** Lots of professional help, but lacking personal connection

**Your goal:** A balanced map with multiple people in each circle, providing different types of support.

## Part 2: Relationship Assessment

### Exercise: Rate Your Key Relationships

For each important person in your life, assess:

**1. Closeness (1-10)**
- How emotionally close do you feel?
- How well do they know the real you?
- Can you be vulnerable with them?

**2. Reliability (1-10)**
- Can you count on them when things are hard?
- Do they show up when they say they will?
- Are they consistent?

**3. Reciprocity (1-10)**
- Is the give-and-take balanced?
- Do you both reach out?
- Can you both be vulnerable?
- Or is one person always "the strong one"?

**4. Energy (1-10)**
- Do you feel energized or drained after connecting?
- Is the relationship nourishing or depleting?
- Do you feel better or worse about yourself after?

**5. Safety (1-10)**
- Can you be yourself without judgment?
- Is it safe to share difficult emotions?
- Do they respect your boundaries?

**Score interpretation:**
- **40-50:** This is a core relationship. Treasure it.
- **30-39:** Good relationship with room to grow.
- **20-29:** This relationship needs attention or reassessment.
- **Below 20:** This may be a relationship to limit or reconsider.

## Part 3: Strengthening Existing Relationships

### Strategies to Deepen Connections

**You don't always need new relationships.** Sometimes you need to deepen the ones you have.

---

### Strategy 1: The Vulnerability Leap

**Vulnerability creates connection.** But it feels risky.

**Start small:**
- Share something real (not just "I'm fine")
- Admit to a struggle (not your deepest secret, but a real one)
- Express appreciation ("I really value our friendship")
- Ask for what you need ("I could use a listening ear")

**Example escalation ladder:**
1. "I've been feeling a bit stressed lately" (small vulnerability)
2. "I'm worried about my job" (medium vulnerability)
3. "Sometimes I feel like I'm not good enough" (larger vulnerability)
4. "I'm struggling with something and I really need help" (deep vulnerability)

**Test the waters.** If they respond with support and empathy, you can go deeper. If they dismiss or minimize, pull back.

---

### Strategy 2: The Consistency Investment

**Relationships grow through consistent small investments.**

**Daily/Weekly:**
- Reach out regularly (texts, calls, quick check-ins)
- Share small moments (not just crises)
- Respond thoughtfully when they reach out
- Remember and ask about things they've shared

**Monthly:**
- Schedule dedicated time (coffee, walk, call)
- Do something together (activity, meal, movie)
- Have a real conversation (beyond surface level)

**Quarterly:**
- Check in on the relationship ("How are we doing?")
- Express appreciation ("I value you because...")
- Recommit to staying connected

---

### Strategy 3: The Clear Request

**People want to help, but they need to know HOW.**

**Instead of vague hints:**
- "I'm going through a hard time" (leaves them wondering what to do)

**Make clear requests:**
- "I'm having a hard time. Can I vent for 10 minutes? I don't need advice, just to be heard."
- "I'm overwhelmed. Can you help me brainstorm solutions?"
- "I'm lonely. Can we grab coffee this week?"
- "I need to get out of my head. Want to go for a walk?"

**The format:**
1. State what's happening
2. Say what you need
3. Make a specific request
4. Give them an out ("If now isn't good, let me know when works")

**Example:**
"Hey [Name], I'm feeling pretty overwhelmed with work this week. I'd love to process it with someone for 15 minutes. Are you free for a call tonight? If not, no worries at all."

---

### Strategy 4: The Appreciation Practice

**What we appreciate, appreciates (grows).**

**Regularly express:**
- "Thank you for being there for me."
- "I really value your perspective."
- "You're a good friend."
- "I appreciate that you always make time for me."

**Be specific:**
- "Thank you for listening yesterday. It really helped."
- "I appreciate that you checked in on me."
- "You gave me great advice about [situation]. It really worked."

**Gratitude strengthens relationships.** Make it a habit.

## Part 4: Building New Connections

### Where to Find Your People

**You need to find YOUR people—not just any people.**

**Consider:**
- What are your interests? (hobbies, causes, activities)
- What are your values? (what matters to you)
- What are your life circumstances? (parenting, career, location, experiences)
- What kind of energy do you want? (serious, playful, deep, light)

**Look for people who:**
- Share your experiences or challenges
- Have similar values or interests
- Are at a similar life stage
- Are slightly further along the path you're on (mentors)
- Are walking a similar path (peers)

---

### Places to Build Connection

**Based on Interests:**
- Classes and workshops (art, cooking, fitness, languages)
- Hobby groups and clubs
- Sports teams or fitness groups
- Volunteer opportunities
- Book clubs or discussion groups

**Based on Life Circumstances:**
- Parent groups (new parents, single parents, special needs parents)
- Support groups (specific challenges: grief, addiction, illness)
- Professional networks (industry groups, alumni associations)
- Religious or spiritual communities
- Neighborhood or apartment communities

**Based on Personal Growth:**
- Therapy groups (led by mental health professionals)
- Support groups (peer-led, often free)
- Workshops and retreats
- Online communities (choose carefully—look for positive, supportive environments)

**Professional Support:**
- Individual therapy or counseling
- Support groups (for specific issues)
- Life or wellness coaching
- Mentorship programs

---

### How to Reach Out (Without Feeling Awkward)

**Initiating connection can feel vulnerable.** Here's how to make it easier:

**The Low-Stakes Approach:**
- "I really enjoyed our conversation at [event]. Would you want to grab coffee sometime?"
- "I saw you're interested in [topic]. I am too. Would you want to chat about it?"
- "I'm looking to build more connections in [community/context]. Would you be open to chatting?"

**The Shared Interest Approach:**
- "I'm going to [event] and don't know many people. Want to go together?"
- "I'm looking for a workout buddy. Want to meet at the gym sometime?"
- "I'm starting a [book club/study group/etc.]. Would you be interested?"

**The Vulnerable Approach:**
- "I'm trying to build more community and connection. Would you be open to getting coffee?"
- "I could use more friends who [share experience]. Are you open to chatting?"

**The Ask-About-Them Approach:**
- People love talking about themselves and their interests
- Ask genuine questions
- Listen more than you talk
- Find common ground

---

### From Acquaintance to Friend

**Connection deepens through:**

**1. Repeated Exposure**
- Show up regularly to the same group/event
- Familiarity breeds comfort (this is psychological research-backed)
- Eventually, repeated interactions lead to conversation

**2. Gradual Vulnerability**
- Start with surface-level topics (work, weather, the shared context)
- Move to personal topics (family, interests, experiences)
- Eventually, deeper topics (struggles, dreams, fears)
- Let it develop naturally—don't rush it

**3. Initiating Outside the Context**
- "Hey, a few of us are grabbing food after this. Want to come?"
- "I enjoyed our conversation last time. Want to continue it over coffee?"
- Move from "context friends" (only see at the event) to real friends

**4. Consistency**
- Follow through on plans
- Reach out between events
- Remember details they've shared
- Show up as a consistent presence

## Part 5: Learning to Ask for Help

### Why We Don't Ask

**Common barriers:**
- "I don't want to burden anyone"
- "I should be able to handle this myself"
- "They're too busy"
- "What if they say no?"
- "I don't even know what I need"

**Let's reframe these:**

**"I don't want to burden anyone"**
→ People who care about you WANT to help. It makes them feel good to support you.

**"I should handle this myself"**
→ Resilience includes knowing when to reach out. Asking is strength, not weakness.

**"They're too busy"**
→ Let them decide if they're too busy. Your job is to ask clearly. Their job is to say yes or no.

**"What if they say no?"**
→ They might. That's okay. A "no" to this request isn't a rejection of you. Ask someone else.

**"I don't know what I need"**
→ That's okay. Be honest about that. "I'm struggling and I'm not sure what I need. Can I just process it with you?"

---

### How to Ask Effectively

**The Formula:**
1. State the situation
2. Express how you're feeling
3. Say what would help
4. Make a clear request
5. Give them an out

**Examples:**

**Good:**
"Hey [Name], I'm really struggling with [situation]. I'm feeling [emotion]. I think [specific thing] would help, but I'm not sure. Do you have time to talk for 15 minutes and help me brainstorm? If now isn't good, no worries."

**Less Good:**
"I'm having a hard time." (Too vague, leaves them wondering what to do)

**Good:**
"I'm overwhelmed with this project. Could you look at it and give me feedback? It would take about 20 minutes. If you're swamped, I totally understand."

**Less Good:**
"Can you help me with my project?" (Doesn't specify what you need or how long)

---

### Practice: Asking Scripts

**For emotional support:**
"I'm going through something hard with [situation]. I don't need advice, just someone to listen. Do you have space for that?"

**For practical help:**
"I'm dealing with [challenge]. Would you be able to help me with [specific task]? It should take about [time]. If not, I understand."

**For advice/perspective:**
"I'm facing a dilemma with [situation]. You're good at [skill]. Could I get your perspective? I'd value your input."

**For company:**
"I'm feeling lonely and would love some company. Want to [activity] this week?"

**For accountability:**
"I'm trying to [goal]. Would you be willing to check in with me about it [frequency]? Knowing you'd ask would really help."

---

### When People Say No

**A "no" is not personal.** People have:
- Limited time and energy
- Their own challenges
- Different capacities
- Specific boundaries

**Respond graciously:**
- "No problem at all!"
- "Thanks for letting me know."
- "I understand completely."
- "No worries!"

**Then:**
- Ask someone else
- Or address the need another way
- Or let it go for now

**DO NOT:**
- Take it personally
- Ruminate on what it "means"
- Let it stop you from asking again in the future

## Part 6: Setting Healthy Boundaries

### Why Boundaries Matter

**Boundaries aren't walls.** They're the guidelines that make relationships sustainable.

**Without boundaries:**
- Resentment builds
- You burn out from over-giving
- Relationships become one-sided
- You lose yourself in others' needs

**With boundaries:**
- Relationships are healthier and more sustainable
- You can give from a place of fullness, not depletion
- Both people's needs are honored
- You maintain your identity while connecting

---

### Types of Boundaries

**Time Boundaries:**
- "I can't talk right now, but I can call you back tonight."
- "I need to leave by 9pm."
- "I'm not available on weekends."

**Energy Boundaries:**
- "I'm feeling drained and need some alone time."
- "I can't hold space for this right now."
- "I need to step away from this conversation."

**Emotional Boundaries:**
- "I care about you, but I can't be your only support."
- "This topic is too heavy for me to discuss right now."
- "I love you, and I'm not responsible for your emotions."

**Task Boundaries:**
- "I can't take this on right now."
- "I'm not the right person for this."
- "This is beyond my capacity."

**Financial Boundaries:**
- "I can't lend money right now."
- "That's not in my budget."
- "I'm not comfortable with this financial arrangement."

---

### How to Set Boundaries

**The Formula:**
1. Acknowledge the request or situation
2. State your boundary clearly
3. Optionally offer an alternative
4. Hold the boundary (don't over-explain or apologize)

**Examples:**

**Time Boundary:**
"I'd love to help, and I don't have capacity this week. Let me know if you still need help next week and I'll see what I can do."

**Energy Boundary:**
"I care about you and what you're going through. I'm feeling pretty drained myself and don't have the emotional space to talk about this right now. Can we revisit this when I'm more resourced?"

**Emotional Boundary:**
"I want to support you, AND I can't be your only support. This situation would be best helped by a therapist. Here are some resources."

**Task Boundary:**
"I appreciate you thinking of me, and I can't take this on right now. Have you tried asking [person]?"

**Key Principles:**
- Be clear, not apologetic
- No over-explaining (that's a sign of weakening)
- Hold it with kindness, not rigidity
- Remember: boundaries make relationships sustainable

---

### When Boundaries Are Tested

**People will push back.** That's normal.

**Responses to pushback:**

**Guilt-tripping:** "But I really need you..."
→ "I understand you need help, and I still can't right now."

**Demanding:** "Why not?"
→ "That doesn't work for me" (no justification needed)

**Negotiating:** "Just this once..."
→ "I can't, and please respect my no."

**Hurting:** "You don't care about me"
→ "I care about you, and I still can't do this. Caring includes respecting my boundaries."

**Remember:** People who respect you will respect your boundaries. People who don't are showing you something important about the relationship.

## Part 8: Giving and Receiving Balance

### The Healthiest Relationships Are Reciprocal

**Signs of healthy reciprocity:**
- Both people reach out
- Both people share vulnerably
- Both people give and receive
- Both people's needs matter
- Energy flows both ways

**Signs of imbalance:**
- One person always initiates
- One person always helps, the other always needs help
- One person does all the emotional work
- One person's needs always come first
- Conversations are always about one person

---

---

### How to Receive Better

**Many people are great at giving, terrible at receiving.**

**Practice receiving:**
- Say "thank you" instead of "you shouldn't have"
- Accept help graciously, not apologetically
- Let people do things for you
- Share your own struggles (not just listen to others')
- Allow yourself to be cared for

**Remember:** Receiving lets others feel good. Everyone needs to be needed.
