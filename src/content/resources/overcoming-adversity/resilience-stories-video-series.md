# Resilience Stories Video Series
## Real Recovery Stories and Practical Resilience Lessons

---

**A video series featuring authentic stories of resilience, interwoven with practical tools you can apply to your own challenges.**

---

## Series Overview

**Format:** 8 videos (10-20 minutes each)
**Style:** Documentary-style interviews + teaching segments + practical exercises
**Vibe:** Authentic, vulnerable, hopeful, never glossy or overly inspirational

**Each video includes:**
- A real resilience story (interview format)
- Teaching: What made resilience possible (break down the principles)
- Application: How viewers can apply these lessons

## Full Scripts for Selected Episodes

### EPISODE 1: Rock Bottom is a Foundation

**[OPENING: montage of empty room, packed boxes, person sitting alone]**

**NARRATOR:** We all know the story. Someone hits rock bottom, then heroically climbs back up. But what happens between those two points? What does rock bottom actually feel like? And how do you take the first step up?

**[TITLE CARD: "The Night I Lost Everything"]**

**[INTERVIEW: MAYA, sitting in a comfortable space]**

**MAYA:** People talk about rock bottom like it's a moment. It wasn't a moment for me. It was a process. The business failed first—that took six months of slow decline. Then the marriage ended—that was sudden but had been coming. Then I couldn't afford the mortgage, so I lost the house.

**[CUT: B-roll of Maya walking, looking at old photos]**

**MAYA (voiceover):** It felt like being erased. Everything that defined me—business owner, wife, homeowner—gone. Who was I without those things?

**[BACK TO INTERVIEW]**

**MAYA:** The night it all finally fell apart, I sat in a hotel room with everything I owned left in my car. And I had this thought: "Well, this is it. There's nothing left to lose." And in a strange way... that was freeing.

**[TEACHING SEGMENT: Saithavy in a warm, thoughtful space]**

**SAITHAVY:** Here's what Maya's story teaches us about rock bottom. When everything is stripped away, you reach a point of zero—which is also a solid foundation. There's a freedom in having nothing left to lose. And more importantly, rock bottom is solid. You can't fall any further. You can only build up from there.

The second lesson is about the tiny decision. When everything feels overwhelming, you don't make a grand decision about your whole life. You make one tiny decision. "I'll call one person." "I'll get out of bed today." "I'll apply for one job."

These tiny decisions compound. They're the first steps up from rock bottom.

**[INTERVIEW CONTINUES]**

**MAYA:** The first thing I did was call my sister. That was hard. The shame was... intense. But she answered. She didn't fix anything. She just said, "I'm here." That was the first step.

Then it was tiny things. One job application. One coffee with a former colleague. One therapy session. None of it felt like it was making a difference. But slowly, it did.

**[VISUAL: Time-lapse of gradual rebuilding—photos over months]**

**MAYA (voiceover):** It took two years to feel like myself again. Not the old self—a new self. One who knew she could survive the worst.

**[APPLICATION SEGMENT]**

**SAITHAVY:** So what can you take from Maya's story? Let's talk about the One Small Thing protocol.

**[GRAPHIC: "One Small Thing Protocol"]**

**SAITHAVY:** When you're in a dark place, big goals are overwhelming. So we make them tiny. Here's how:

First, identify one small thing you can do today. Not "fix your life." Not "get a new job." Just one small thing. Send one email. Make one call. Get out of bed. Shower. Eat something nourishing.

Second, do that one small thing. That's it.

Third, acknowledge it. You did that thing. It counts.

Fourth, tomorrow, choose one small thing again.

**[ON SCREEN: Worksheet template appears]**

**SAITHAVY:** I'm going to leave you with a worksheet. In it, you'll identify:
- Where you are right now
- One small thing you can do today
- One small thing you can do tomorrow
- One person you can call

Rock bottom feels permanent. But it's not. It's a foundation. You can build from here.

**[CLOSING: Maya, present day, smiling]**

**MAYA:** I wouldn't choose to go through that again. But I'm not sorry I did. I learned what I'm made of. And the life I built after is better than the one I had before.

**[FADE TO BLACK]**

**[TEXT: "You can rebuild. One small thing at a time."]**

### EPISODE 6: Depression and the Long Climb

**[OPENING: montage of person in bed, looking at phone unmotivated, staring out window]**

**NARRATOR:** We often talk about resilience as bouncing back. But what if bouncing back isn't possible? What if resilience is simply... staying? This is the story of depression and the long climb.

**[TITLE CARD: "The Year I Couldn't"]**

**[INTERVIEW: ALEX, in a warm, comfortable space]**

**ALEX:** I think the hardest thing was how logical it all felt. I knew I had reasons to be happy. I knew people had it worse. I knew I should be grateful. But I just... couldn't. Feel anything. Except exhausted. And hopeless.

**[CUT: Visual metaphor of walking through fog]**

**ALEX (voiceover):** It wasn't sadness. It was absence. Like someone had turned down the volume on everything. Colors were dull. Food had no taste. Everything took enormous effort.

**[BACK TO INTERVIEW]**

**ALEX:** The first time I tried to get help, I was turned away. "You're not severe enough." So I got worse before I got better. When I finally got into treatment, it wasn't one thing that worked. It was everything. Therapy. Medication. Exercise. Sleep. And time. So much time.

**[TEACHING SEGMENT: Saithavy in thoughtful space]**

**SAITHAVY:** Alex's story highlights something important about resilience. Sometimes resilience is gritty, medical, and non-linear.

First: Depression is an illness, not a weakness. You wouldn't tell someone to "just shake off" diabetes. Depression is the same. It's real, it's serious, and it deserves treatment.

Second: Recovery is not a straight line. You get better, then worse, then better again. This is normal.

Third: "Strong" doesn't mean "unaffected." The strongest thing Alex did was admit he couldn't do it alone. He asked for help. Repeatedly.

**[INTERVIEW CONTINUES]**

**ALEX:** There was this day, about a year into treatment, where I realized I felt... okay. Not good. Just okay. And that was enormous. Because okay was so much better than not okay.

**[VISUAL: Alex doing normal things—walking, cooking, talking with friends]**

**ALEX (voiceover):** I'm not "cured." I don't think that's a thing. But I have tools now. I know my warning signs. I have a support system. I know that even if I dip down again, I can come back up.

**[APPLICATION SEGMENT]**

**SAITHAVY:** If you're relating to Alex's story, I want you to know: you're not alone, and help is available.

**[GRAPHIC: Mental Health Check-In Tool]**

**SAITHAVY:** Let's talk about the Mental Health Check-In. This is something you can do weekly to track how you're doing.

**[ON SCREEN: Checklist appears]**

**SAITHAVY:** Rate each area from 1-10:
- Sleep quality
- Energy level
- Mood
- Ability to concentrate
- Interest in things you usually enjoy
- Hopefulness about the future

If your scores are consistently low, or if you're having thoughts of harming yourself, that's information. It means it's time to reach out for support.

**[GRAPHIC: Support System Builder]**

**SAITHAVY:** And build your support system before you need it. Identify:
- A mental health professional
- A friend or family member you can be honest with
- A crisis resource (hotline, text line, emergency services)

**[ON SCREEN: Crisis resources appear]**

**SAITHAVY:** If you're in crisis, please reach out:
- National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988
- Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
- International: findahelpline.com

**[CLOSING: Alex, present day]**

**ALEX:** I used to think resilience meant never falling apart. Now I know it means learning how to put yourself back together, slowly, with help. There's no shame in the long climb. Every step counts.

**[FADE TO BLACK]**

**[TEXT: "You don't have to do this alone. Help is available."]**

## Production Guide

### Visual Style

**Interview segments:**
- Warm lighting
- Comfortable, authentic spaces (not sterile studios)
- Close-ups for emotional moments
- Respectful framing

**B-roll:**
- Metaphorical imagery (fog clearing, paths, light through trees)
- Everyday moments that show resilience (making coffee, walking, reaching out)
- Avoid cliché "overcoming adversity" imagery (mountain tops, raising arms in victory)

**Graphics:**
- Clean, modern, warm color palette
- Clear, readable text
- Animated transitions that feel smooth, not jarring

---

### Audio Design

**Voiceover:** Warm, empathetic, never overly dramatic

**Music:**
- Opening: Slightly melancholic, building toward hope
- Interview segments: Minimal, underscoring emotion
- Teaching segments: Warm, thoughtful
- Application segments: Practical, energizing
- Closing: Hopeful, resolved

**Sound design:**
- Natural ambient sound where appropriate
- Silence for impact in key moments
- Avoid heavy-handed emotional manipulation

---

### Interview Guidelines

**Creating safe, authentic interviews:**

**Before:**
- Establish trust and consent
- Discuss boundaries—what's on and off limits
- Assure them they can stop anytime
- Discuss editing—how their story will be treated

**During:**
- Listen more than you speak
- Allow silence and emotion
- Follow genuine curiosity, not a rigid agenda
- Ask "how" more than "why" (why can feel accusatory)

**After:**
- Check in about how they're feeling
- Offer resources if the conversation was triggering
- Thank them for their vulnerability
