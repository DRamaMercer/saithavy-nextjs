## The Delegation Decision Framework

Not everything should be delegated. Use this framework to decide what stays, what goes, and what gets eliminated entirely.

### The 4 Ds of Delegation

**Do** - Keep work that:
- Requires your unique expertise, authority, or relationships
- Involves high-stakes decisions or confidential information
- Aligns with your core role and highest-value contributions
- Develops capabilities you personally need to strengthen

**Delegate** - Assign work that:
- Others can do 70% as well (they'll improve with practice)
- Recurs frequently (building someone else's capacity creates ongoing leverage)
- Fits team members' strengths and development goals
- Provides growth opportunities and stretches capabilities appropriately

**Delay** - Schedule work that:
- Isn't urgent but is important for long-term success
- Requires more information or resources than currently available
- Can be batched with similar tasks for efficiency

**Delete** - Eliminate work that:
- No longer serves a meaningful purpose
- Can be automated or streamlined away
- Was important once but isn't anymore (legacy tasks)

### Delegability Assessment

For each task under consideration, assess:

1. **Skill requirements** - Does someone on the team have (or can they develop) the necessary skills?
2. **Risk level** - What's the cost of failure? Can the team member handle this level of risk?
3. **Time investment** - Does delegating free you for higher-value work that justifies the initial investment?
4. **Development value** - Will this task stretch and grow the team member in meaningful ways?
5. **Frequency** - Does this task recur? High-frequency tasks are prime delegation targets

**Score each dimension 1-5. Total 15+ = prime delegation candidate.**

## Matching People to Tasks

Effective delegation matches work with the right person based on capability, capacity, and development needs.

### Readiness Assessment

Before delegating, assess the team member's readiness:

**High Readiness (Ready to run):**
- Track record of similar work
- Proactive problem-solving
- Minimal supervision needed
- *Approach: Delegate outcome, not process. Check in sparingly.*

**Medium Readiness (Needs some guidance):**
- Capable but inexperienced with this specific task
- Motivated but uncertain
- Occasional questions and course corrections expected
- *Approach: Delegate with clear parameters and scheduled check-ins.*

**Low Readiness (Developing capability):**
- Limited experience with similar work
- Uncertain about requirements or approach
- Will need significant guidance initially
- *Approach: Delegate in phases. Start with smaller pieces, increase scope as capability grows.*

### Development-First Matching

Great leaders view delegation as development, not just task transfer:

- **Stretch assignments** - Assign work slightly beyond current capability to foster growth
- **Strength building** - Delegate tasks that leverage and extend natural talents
- **Gap closing** - Use delegation to build specific skill gaps for future roles
- **Exposure opportunities** - Provide visibility to other leaders, stakeholders, or projects

**Rule of thumb:** 70% of delegated tasks should match current strengths, 30% should stretch capabilities.

## The Delegation Conversation

Clear communication in the assignment phase prevents most delegation failures. Use this structure for every delegation conversation.

### The 7 Elements of Effective Delegation

1. **Context and Purpose**
   - Why does this task matter?
   - How does it fit into larger goals or projects?
   - Why them specifically (recognition of their strengths)?

2. **Clear Outcome Definition**
   - What does "done" look like?
   - What are the success criteria?
   - What won't be included (scope boundaries)?

3. **Resources and Authority**
   - What resources are available (budget, tools, access)?
   - What decisions can they make independently?
   - What decisions require your involvement?

4. **Timeline and Checkpoints**
   - When is this due?
   - What are the intermediate milestones?
   - When will we check in on progress?

5. **Support Available**
   - What help can they expect from you?
   - Who else can they consult or collaborate with?
   - What's the best way to ask questions?

6. **Risk Management**
   - What could go wrong?
   - What should they do if they encounter obstacles?
   - What requires immediate escalation vs. independent problem-solving?

7. **Learning Intentions**
   - What skills or capabilities will this build?
   - What should they pay attention to for growth?
   - How will we debrief and learn from this experience?

### Delegation Script Template

**Opening:**
"I'd like your help with [task]. This matters because [context/purpose], and I think you're well-suited because [recognition of strengths/past success]."

**Outcome Definition:**
"What I need is [specific outcome]. Success looks like [measurable criteria]. This doesn't include [boundaries/what's out of scope]."

**Authority and Resources:**
"You have full authority to [decisions they can make]. You'll have access to [resources/budget/tools]. For decisions involving [boundaries], please check with me first."

**Timeline:**
"I need this by [deadline]. Let's touch base on [checkpoints: e.g., every Friday, or at specific milestones]."

**Support:**
"I'm available for [type of support: questions, guidance, obstacles]. The best way to reach me is [channel]. You can also consult with [other resources]."

**Closing:**
"How does this sound? Do you feel clear on expectations? What questions or concerns do you have?"

## Support and Check-in Systems

The right level of oversight builds confidence without creating dependency. Adjust based on task complexity and team member readiness.

### The Progressive Release Model

**Phase 1: Close Support (Low-readiness team members or high-risk tasks)**
- Daily or twice-daily check-ins initially
- Review work in progress, not just final output
- Provide specific feedback and guidance
- Gradually lengthen time between check-ins as confidence grows

**Phase 2: Scheduled Check-ins (Medium-readiness or medium-risk tasks)**
- Weekly or milestone-based check-ins
- Review progress against timeline and outcomes
- Address blockers and questions
- Reduce frequency as team member demonstrates capability

**Phase 3: Office Hours (High-readiness or low-risk tasks)**
- Team member initiates check-ins when needed
- Available support but no scheduled oversight
- Focus on removal of obstacles rather than progress monitoring
- Celebrate autonomy and initiative

### Check-in Questions

**At each check-in, ask:**
1. What progress have you made since we last spoke?
2. What's going well?
3. What obstacles or challenges are you facing?
4. What do you need from me (resources, decisions, guidance)?
5. What are your next steps before our next check-in?

**Red flags that require more support:**
- Missed deadlines or milestones without prior communication
- Repeated similar mistakes or confusion about expectations
- Withdrawal or avoidance behavior
- Quality issues below expected standards

## Debrief and Learn

Every delegated task—successful or not—is a learning opportunity. Build debriefs into your delegation practice.

### Post-Project Debrief Questions

**For the team member:**
1. What went well in this project?
2. What was most challenging or surprising?
3. What did you learn about your own capabilities?
4. What would you do differently next time?
5. What support would help you tackle similar projects in the future?

**For yourself (the leader):**
1. Did I delegate appropriately for this person's readiness level?
2. Was my support level too much, too little, or just right?
3. What did I learn about this person's capabilities and growth areas?
4. How would I adjust my approach for similar delegations in the future?
5. What capability has this team member built that I can leverage in future assignments?

### Building Your Delegation Muscle

Delegation is a skill that improves with practice. Start small and build complexity:

**Month 1:** Delegate 2-3 low-complexity, low-risk tasks
**Month 2:** Delegate 3-5 tasks of varying complexity
**Month 3:** Delegate 5-8 tasks including some high-value work
**Ongoing:** Aim to delegate 40-60% of work that doesn't require your unique contribution

## Common Delegation Pitfalls (and How to Avoid Them)

**Pitfall 1: Abdication vs. Delegation**
- *Abdication:* "Here, you handle this" with no clarity or support (sets people up to fail)
- *Delegation:* Clear outcome, authority, resources, and support (sets people up to succeed)

**Pitfall 2: Reverse Delegation**
- Team member brings every question and obstacle back to you
- *Solution:* Ask "What do you think we should do?" before providing answers. Build problem-solving, not dependency.

**Pitfall 3: Under-delegating**
- "It's faster to do it myself" or "No one does it as well as I can"
- *Reality:* True initially, but stays true only if you never delegate. Build others' capacity through intentional delegation.

**Pitfall 4: Over-delegating**
- Dumping work without clarity, support, or regard for capacity
- *Result:* Burnout, resentment, poor quality, turnover
- *Solution:* Assess team capacity before delegating. Match tasks to development goals, not just convenience.

**Pitfall 5: Micromanagement**
- Hovering, second-guessing, or re-doing work
- *Result:* Undermined confidence, learned helplessness, disengagement
- *Solution:* If you can't trust someone to do the work, either (a) build their capability through渐进 delegation, or (b) don't delegate that task to that person.
