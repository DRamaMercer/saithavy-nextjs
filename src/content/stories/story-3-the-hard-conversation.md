# Story 3: The Hard Conversation
**Authentic Leadership Audio Series**

**Duration:** 10-12 minutes
**Theme:** Authenticity in difficult conversations
**Tone:** Serious, intimate, courageous

---

## TRANSCRIPT

### [0:00-0:30] INTRO
*(Low, contemplative piano)*

**Speaker:**
Welcome back.

This story is about the hardest thing I do as a leader: The hard conversation.

Speaking truth when it's uncomfortable. Saying what needs to be said when everything in me wants to avoid.

Here's what I've learned: Hard conversations are not obstacles to leadership. They are the work of leadership.

And authenticity - showing up real - is what makes them possible.

### [0:30-3:30] THE STORY

I had a direct report who was struggling. Let's call him Marcus.

Marcus was talented. Experienced. Everyone liked him.

And he was failing in his role.

*[PAUSE 3 seconds]*

For six months, I didn't say anything.

I gave hints. I gave feedback sandwiched between compliments. I hoped he would figure it out.

I told myself I was being kind. Being supportive. Giving him time.

But here's the truth: I was avoiding.

I was afraid of hurting his feelings. Afraid of damaging the relationship. Afraid he would quit and I'd have to hire someone new. Afraid I was a bad leader for not "developing" him successfully.

So I avoided.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

The cost of that avoidance showed up everywhere.

Marcus's performance kept declining. His team was frustrated. Projects were delayed. Trust was eroding.

And worst of all: Marcus had no idea. He thought things were fine. I was protecting him from the truth.

I was protecting myself at the expense of his growth, his team's experience, and the work.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

The day I finally had the conversation, it didn't go the way I feared.

I invited Marcus to coffee - not my office, which felt too formal. I said I wanted to talk about how things were going.

I started with the truth:

"I haven't been fully honest with you. I've been avoiding this conversation because I was afraid of hurting you. But I've realized that avoiding is actually hurting you more."

Then I named what I was seeing. Specific examples. Data. Impact.

"I've noticed you've missed the last three deadlines. In meetings, you seem disengaged. Your team has come to me multiple times feeling unsupported. This isn't the Marcus I know you can be."

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

Marcus was quiet for a long time.

Then he said: "I had no idea. Nobody's ever told me this directly. Why didn't you say something sooner?"

He said it with confusion, not anger. He had been operating in the dark. I had left him there.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

That conversation wasn't easy. But it was the beginning of change.

Marcus and I worked together on a development plan. He eventually moved into a different role that played to his strengths.

But what I remember most is what he said six months later:

"That conversation was hard to hear. But it was the first time in three years of leadership that anyone was straight with me. Thank you."

### [3:30-6:30] WHAT I LEARNED

Here's what I learned about hard conversations and authentic leadership:

**First: Avoidance is not kindness. It's abandonment.**

I told myself I was being supportive. I was actually abandoning Marcus to struggle without information.

Real kindness tells the truth. Even when it's hard. Especially when it's hard.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

**Second: Care and candor are not opposites.**

I used to think: I can either care about you OR be direct with you.

That's false. You can't really care about someone and withhold truth that would help them grow.

Care makes candor necessary. Candor makes care real.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

**Third: Your discomfort is not a reason to avoid.**

I avoided because I was uncomfortable. Because I was afraid. Because I didn't want to feel awkward.

But leadership isn't about feeling comfortable. It's about doing what needs to be done, even when - especially when - it's uncomfortable.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

**Fourth: Hard conversations build trust.**

I thought candor would damage the relationship. The opposite was true.

Marcus trusted me more because I was honest. He knew I would tell him the truth, not just what he wanted to hear.

That trust extended to everything else we did together.

*[PAUSE 5 seconds]*

**Fifth: Preparation matters. Structure matters.**

I learned to prepare for hard conversations:

- Clear intention (why am I having this conversation?)
- Specific examples (not general feedback)
- Impact (what's the effect?)
- Question (what do they think?)

And always: Start with authenticity. Name what's hard about the conversation.

"I'm nervous about this conversation because I care about you and I don't want to hurt you. But I care too much to avoid this."

### [6:30-10:30] GUIDED REFLECTION

*(Music becomes minimal)*

Now I want to invite you to prepare for your hard conversation.

Let's start with awareness:

**What conversation are you avoiding?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Maybe it's feedback you need to give. Maybe it's a boundary you need to set. Maybe it's truth you need to speak.

Name it. Just to yourself.

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Now, get curious about your avoidance:

**Why haven't you had this conversation yet?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

What are you afraid will happen?
What story are you telling yourself about what it means?

Maybe: "It will damage the relationship."
Maybe: "They won't be able to handle it."
Maybe: "I'm not being supportive if I say this."
Maybe: "I should be able to handle this myself."

Notice the story. Notice the fear.

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Now I want you to ask yourself:

**What's the cost of not having this conversation?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

For them? (What are they missing without this information?)
For yourself? (What is it costing you to carry this?)
For the work? (What's not happening because this isn't addressed?)
For trust? (What does your avoidance communicate?)

Let the cost land.

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Now, let's prepare:

**What is your intention?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Not what you want to say. What you want to CREATE.

Relationship? Growth? Clarity? Alignment?

Start with the why.

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

**What are the specific examples?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Not general feedback. Specific moments, behaviors, data.

The more specific, the less personal.

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

**What is the impact?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

On the work. On the team. On trust. On them.

Let them see the ripple effect.

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

And finally, **how will you start the conversation authentically?**

*[PAUSE 10 seconds]*

Not with a feedback sandwich. With truth about the conversation itself.

Maybe: "I've been avoiding this conversation because I was afraid of hurting you. But I care too much to avoid this anymore."

Maybe: "This is hard for me to say, and I want to say it anyway because I care about your growth."

Maybe: "I want to be honest with you because I trust you can handle it."

*[PAUSE 15 seconds]*

### [10:30-12:00] CLOSING

*(Piano returns with warmth and strength)*

Let me be clear: Hard conversations don't always go well.

Sometimes people get defensive. Sometimes feelings get hurt. Sometimes relationships do end.

But I've learned that the alternative - avoiding, hinting, hoping - is worse.

Avoidance guarantees that nothing changes. Authenticity creates the possibility of change.

*[PAUSE 3 seconds]*

Here's my invitation for you this week:

Have the hard conversation.

Not perfectly. Not with guaranteed outcome.

But authentically. With care. With candor. With courage.

Start by naming what's hard about it.

"Can I share something with you that's difficult for me to say?"

And then say it.

*[PAUSE 3 seconds]*

Lead yourself first.

Your presence is your primary leadership tool.

Bring your authentic presence - even when - especially when - it's hard.

Thank you for being here.

- "Hard conversations build trust." - hopeful, not pollyanna

- Story section: build tension through details

- Serious but not heavy
- Direct but not harsh
- Vulnerable about own failures
- Courageous but not heroic

- This is the most practical of the four stories
- Make reflection questions feel actionable
- Speaker should sound like a coach, not just a storyteller
- Each question should feel like a genuine invitation to work
