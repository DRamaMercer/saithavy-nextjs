### [0:10-2:30] WELCOME + CRISIS STORY + TEACHING

[Voice warm, serious, like sharing wisdom after a storm]

"Welcome to the final part of the Resilience Audio Series. I'm glad you're here—but if I'm being honest, I hope you're listening to this during a calm time, not in the middle of a crisis.

Because that's what we're talking about today: What to do when everything falls apart. When crisis hits. When you're in the middle of the storm, not just preparing for it.

Let me tell you a story. I mentioned in Part 1 that I hit a wall a few years ago. Let me tell you what that actually looked like.

It was a Tuesday morning. I woke up to an email that changed everything. A project I'd poured two years of my life into—gone. A relationship that was the foundation of my support system—ended the same day. And within 48 hours, I got news about a health issue that terrified me.

It wasn't just one thing. It was everything. All at once.

I remember sitting on my kitchen floor, at 3 AM, staring at nothing. I felt like I was free-falling. No ground beneath me. No safety net. Just darkness and the terrifying question: 'How am I going to get through this?'

I share this because I know what it's like to feel like everything is crumbling. I know the feeling of your chest tightening so much you can barely breathe. I know the midnight panic. I know the exhaustion that goes bone-deep.

And I also know something else: You can get through it. Not because you're superhuman. Not because you have some special strength. But because resilience is built into you. It's part of being human.

Adversity reveals what you're made of—and crisis reveals that you're stronger than you know.

During that period, I developed what I call the Emergency Resilience Protocol. These aren't pretty practices. They're not Instagram-worthy. They're survival tools. They're what kept me going when I didn't think I could.

I'm going to share them with you today. Not because I want you to need them—but because if you do need them, I want you to have them.

You don't need to be perfect. You need to be here, on the hardest days, and keep going.

Let me walk you through the Emergency Resilience Protocol."

---

### [2:30-12:30] GUIDED PRACTICE: EMERGENCY RESILIENCE PROTOCOL

**Action:** Music becomes very minimal, almost no background

"The Emergency Resilience Protocol has five steps. We're going to practice each one, but here's the thing: This practice is different from the others. It's not about relaxation or insight. It's about survival and stabilization.

**STEP 1: The Reality Anchor (90 seconds)**

When crisis hits, your mind races. You spiral into worst-case scenarios. You're five steps ahead, imagining everything that could go wrong.

The first step is to anchor yourself in reality.

Right now, in this moment, are you safe? Are you physically in danger?

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

If the answer is yes—you're safe right now—then let yourself feel that.

Say to yourself: 'In this moment, I am safe.'

[PAUSE 5 seconds]

'In this moment, I have what I need.'

[PAUSE 5 seconds]

'In this moment, I can breathe.'

[PAUSE 5 seconds]

I know there's a crisis. I know everything feels overwhelming. But right now, in this specific moment, you're okay. That's not minimizing the problem. That's grounding yourself in reality.

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Now, look around you. Name three things you can see.

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Name two things you can hear.

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Name one thing you can feel.

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

That's the Reality Anchor. You're here. You're present. And in this moment, you're okay.

**STEP 2: The Breathing Anchor (120 seconds)**

Crisis triggers your fight-or-flight response. Your heart races. Your breathing gets shallow. You're in survival mode—whether there's a tiger in front of you or just an email that broke your heart.

You have to manually override this response.

We're going to do box breathing. It's simple, but it works.

Inhale for a count of 4.

[PAUSE as you count: 1, 2, 3, 4]

Hold for a count of 4.

[PAUSE as you count: 1, 2, 3, 4]

Exhale for a count of 4.

[PAUSE as you count: 1, 2, 3, 4]

Hold empty for a count of 4.

[PAUSE as you count: 1, 2, 3, 4]

Let's do that again. Inhale 2, 3, 4. Hold 2, 3, 4. Exhale 2, 3, 4. Hold 2, 3, 4.

[Repeat the cycle two more times with pacing]

One more time. Inhale 2, 3, 4. Hold 2, 3, 4. Exhale 2, 3, 4. Hold 2, 3, 4.

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Notice how your body feels. Even a little bit calmer? Even a little more grounded?

That's the Breathing Anchor. Use it whenever panic rises.

**STEP 3: The One-Hour Rule (90 seconds)**

Here's what destroys people in crisis: They try to solve everything at once. They're thinking about next week, next month, next year—and it's overwhelming.

The One-Hour Rule is simple: You don't have to solve your whole life today. You just have to get through the next hour.

I want you to ask yourself: 'What is the one thing I need to do in the next hour to take care of myself?'

[PAUSE 15 seconds]

It could be:
- Drinking a glass of water
- Calling one person
- Taking a shower
- Just breathing

What's your one thing?

[PAUSE 15 seconds for reflection]

Say it out loud or internally: 'For the next hour, my only job is [your one thing].'

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

That's it. Just the next hour. You can handle one hour.

**STEP 4: The Resource Line-Up (90 seconds)**

When I was in crisis, I felt completely alone. But here's what I learned: Resilience is not a solo sport. You need support.

I want you to think about who you can call. Not who you want to call (your best friend who's also going through it). Who can actually help?

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Maybe it's:
- A family member
- A therapist or counselor
- A crisis hotline
- A friend who's good in emergencies
- A support group

[PAUSE 15 seconds]

Identify three people or resources you can reach out to.

[PAUSE 15 seconds]

Now, if you're actually in crisis right now, I want you to commit to reaching out to one of them today. Not when you feel like it. Today.

If you're not in crisis right now, save this list. Put it in your phone. Know who you'll call before you need to call them.

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

You don't have to do this alone.

**STEP 5: The Survival Mantra (60 seconds)**

The last step is about self-talk. Because in crisis, your mind turns against you. It says things like 'I can't handle this' or 'This will never end.'

You need a counter-voice. Here's what I told myself, over and over:

'I have survived 100% of my difficult days so far.'

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Say it with me: 'I have survived 100% of my difficult days so far.'

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

'I don't know how I'll get through this, but I will.'

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

'I don't have to be okay right now. I just have to keep going.'

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Find your survival mantra. Something true that you can repeat when things get dark.

**INTEGRATION**

So that's the Emergency Protocol:

1. Reality Anchor: Ground yourself in the present moment
2. Breathing Anchor: Override fight-or-flight
3. One-Hour Rule: Focus only on the next hour
4. Resource Line-Up: Know who to call
5. Survival Mantra: Repeat what's true

[PAUSE 10 seconds]

Before we close, I want to acknowledge something: If you're actually in crisis right now, listening to this—please reach out for help. Call someone. Go to an emergency room. Call a crisis hotline.

This protocol helps, but it's not a substitute for real support.

[PAUSE 5 seconds]

Now, slowly bring your attention back to the room.

[PAUSE 5 seconds]

Open your eyes when you're ready.

**Action:** Music gently returns

You've just learned the Emergency Resilience Protocol."

### [12:30-15:00] CLOSING + ENCOURAGEMENT + NEXT STEPS

**Action:** Uplifting, triumphant but grounded music

"Thank you for staying with me through this entire series.

We've covered a lot together:

In Part 1, we learned that resilience is about integration, not bouncing back. That your body has carried you through 100% of your bad days.

In Part 2, we practiced the Five-Minute Protocol for building resilience daily. That you don't need to be perfect; you need to be consistent.

And today, in Part 3, we learned the Emergency Protocol for crisis. That you don't have to solve everything at once—just the next hour.

Here's what I want you to remember as we close:

**Resilience is not a trait. It's a practice.**

It's not something you have or don't have. It's something you do, day after day, in small ways and big ways.

Adversity reveals what you're made of—and practice determines what that is.

**For this week:**
If you're in a calm period, create your crisis resource list. Put it in your phone. Know who you'll call before you need to call them.

Practice the Emergency Protocol once, so you know it when you need it.

And keep doing the daily practices from Part 2. They're your foundation.

**Final words:**

I want to acknowledge that this work is hard. Looking at your resilience honestly—especially when you've been through a lot—can bring up pain. It can bring up grief.

But here's what I also know: You're still here. You made it through everything that came before. And that's not nothing. That's everything.

You've faced difficulties before, and you'll face them again. But you don't have to face them alone. And you don't have to face them without tools.

You don't need to be perfect. You need to be present—especially on the hard days.

I believe in you. Not because you're superhuman. But because you're human, and humans are resilient.

Adversity reveals what you're made of—and what you can become.

Thank you for practicing with me. Thank you for showing up for yourself.

You've got this.

**Action:** Music swells and fades out over 15 seconds
