### [2:00-6:00] TEACHING: The 3 Types of Focus

**[MUSIC: Focused, steady background - building momentum]**

**HOST:**
Alright. Let's talk about the 3 types of focus. Because here's the mistake most people make: they treat all focus the same.

They think "I need to focus" and try to bring the same intensity to every task. But not all work requires the same kind of attention. And when you understand this, everything changes.

**[MUSIC: Brief pause for emphasis - 2 seconds]**

**Type 1: Shallow Focus**

This is low-intensity, easy-to-switch attention. Checking email, Slack messages, quick calls, routine tasks. Shallow focus isn't bad - it's necessary. But it's NOT deep work.

The problem? Most people spend 80-90% of their day in shallow focus, then wonder why they can't do deep work.

**[MUSIC: Shift - more thoughtful]**

**Type 2: Moderate Focus**

This is sustained attention on tasks that require some thinking but aren't cognitively demanding. Writing a straightforward email, reviewing a document, routine coding, planning your day.

You can sustain moderate focus for 2-3 hours. It's your "maintenance mode" - productive but not transformative.

**[MUSIC: Build intensity slightly]**

**Type 3: Deep Focus**

This is where the magic happens. Deep focus is complete concentration on cognitively demanding tasks. Complex problem-solving, creative work, learning something difficult, writing from scratch.

**Deep focus is where breakthrough work happens.** But it's expensive - your brain can only sustain it for about 90 minutes before needing recovery.

Here's what transformed my work: **I stopped trying to stay in deep focus all day.** Instead, I built a system that protects 2-3 hours of deep focus per day, and lets me be intentional about when I use each type of focus.

**[MUSIC: Transition - more spacious]**

Let me give you a real example from my life:

Before I understood the 3 types of focus, I'd start my day with deep work - writing or complex problem-solving. But then I'd check email "quickly," which would pull me into shallow focus. And I'd spend the next 2 hours trying to get back to deep focus and failing.

Now? Here's my system:

**8:00-10:00am: Deep Focus** - Phone in another room, notifications off, one clear task
**10:00-10:30am: Recovery** - Walk, tea, default mode time
**10:30am-12:30pm: Moderate Focus** - Calls, emails, planning
**12:30-1:30pm: Break** - Real break, away from screens
**1:30-4:30pm: Mix** - Another deep focus block if needed, or moderate focus work

**This isn't about perfection.** It's about designing my day to protect my best focus hours for my most important work.

**[MUSIC: Warm, supportive]**

**Your environment shapes your behavior.** If your environment pulls you into shallow focus constantly, that's what you'll do. So let's design an environment that supports all three types of focus - intentionally.

### [6:00-9:00] DESIGN EXERCISE: Environment Design

**[MUSIC: More expansive, creative - thinking music]**

**HOST:**
Now I want to walk you through designing your focus environment. This is something you can literally do after this episode ends.

**[MUSIC: Lighten slightly - more conversational]**

**Step 1: Identify Your Deep Focus Hours**

When is your brain at its best? For me, it's morning. For some people, it's late at night. There's no right answer - just what works for YOUR biology.

**Ask yourself:** "When do I feel most clear and capable?"

Block those hours for deep focus. I'm serious - put them in your calendar like they're a meeting with your most important client. Because they ARE a meeting with your most important client: your future self.

**[MUSIC: Brief pause]**

**Step 2: Design Your Deep Focus Environment**

Here's what works - I'll share my setup, then you can adapt it:

**PHYSICAL:**
- Dedicated workspace (even if it's just one corner of a table)
- Phone in another room or on Do Not Disturb
- Noise-canceling headphones or white noise
- Water, tea, or coffee ready beforehand
- Clear desk - nothing competing for attention

**DIGITAL:**
- Browser tabs closed (except what's needed)
- Notifications OFF (Slack, email, phone)
- Full-screen mode on your work
- Website blocker for distractions during deep focus

**MENTAL:**
- One clear task defined before starting
- 90-minute maximum per session
- Recovery time scheduled AFTER

**[MUSIC: Shift - more reflective]**

**Step 3: Create Focus Rituals**

Rituals are powerful because they train your brain: "Oh, we're doing THIS now."

Here are mine:
- **Start ritual:** Put phone away, make tea, put on headphones, say out loud: "Deep work starting now"
- **During ritual:** When distraction arises, write it on a notepad (don't act on it), return to work
- **End ritual:** Close work, stretch, review what you accomplished

**Your rituals might be different.** The key is consistency - same sequence every time.

**[MUSIC: Slight build - excitement]**

**Step 4: Create Your Focus Protocol**

A protocol is just a clear set of rules you follow. Here's mine:

1. **Deep focus happens 8-10am, Monday-Thursday**
2. **Phone goes in another room during deep focus**
3. **One task per deep focus session**
4. **90 minutes max, then 30 minutes recovery**
5. **No shallow work before deep work**

Write down YOUR protocol. What are YOUR rules? When does deep focus happen? What's allowed and what's not?
