# Crisis Decision-Making Framework

**How to Make High-Stakes Decisions Under Extreme Stress**

---

## The Core Problem

**When you're in crisis:**
- Your brain is in fight-or-flight mode
- Rational thinking is impaired
- Every decision feels life-or-death
- Fear clouds your judgment
- You're prone to either paralysis or impulsivity

**This framework helps you:**
- Slow down despite the urgency
- Access rational thinking
- Make decisions you won't regret
- Take action without panic
- Trust yourself again

---

## Part 1: Decision Speed Matrix

### Decide NOW (<1 hour)

**These decisions cannot wait. If you delay, harm occurs.**

**Characteristics:**
- Immediate safety is at risk
- Legal or medical deadlines
- Time-sensitive financial obligations
- Dependent care emergencies

**Examples:**
- Call 911 if someone is in danger
- Go to ER for medical emergency
- Pay time-sensitive bill to avoid eviction/utility shutoff
- Pick up child from school emergency
- Take required medication

**Decision Process:**
1. **Is someone in danger?** → Act immediately
2. **Will delay cause irreversible harm?** → Act immediately
3. **Is there a legal/medical deadline?** → Act immediately
4. **Otherwise:** Move to "Decide TODAY" category

---

### Decide TODAY (within 24 hours)

**These decisions feel urgent but have 24-hour window.**

**Characteristics:**
- Important but not emergency-critical
- You have time to gather information
- Decisions affect next 1-7 days
- Reversible if wrong

**Examples:**
- Communication plan (who to tell, when)
- Short-term financial moves (which bills to pay first)
- Housing next steps (where to stay tonight/tomorrow)
- Initial work/business communication
- Support team activation

**Decision Process:**
1. Use the 10-10-10 Rule (see Part 2)
2. Consult one trusted person
3. Sleep on it if possible (or take 2-hour break)
4. Decide with 70% confidence threshold
5. Take action

---

### Decide THIS WEEK (within 7 days)

**These decisions are important but not urgent.**

**Characteristics:**
- Affects next 1-4 weeks
- Requires research or information gathering
- Can involve consulting professionals
- Reversible with effort

**Examples:**
- Financial planning (30-60-90 day outlook)
- Career strategy (job search approach, business pivot)
- Living situation adjustments (move, roommates, downsizing)
- Treatment/therapy plans
- Major purchases or sales

**Decision Process:**
1. Research your options (2-3 alternatives minimum)
2. Consult 2-3 trusted advisors
3. Use the Decision Matrix (see Part 3)
4. Pros/cons list for each option
5. Decide with 80% confidence threshold

---

### Decide THIS MONTH (within 30 days)

**These decisions are significant but have extended timeline.**

**Characteristics:**
- Affects long-term trajectory
- Major life changes
- Requires deep reflection
- Difficult to reverse

**Examples:**
- Long-term financial plans (bankruptcy, major debt strategy)
- Major life changes (relocation, career pivots)
- Relationship decisions (divorce, separation)
- Business strategy (close, sell, pivot)
- Legal strategies

**Decision Process:**
1. Extensive research (all viable options)
2. Professional consultation (lawyer, financial advisor, therapist)
3. Values assessment (does this align with who you want to be?)
4. Scenario planning (best case, worst case, likely case)
5. Decide with 90% confidence threshold

---

## Part 2: The 10-10-10 Rule

### How It Works

**For any decision, ask:**
- How will I feel about this in **10 minutes**?
- How will I feel about this in **10 months**?
- How will I feel about this in **10 years**?

**Why it works:**
- **10 minutes:** Captures immediate emotion/fear
- **10 months:** Captures practical reality
- **10 years:** Captures long-term values

---

### Real Examples

**Decision: "Should I tell my family about my crisis?"**

- **10 minutes:** Scared, vulnerable, exposed
- **10 months:** Grateful I had support, glad I didn't isolate
- **10 years:** Proud I was brave enough to ask for help

**Decision: "Should I quit my job immediately?"**

- **10 minutes:** Relieved, empowered, free
- **10 months:** Regretful if I have no income, stressed about money
- **10 years:** Impact depends on whether I had a plan

**Decision: "Should I file for bankruptcy?"**

- **10 minutes:** Ashamed, like a failure, overwhelmed
- **10 months:** Relief from debt stress, rebuilding credit
- **10 years:** Grateful I made the hard choice to restart

## Part 3: The Decision Matrix

### Use This For: Week/Month-Level Decisions

**List your options. Score each criterion 1-5 (5 = best). Total score = decision.**

| Option | Safety | Values Alignment | Reversibility | Support System | Long-Term Impact | TOTAL |
|--------|---------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|------------------|-------|
| Option A | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [ /25] |
| Option B | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [ /25] |
| Option C | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [1-5] | [ /25] |

**Criteria explained:**
- **Safety:** Does this option keep you and dependents safe?
- **Values Alignment:** Does this reflect who you want to be?
- **Reversibility:** Can you change course if this is wrong?
- **Support System:** Do you have support for this option?
- **Long-Term Impact:** Does this help your future self?

**Choose the option with the highest total.**

---

### Example: Job Loss Decision

**Situation:** You were laid off. Should you: A) Immediately apply to any job, B) Take time to strategize, C) Start consulting?

| Option | Safety | Values Alignment | Reversibility | Support System | Long-Term Impact | TOTAL |
|--------|---------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|------------------|-------|
| A: Any job | 5 | 1 | 3 | 2 | 2 | 13 |
| B: Strategize | 4 | 5 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 23 |
| C: Consulting | 3 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 18 |

**Winner:** Option B (Strategize)

## Part 4: The "Good Enough" Protocol

### The Problem with Perfection in Crisis

**Perfect decisions require:**
- Clear thinking (not available in crisis)
- Full information (never available)
- No time pressure (not applicable in crisis)
- Emotional stability (impaired in crisis)

**Therefore:** Perfect decisions are impossible in crisis.

**Solution:** Aim for "good enough."

---

### Good Enough Criteria

**A decision is "good enough" if:**
1. It doesn't make things worse
2. It addresses an immediate need
3. You can adjust course later
4. It aligns with your core values
5. You can live with the consequences

**Example:** "Should I move in with my parents after job loss?"

- **Makes things worse?** No, saves money
- **Addresses immediate need?** Yes, reduces expenses
- **Adjustable later?** Yes, can move out when employed
- **Aligns with values?** Maybe (depends on your values)
- **Live with consequences?** Yes, temporary arrangement

**Decision:** Good enough → Move forward

---

### The 70% Rule

**If you're 70% confident and it won't cause irreversible harm → Decide.**

**Why 70%?**
- Waiting for 100% = paralysis
- Acting below 50% = recklessness
- 70% = informed but not overthinking

**Action > Perfection in crisis.**

## Part 7: Avoiding Decision Traps

### Common Crisis Decision Traps

**1. All-or-Nothing Thinking**
- **Trap:** "If I don't do X perfectly, there's no point."
- **Reality:** Partial progress > no progress
- **Fix:** Ask "What's one small step I can take?"

**2. Catastrophizing**
- **Trap:** "This decision will ruin everything forever."
- **Reality:** Most decisions are adjustable
- **Fix:** Ask "What's the actual worst case? Can I handle it?"

**3. Shoulds**
- **Trap:** "I should do X" (based on others' expectations)
- **Reality:** You're in crisis—external expectations don't apply
- **Fix:** Ask "What do I need to do for my situation?"

**4. Decision Fatigue**
- **Trap:**
  - Making too many decisions → brain shuts down
  - Avoiding all decisions → paralysis
- **Fix:** Batch decisions, simplify routines, delegate

**5. Emotional Reasoning**
- **Trap:** "I feel scared, so this must be dangerous."
- **Reality:** Fear ≠ actual danger (crisis makes everything feel scary)
- **Fix:** Use frameworks, not feelings, for decisions

## Part 8: Deciding with Others

### Group Decision-Making in Crisis

**When others are affected:**
- Family members
- Business partners
- Caregivers

**Process:**
1. **Individual reflection** (each person uses this framework alone)
2. **Share perspectives** (each person shares their thinking)
3. **Identify shared values** (what matters to everyone)
4. **Generate options together** (3-5 alternatives)
5. **Use decision matrix** (score each option together)
6. **Decide by consensus** (or by person most affected)

---

### When You Disagree with Support People

**Common scenario:**
You: "I want X."
Support person: "I think you should do Y."

**Process:**
1. **Acknowledge their input:** "I hear you, and I appreciate your perspective."
2. **Clarify your values:** "Here's what matters to me in this decision: [values]."
3. **Explain your reasoning:** "Here's my thinking: [reasoning]."
4. **Make your decision:** "I've decided to [decision]."
5. **Request support:** "I hope you'll support me in this. If you can't, I understand."

---

### When You Must Decide for Someone Else

**Examples:** Parent for child, caregiver for incapacitated person

**Process:**
1. **Clarify their values** (if known): "What would they want?"
2. **Prioritize safety** (non-negotiable)
3. **Consider long-term impact** (10-10-10 rule)
4. **Document your reasoning** (for accountability)
5. **Consult others** (don't decide alone if possible)
6. **Act with love** (even if the decision is hard)

## Part 9: Post-Decision Reflection

### After You Decide

**Immediate (within 24 hours):**
- [ ] Did I follow a framework? (which one?)
- [ ] Do I feel at peace with this decision?
- [ ] What would I do differently next time?

**Short-term (1-2 weeks):**
- [ ] Is this decision working? (yes/no)
- [ ] Do I need to adjust course?
- [ ] What did I learn from this decision?

**Long-term (1-3 months):**
- [ ] Was this the right decision?
- [ ] What would I tell someone else in a similar situation?
- [ ] How did this decision align with my values?

---

### Living with Your Decision

**If it was right:** Celebrate the win. Acknowledge your strength.

**If it was wrong:**
- Forgive yourself (you did the best you could with the information you had)
- Learn from it (what would you do differently?)
- Adjust course (decisions are reversible)
- Be kind to yourself (crisis decisions are hard)

**Remember:** You can't know the future. You can only make the best decision possible with what you know right now. That's all anyone can do.

## Final Reminder

**Decision-making in crisis is not about perfect choices.** It's about making good enough choices with the information, support, and clarity you have right now.

**Trust yourself.** You've made thousands of decisions in your life. You know how to do this. The crisis just makes it harder—not impossible.

**Use the frameworks.** They're not crutches—they're tools to access your wisdom when your brain is stressed.

**Take action.** A good enough decision made is better than a perfect decision delayed.

**You've got this.**
