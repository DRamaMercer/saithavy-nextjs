# Crisis & Safety Resources
## When Boundaries Aren't Enough

*Some situations go beyond boundary-setting into safety concerns. Help is available.*

---

## The Critical Distinction

**Boundaries are for:** Managing normal relationship dynamics, communicating needs, protecting energy, maintaining healthy relationships.

**Safety interventions are for:** Abuse, violence, harassment, stalking, threats, dangerous situations.

**This document covers:**
- Recognizing when you're in danger
- Safety planning resources
- When boundaries aren't enough
- Getting professional help
- Crisis resources

**If you're in immediate danger:** Call emergency services (911 in US) or go to a safe place.

---

## Abuse Warning Signs

### Domestic/Intimate Partner Abuse

**Abuse isn't just physical.** It includes:

**Emotional/Verbal Abuse:**
- Name-calling, put-downs, humiliation
- Constant criticism
- Blaming you for their behavior
- Threatening to hurt themselves if you leave
- Isolating you from friends/family
- Controlling where you go, what you do, who you see
- Gaslighting (denying reality, making you question your sanity)
- Using money to control you

**Physical Abuse:**
- Any physical violence (hitting, slapping, pushing, etc.)
- Destroying your property
- Hurting pets
- Preventing you from leaving
- Using weapons to intimidate

**Sexual Abuse:**
- Any sexual contact without consent
- Pressuring or forcing sexual acts
- Refusing to use protection when requested
- Using sex as a weapon or reward

**Digital Abuse:**
- Demanding to know your passwords
- Constantly checking your phone/location
- Monitoring your social media
- Posting things about you online
- Threatening to share private images

**Remember:** If it feels unsafe, it probably is. Trust your gut.

---

### Workplace Abuse/Harassment

**Warning signs:**
- Hostile work environment
- Persistent targeting/bullying
- Sabotaging your work
- Excluding you from opportunities
- Inappropriate comments about appearance/identity
- Sexual harassment (comments, touching, pressure)
- Retaliation for setting boundaries
- Discrimination based on protected characteristics

---

### Stalking Behaviors

**Red flags:**
- Showing up where you are uninvited
- Monitoring your activities/location
- Repeated unwanted contact
- Spreading rumors about you
- Threatening you
- Damaging your property
- Using technology to track you

**Take stalking seriously.** It often escalates.

---

### Family Abuse

**Abuse can happen in any relationship, including family:**
- Parents using money to control adult children
- Siblings who are violent or threatening
- Extended family who harass or stalk
- Financial abuse (stealing, controlling access to money)
- Emotional abuse patterns

**Family membership doesn't give anyone the right to abuse you.**

## Safety Planning

### If You're Considering Leaving an Abusive Situation

**This is dangerous.** Having a plan increases safety.

**Step 1: Assess Immediate Danger**
- Are you in danger right now?
- Do they have weapons?
- Have they threatened your life?
- Have they hurt you before?

**If yes to any:** Get professional help immediately. Don't try to handle alone.

---

**Step 2: Build Your Support System**

**People to tell:**
- Trusted friends/family
- Domestic violence professionals
- HR (if workplace)
- Therapist/counselor
- Legal professionals

**Why:** Isolation is dangerous. You need allies.

---

**Step 3: Gather Resources**

**Documents to secure (copies):**
- ID (passport, driver's license)
- Birth certificates
- Marriage certificate
- Financial documents (bank accounts, insurance)
- Medical records
- School records (for children)
- Restraining orders (if any exist)
- Evidence of abuse (photos, messages, emails)

**Store these:** Outside the home if possible. At work, with a friend, in cloud storage they can't access.

---

**Step 4: Financial Preparation**

**If possible:**
- Open a bank account in your name only
- Build emergency cash fund (even small amounts help)
- Secure important financial documents
- Understand joint accounts and how to separate
- Know your credit score/report

---

**Step 5: Safety During Leaving**

**If possible: leave when they're not around.**

**Take with you:**
- Essential documents (from Step 3)
- Medications
- Change of clothes
- Phone/computer (if safe)
- Favorite items for children
- Pets (if safe)

**Go:** To a safe place they won't look first.

---

**Step 6: After Leaving Safety**

**Immediately:**
- Change locks (if you control the space)
- Change passwords on everything
- Block them on social media
- Inform key people you've left
- Get a new phone number if needed
- Consider restraining order

**Ongoing:**
- Vary your routine
- Let trusted people know your whereabouts
- Document any contact/attempts
- Keep evidence of harassment
- Have a safety plan for encounters

## When Boundaries Aren't Enough

### Signs You're Beyond Boundary-Setting

**Red flags that this isn't a normal boundary situation:**

1. **Fear:** You're afraid of their reaction
2. **Walking on eggshells:** You carefully monitor everything you say/do
3. **Isolation:** They've cut you off from support
4. **Control:** They control your money, time, relationships, choices
5. **Violence:** Any physical violence (once is too much)
6. **Threats:** They threaten you, your pets, your family
7. **Gaslighting:** You question your own reality
8. **Cycles:** Tension builds, explosion, apology, repeat
9. **You feel crazy:** You're constantly confused/doubting yourself
10. **Nothing works:** Boundaries are consistently violated with no consequence or change

**If these are true:** This is likely abuse, not a boundary problem. Boundaries won't fix abuse. Safety planning will.

---

### The Hard Truth

**Abusive people don't respond to boundaries.** They respond to:
- Loss of control
- Consequences
- External pressure (legal, professional, social)
- Removal of their victim (leaving)

**You cannot:**
- Love them into treating you well
- Set enough boundaries to fix abuse
- Explain clearly enough to change them
- Be perfect enough to prevent the abuse

**The abuse is not your fault.** Nothing you did caused it. Nothing you do will stop it except leaving/getting help.

## Making the Decision to Leave

### Common Barriers

**"I still love them."**
- Love and abuse can coexist
- Love doesn't fix abuse
- You can love someone and still leave for your safety

**"They'll change."**
- People rarely change abusive patterns without intervention
- Promises are part of the abuse cycle
- Actions matter, not promises

**"It's not that bad."**
- Abuse escalates over time
- "Not that bad" is still abuse
- You deserve safety, not "not that bad"

**"I can't afford to leave."**
- There are resources to help
- Staying may cost more long-term
- Domestic violence organizations can help with planning

**"They'll find me."**
- Safety planning works
- Restraining orders provide protection
- Many people successfully leave and stay safe

**"What about the children?"**
- Children witnessing abuse is trauma
- Children are often targets too
- They need you to be safe

---

### Leaving Safely

**Leaving is the most dangerous time.** Proper planning is crucial.

**DO:**
- Call a domestic violence hotline first (they'll help you plan)
- Build your support system
- Secure documents and resources
- Leave when they're not around if possible
- Have a safe place to go
- Get a protection order if needed
- Cut off contact after leaving

**DON'T:**
- Don't tell them you're leaving until you're gone
- Don't leave without a plan
- Don't go somewhere they'll look first
- Don't try to handle this alone

---

## After Leaving

### First 72 Hours

- **Secure your location:** Stay somewhere they won't expect
- **Secure your communication:** Change passwords, get new number if needed
- **Secure your finances:** Alert bank, freeze joint accounts if needed
- **Secure your social media:** Block them, tighten privacy settings
- **Document everything:** Keep evidence of any contact/attempts
- **Get support:** Call hotlines, connect with advocates

---

### First Weeks

- **File for protection order:** If you have grounds
- **Find long-term housing:** If you're in temporary shelter
- **Secure income:** Apply for benefits, job hunt, legal support
- **Build routine:** Predictability feels safe
- **Connect with support:** Therapy, support groups, trusted friends
- **Know your rights:** Consult with advocates/lawyers

---

### Common Challenges

**They're hoovering (trying to suck you back in):**
- Love bombing (excessive affection/gifts)
- Promises to change
- Apologies without behavior change
- Using children/family as messengers
- Playing the victim

**Response:** Don't engage. Stay firm. Let your support system help.

**You're missing them:**
- Normal - you lost a relationship
- Doesn't mean leaving was wrong
- Process with therapist/support group
- Remember the bad times too

**You're doubting your decision:**
- Normal after leaving
- Make a list of why you left
- Re-read it when you're doubting
- Trust your past self who was afraid

---

## Supporting Someone Else

### If Someone You Know Is Being Abused

**DO:**
- Believe them
- Listen without judgment
- Support their decisions (even if you disagree)
- Help them safety plan
- Connect them with resources
- Be patient
- Check in regularly

**DON'T:**
- Don't blame them
- Don't pressure them to leave before they're ready
- Don't confront the abuser (dangerous)
- Don't share their story without permission
- Don't give up on them

**Remember:** Leaving is a process, not an event. They may try multiple times. Support them anyway.

## Remember
