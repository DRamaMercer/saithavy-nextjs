# Delegation Readiness Checklist

> **"I'd rather do it myself" is not a valid leadership strategy. It's a bottleneck in disguise.**

Use this checklist BEFORE you delegate any important task. It ensures both you and your team member are set up for success.

---

## Quick Version

Run through these 8 questions before delegating:

1. **Task clarity**: Is the task clearly defined?
2. **Success criteria**: Are success criteria specific and measurable?
3. **Resources**: Are resources (budget, tools, access) available?
4. **Capacity**: Does the team member have capacity?
5. **Skills**: Does the team member have basic skills (or can learn quickly)?
6. **Handoff time**: Is time available for proper handoff and questions?
7. **Checkpoints**: Is a checkpoint schedule established?
8. **Feedback**: Is the feedback approach clear?

**If you can't check all 8 boxes, don't delegate yet.**

---

## Detailed Checklist

### 1. Task Clarity

□ **Task is clearly defined**
- What exactly needs to be done?
- What's the scope?
- What's out of scope?

**Red flag:** "Help with the project" (too vague)
**Green flag:** "Create project timeline with milestones and dependencies for Phase 1"

---

### 2. Success Criteria

□ **Success criteria are specific**
- What does "done" look like?
- How will we measure success?
- Who needs to approve?
- What are the quality standards?

**Red flag:** "Make it good" (subjective)
**Green flag:** "Presentation approved by VP of Marketing, includes 3 customer testimonials, under 20 slides"

---

### 3. Resources Available

□ **Team member has access to needed resources**
- Budget authority or access
- Software/tools they'll need
- Data/information sources
- People they need to collaborate with
- Templates or examples to guide them

**Red flag:** "Figure out how to get access" (sets them up for frustration)
**Green flag:** "You'll have access to Salesforce, $500 budget, and can interview the sales lead for context"

---

### 4. Team Member Capacity

□ **Team member has capacity for this work**
- Current workload is manageable
- This won't cause burnout
- They can realistically complete it by the deadline

**Red flag:** They're already working weekends
**Green flag:** This fits within their 40-hour week, or you're reprioritizing other work to make room

---

### 5. Skills or Learning Curve

□ **Team member has basic skills (or can learn quickly)**
- They understand the fundamentals
- Or, the task is a stretch but achievable
- Or, there's time for learning and support

**Red flag:** Assigning a junior person a high-stakes executive presentation without support
**Green flag:** Assigning a strong analyst a report format they haven't used before, with template and checkpoint support

**Hard truth:** If they don't have the skills AND there's no time to learn, this isn't delegation - it's sabotage.

---

### 6. Time for Proper Handoff

□ **You've blocked time for handoff**
- Not just an email or Slack message
- Real conversation (30+ minutes)
- Time for their questions
- Time to clarify expectations

**Red flag:** "Here's the brief, let me know if you have questions" (inbox-zero delegation)
**Green flag:** "Booked 45 minutes tomorrow to walk through the brief together and answer your questions"

**I've made this mistake:** I once delegated a major project via email while rushing to a meeting. The result? Two weeks of rework because I didn't take 30 minutes to clarify expectations. That 30 minutes would have saved 20 hours.

---

### 7. Checkpoint Schedule

□ **Checkpoint schedule is established**
- When will you check in?
- What should they prepare for each checkpoint?
- How will you handle roadblocks between checkpoints?
- What's the escalation path if they're stuck?

**Red flag:** "Let me know when you're done" (sets up for surprise failures)
**Green flag:** "We'll review outline Tuesday, draft Thursday, and final Monday. Slack me if blocked anytime."

---

### 8. Feedback Approach

□ **Feedback process is clear**
- When will they get feedback?
- How will feedback be delivered?
- Will there be a post-project review?
- What happens if things go wrong?

**Red flag:** "We'll see how it goes" (ambiguous)
**Green flag:** "We'll review at each checkpoint. After completion, we'll do a 30-min debrief on lessons learned."

---

## Scoring Your Readiness

**Total boxes checked:**

**8/8**: Green light - you're ready to delegate
**6-7/8**: Yellow light - fix the unchecked items before delegating
**<6/8**: Red light - not ready yet. What's missing? Can you fix it quickly? Or is this the wrong task/person/timing?

---

## Common Failure Patterns

### Pattern 1: Rushing the Handoff

**What it looks like:**
- Quick email with the brief
- No time for questions
- "I trust you, figure it out"

**Why it fails:**
- Assumptions don't get surfaced
- Questions don't get asked
- Misalignments don't get caught until deadline

**Fix:**
- Block 30-45 minutes for handoff conversation
- Come with questions prepared: "What questions might they have?"
- Make it safe for them to say "I don't understand"

---

### Pattern 2: Delegating to Overloaded Team

**What it looks like:**
- Adding work without discussing capacity
- "Figure out how to fit it in"
- Assuming they'll speak up if overloaded

**Why it fails:**
- Quality suffers
- Deadlines get missed
- Good people burn out and leave

**Fix:**
- Review their current workload before delegating
- Ask explicitly: "What would you need to deprioritize to take this on?"
- If they're overloaded, delegate to someone else or hire

---

### Pattern 3: Unclear Success Criteria

**What it looks like:**
- "Make it great"
- "Use your judgment"
- "I'll know it when I see it"

**Why it fails:**
- You'll be disappointed
- They'll be frustrated
- Rework is inevitable

**Fix:**
- Define "done" before delegating
- Specify: format, length, key elements, approval chain, quality standards
- Show examples if possible

---

### Pattern 4: No Checkpoints

**What it looks like:**
- "Let me know when you're done"
- Radio silence until deadline
- Surprise at final delivery (not the good kind)

**Why it fails:**
- Course corrections happen too late
- Small problems become big problems
- You micromanage out of anxiety

**Fix:**
- Schedule checkpoints upfront
- Make them frequent enough to catch issues early
- Make them infrequent enough to avoid hovering

## After the Checklist

If you're ready to delegate:

1. **Create the delegation brief** (use template Document 2)
2. **Schedule handoff meeting** (minimum 30 minutes)
3. **Set up checkpoints** (put them on both calendars)
4. **Delegate** (have the conversation, answer questions, confirm understanding)

If you're NOT ready to delegate:

1. **Identify the gap** (what's missing?)
2. **Fix it** (can you resolve it quickly?)
3. **Consider alternatives** (different task? different person? different timing?)
4. **Don't force it** (bad delegation is worse than no delegation)

---

## Leadership Reality Check

**Be honest:**

- Have you ever delegated without checking if the person had capacity?
- Have you ever delegated with vague success criteria, then been disappointed?
- Have you ever delegated via email without a real conversation?

**I have. Multiple times.** And every time, it created more work, more stress, and worse results than if I'd just done it myself (or waited until I could delegate properly).

**The lesson:** 15 minutes of preparation saves hours of rework.

**The hard truth:** If you don't have time to delegate properly, you don't have time not to delegate. Because doing everything yourself is not sustainable.

---

**Your next delegation: Run through this checklist first. Every time.**

*Eventually, this becomes second nature. But until it does, use the checklist. Your team will thank you.*
