## Email Templates (10 Scenarios)

### Scenario 1: Delivering Difficult Feedback

**Subject:** Conversation about [topic]

Hi [Name],

I want to schedule time to discuss [specific topic/behavior]. This is an important conversation, and I want to make sure we have dedicated time to talk through it properly.

Are you available on [date/time] or [date/time]?

Thank you,

[Your name]

**After the conversation (follow-up email):**

**Subject:** Recap of our conversation

Hi [Name],

Thank you for the conversation earlier today. I want to recap what we discussed to make sure we're aligned.

**Key points:**
- [Summary of their perspective]
- [Summary of your feedback]
- [Agreed action items]

**Next steps:**
- You'll [action item] by [date]
- I'll [action item] by [date]
- We'll check in on [date]

Let me know if I missed anything or if you have questions.

Best,

[Your name]

## Change Communication Script

### The ADKAR Framework for Change

**Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement**

Use this framework to structure any change communication.

#### Step 1: Awareness (Why change?)

**Script:**

"I want to talk about a change that's coming. Before I share the details, I want to make sure you understand why this change is happening.

**The current situation:** [Describe current state - the problem or opportunity]

**Why it's not sustainable:** [Why status quo must change]

**The external forces:** [Market, customer, competitive, or organizational pressures]

**The decision:** [Change that's been made]

I know change can feel unsettling. I want to be transparent about why this decision was made and what it means for you."

#### Step 2: Desire (Why should I care?)

**Script:**

"You might be wondering why this matters to you personally.

**What's in it for you:**
- [Benefit 1]
- [Benefit 2]
- [Benefit 3]

**What we're asking of you:**
- [Specific expectation]

**I understand that:**
- [Acknowledge valid concerns]
- [Acknowledge difficulties]
- [Acknowledge losses]

My commitment to you: [How you'll support them through the change]"

#### Step 3: Knowledge (What do I need to know?)

**Script:**

"Here's exactly what's changing and when.

**The timeline:**
- [Date]: [What happens]
- [Date]: [What happens]
- [Date]: [What happens]

**What's staying the same:**
- [Stability elements]

**What's changing:**
- [Change 1]
- [Change 2]
- [Change 3]

**Where to learn more:**
- [Documentation]
- [Training]
- [Resources]"

#### Step 4: Ability (How do I do it?)

**Script:**

"I want to make sure you have what you need to succeed in this change.

**Training available:**
- [Training 1]
- [Training 2]

**Support available:**
- [Support resource 1]
- [Support resource 2]

**Practice opportunities:**
- [How they can try the new thing safely]

**Questions to ask:**
- [Encourage specific questions about implementation]"

#### Step 5: Reinforcement (How do we stick with it?)

**Script:**

"Change isn't a one-time event—it's a journey. Here's how we'll make it stick.

**How we'll measure success:**
- [Metric 1]
- [Metric 2]

**Check-ins:**
- [Schedule of follow-up conversations]

**Celebrating progress:**
- [How wins will be recognized]

**Addressing setbacks:**
- [How challenges will be handled]

**Your feedback:**
- [How they can share input during implementation]"

## Crisis Communication Outline

### Crisis Response Framework

**Time → Message → Action → Follow-up**

#### Phase 1: Immediate Response (0-2 hours)

**Communication Goal:** Acknowledge, express concern, commit to updates

**Template:**

**Subject:** Important: [Brief description of situation]

[Team/Community/Stakeholders],

I'm writing to let you know that [situation] has occurred.

**What we know:**
[Only confirmed facts - no speculation]

**What we don't know:**
[Honestly acknowledge what's unclear]

**What we're doing:**
[Immediate actions being taken]

**What you should do:**
[Any immediate actions for recipients]

**Next update:**
[Specific time when you'll provide more information]

I understand [acknowledge impact/concern]. We're committed to [keeping you informed/resolving this].

[Your name]

#### Phase 2: Update Communication (Daily or as needed)

**Template:**

**Subject:** Update: [Situation] - [Date]

Hi [Team/Community/Stakeholders],

I want to provide an update on [situation].

**Current Status:**
[Latest information]

**Actions Taken:**
- [Action 1]
- [Action 2]
- [Action 3]

**Remaining Challenges:**
[Honest assessment of what's unresolved]

**Next Steps:**
[What happens next]

**Next Update:**
[Specific time for next communication]

Thank you for your [patience/understanding/support] as we work through this.

[Your name]

#### Phase 3: Resolution Communication

**Template:**

**Subject:** Resolution: [Situation]

Hi [Team/Community/Stakeholders],

I'm writing to share that [situation] has been [resolved/contained/addressed].

**What happened:**
[Full, transparent explanation once crisis is over]

**How we addressed it:**
[Actions taken]

**What we learned:**
[Insights from the situation]

**What we're changing:**
- [Change 1 to prevent recurrence]
- [Change 2 to prevent recurrence]

**Questions:**
[How to ask questions or get more information]

I want to acknowledge [impact on recipients]. Thank you for [trust/patience/support] during this time.

Sincerely,

[Your name]

### Crisis Communication Principles

1. **Speed over perfection:** Communicate quickly, even with incomplete information
2. **Facts over speculation:** Only share what you know, be honest about what you don't
3. **Consistency over frequency:** Regular updates are better than constant updates
4. **Empathy over defensiveness:** Acknowledge impact before explaining causes
5. **Action over reassurance:** Show what you're doing, don't just say it will be fine

## Written Communication Best Practices

### Email Specific

**Subject Lines:**
- ✅ Specific: "Project timeline adjustment: Phase 1 now 2/15-3/1"
- ❌ Vague: "Update" or "Question"

**Structure:**
- **Bottom line up front (BLUF):** Put the main point in the first paragraph
- **Use headers:** Break up long content with clear section headers
- **Bullet points:** Easier to scan than paragraphs
- **One topic per email:** Multiple topics = multiple emails

**Tone:**
- Direct but kind
- Professional but human
- Clear but not blunt

**Length:**
- Shorter is better
- If >300 words, consider a meeting instead
- Attach details, summarize in email

### Slack/Teams Specific

**When to use:**
- Quick questions that need fast answers
- Non-sensitive information
- Team updates that aren't urgent
- Casual communication

**When NOT to use:**
- Difficult feedback
- Performance discussions
- Sensitive information
- Complex topics

**Best practices:**
- Use threads to keep channels organized
- Use @channel sparingly (true urgency only)
- Default to async (don't expect immediate response)
- Use reactions for acknowledgment (👍 vs "got it")

### Documentation Specific

**Decision Log Template:**

```markdown
# Decision: [Decision Title]

**Date:** [Date made]
**Decision Maker:** [Who made the decision]
**Status:** [Proposed/Approved/Implemented]

## Context
[Why the decision was needed]

## Decision
[What was decided - clear, specific statement]

## Alternatives Considered
1. [Alternative 1]: [Why rejected]
2. [Alternative 2]: [Why rejected]

## Consequences
- **Positive:** [Expected benefits]
- **Negative:** [Expected drawbacks]
- **Risks:** [Potential problems]

## Implementation
- **Owner:** [Who's implementing]
- **Timeline:** [When it happens]
- **Success metrics:** [How we'll know it worked]

## Review Date
[When this decision will be revisited]
```

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**

Every email, announcement, or message is an opportunity to build or erode trust. Use these templates as a starting point, then adapt them to your voice and context.

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

The words matter, but the intention behind them matters more. Write from a place of care, clarity, and commitment to shared understanding.
