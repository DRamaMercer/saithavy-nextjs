## Performance Discussion

### Opening Statement

"Thank you for meeting with me today. I want to have an open conversation about how things are going in your role. I've noticed some gaps between expectations and outcomes, and I want to understand your perspective before we discuss next steps. This conversation is about supporting your success, not about criticism. Can you start by sharing how you think things have been going lately?"

### Active Listening Prompts

- "Tell me more about what led to that outcome."
- "What challenges were you facing that I might not be aware of?"
- "How do you see the situation from your perspective?"
- "What support do you wish you had received?"
- "How does this match with your own expectations?"

### De-escalation Phrases

Use when the conversation becomes defensive or emotional:

- "I can hear that this is frustrating for you. Your feelings are valid."
- "Let's pause for a moment. I want to make sure I truly understand your perspective."
- "We're on the same team. My goal is to help you succeed, not to assign blame."
- "I appreciate you being honest with me. That takes courage."
- "Let's focus on the problem, not the person. We can solve this together."

### Specific Concern Template

"I've noticed that [specific behavior/outcome] has been happening. For example, [concrete example from the past week]. This impacts [specific consequence on team/work]. My expectation is [clear standard]. Help me understand what's happening from your perspective."

### Closing with Clarity

"Let me recap what we've discussed to make sure I understand:

- You shared that [their perspective]
- We agreed that [action items]
- You'll [specific commitment] by [date]
- I'll [your commitment] by [date]
- We'll check in on [date] to review progress

Does that capture it accurately? What did I miss?"

## Behavioral Concern

### Opening Statement

"I want to talk with you about something that's been on my mind. The goal of this conversation is to address a concern I have, understand your perspective, and find a path forward that works for everyone. I want to be direct because I respect you and believe you can handle this conversation. Are you open to discussing this?"

### When They React Defensively

"I understand this might feel uncomfortable. If I were in your position, I'd probably feel defensive too. What I need you to know is that this conversation is happening because I believe in your ability to grow and succeed here. Can we continue with that shared understanding?"

### Impact Statement Template

"When [specific behavior occurred], I noticed that [observed impact]. This matters because [broader consequence]. I want to understand what was going on for you in that moment."

### Examples by Behavior Type

#### Disrespectful Communication

"When you raised your voice in yesterday's meeting, I noticed that several team members stopped participating. This matters because we need everyone's perspective to make good decisions. What was happening that led to that moment?"

#### Missed Deadlines Without Communication

"When the project deliverable was submitted two days late without any prior communication, the client team was left scrambling. This matters because our reputation depends on reliability. What prevented you from reaching out earlier?"

#### Resisting Feedback

"When I shared feedback about your presentation, you responded by explaining why each point didn't apply. This matters because growth requires being open to input. What makes receiving feedback difficult for you?"

### Closing with Action Plan

"Based on our conversation, here's what success looks like going forward:

- [Specific behavior change]
- [Check-in cadence]
- [Support/resources]

I'm committed to supporting you in this. Can you tell me in your own words what we've agreed to?"

## Role Change Conversation

### Opening Statement

"I want to have an important conversation with you about your role. I've been thinking about how your strengths could be better utilized, and I want to explore a potential change with you. This conversation is about finding the right fit, not about performance concerns. Does that make sense?"

### When the Change is Due to Performance

"I want to be direct with you. The current role isn't working out as hoped, and I think we need to consider a different path. This isn't about your worth as a person or professional—it's about fit. I want to explore options that set you up for success. Are you open to that conversation?"

### Presenting Options

"I see a few possible paths forward, and I want to get your input on which feels right:

**Option 1:** [Describe role/responsibility change]
**Option 2:** [Describe different opportunity]
**Option 3:** [Describe transition plan]

What's your initial reaction to these options?"

### When They're Upset or Emotional

"I can see this is difficult to hear. I want to acknowledge that role changes are challenging, and it's okay to feel upset. At the same time, I want to make sure we find a path where you can thrive. Let's take a breath and then continue this conversation whenever you're ready."

### Closing with Next Steps

"Here's what I propose:

- Take [timeframe] to think about your options
- We'll meet again on [date] to discuss your decision
- Once decided, we'll create a [week/month] transition plan
- I'll provide [support/resources] during the transition

What questions do you have before we wrap up?"

## Conflict Between Team Members

### Opening Statement

"I've asked to meet with both of you because there's tension that's affecting the team. My goal isn't to assign blame or determine who's right—my goal is to help you find a way to work together effectively. This conversation is confidential, and I expect both of you to speak respectfully and listen openly. Can we agree to that?"

### Setting Ground Rules

"Before we dive in, let's establish some ground rules:

- Each person will have uninterrupted time to share their perspective
- We'll use 'I' statements and speak from our own experience
- We'll focus on the issue, not personal attacks
- We'll seek to understand before seeking to be understood

Does this work for everyone?"

### Structured Sharing Template

"Person A, I'd like you to share your perspective on the situation. Start with 'I felt...' or 'I experienced...' and focus on specific behaviors, not character judgments. Person B, your role is to listen without responding. You'll have your turn next."

[After Person A speaks]

"Person B, can you repeat back what you heard Person A say before you respond?"

### When Escalation Occurs

"I can see emotions are running high. Let's pause for a moment. We're not going to resolve this through anger. Let's take [timeframe] break and come back when we can have this conversation productively."

### Finding Common Ground

"I want to identify where you both agree. From what I've heard:

- You both agree that [shared concern]
- You both want [shared goal]
- You both care about [shared value]

Is that accurate? Let's build from this common ground."

### Closing with Agreements

"Based on our conversation, here's what I'm hearing you both agree to:

- [Behavioral agreement 1]
- [Behavioral agreement 2]
- [Check-in plan]

I'll follow up in [timeframe] to see how things are going. Any final thoughts?"

## Layoff/Termination

### Preparation Checklist (Internal)

Before the conversation:
- [ ] Decision is final and documented
- [ ] HR has reviewed the plan
- [ ] Separation details are prepared (severance, benefits, etc.)
- [ ] Logistics are planned (day/time, private location)
- [ ] Team communication plan is ready
- [ ] Personal items are considered (access, equipment, etc.)

### Opening Statement

"Thank you for meeting with me. I have some difficult news to share, and I want to deliver it directly and with respect. I want to be clear that this decision has been made after careful consideration, and it's not open for negotiation. I understand this will be difficult to hear, and I want to acknowledge the impact this will have."

**NOTE:** Never start with "How are you?" or small talk. It feels disingenuous once the news is delivered.

### Delivering the Message

"Your position is being eliminated effective [date]. This decision is based on [reason - business need, restructuring, etc.]. This is not a reflection of your personal worth or capabilities—it's a business decision about [specific organizational need]."

### When They Ask "Why Me?" or "Is It Something I Did?"

"I want to be clear: this decision is not about performance or anything you did wrong. This is a [restructuring/budget decision/role elimination] that affects [X] positions. I understand this doesn't change the impact, but I want to be honest about the reasoning."

### Logistics Discussion

"Let's discuss the logistics:

- Your final day will be [date]
- You'll receive [severance details]
- Benefits will continue through [date]
- You'll receive [outplacement support/resources]
- I'll provide [reference/LinkedIn recommendation/etc.]
- Next steps for [equipment, access, etc.] are [details]

Do you have questions about any of this?"

### Emotional Support

"I can see this is overwhelming. Take the time you need. There's no rush to process this right now. What support do you need in this moment?"

### Closing

"I want to acknowledge your contributions to [specific contributions]. You should be proud of [specific achievement]. I'm sorry it's ending this way.

I'll follow up with [next steps] by [timeframe]. Is there anything else you need from me right now?"

### After the Conversation

**Immediately:**
- Send written confirmation of the conversation
- Revoke system access as appropriate
- Communicate with the team (have a prepared statement)

**Within 24 hours:**
- Check in (if appropriate)
- Provide promised resources/references
- Answer follow-up questions

## Universal De-escalation Phrases

Keep these in your back pocket for any difficult conversation:

### When They're Angry

- "I can hear that you're frustrated. Your anger is valid."
- "Let's take a breath. We can work through this."
- "I'm not going anywhere. Let's continue when you're ready."

### When They're Shutting Down

- "I notice we've lost your input. I want to hear your perspective."
- "It's okay to need time. Should we pause and come back?"
- "Your voice matters in this conversation."

### When They're Emotional

- "It's okay to feel emotional. This is a difficult situation."
- "Take the time you need. I'm here when you're ready."
- "Emotions are part of this process. That's human."

### When They're Defensive

- "I understand why you'd feel defensive. This is hard."
- "Let's focus on the problem, not on defending yourself."
- "I'm on your side. We're solving this together."

## Post-Conversation Checklist

After any difficult conversation:

### Immediately After

- [ ] Document key points shared
- [ ] Note what went well
- [ ] Note what you'd do differently
- [ ] Schedule follow-up if needed

### Within 24 Hours

- [ ] Send written summary if appropriate
- [ ] Check in on their wellbeing (if appropriate)
- [ ] Reflect on your own emotional response
- [ ] Plan follow-up actions

### Ongoing

- [ ] Follow through on commitments made
- [ ] Monitor progress on agreed actions
- [ ] Continue building the relationship
- [ ] Learn and adjust for next time
