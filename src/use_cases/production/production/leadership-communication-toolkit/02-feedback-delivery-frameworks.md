# Feedback Delivery Frameworks

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**

Feedback isn't about fixing people—it's about investing in their growth and your shared success. These frameworks have evolved from years of getting it wrong, then learning what actually works.

## Positive Reinforcement Template

Positive feedback isn't just "nice to have"—it's the foundation of psychological safety and growth. Most leaders give far too little positive feedback, and what they give is often too vague to be useful.

### The SBI Model for Positive Feedback

**Situation → Behavior → Impact**

#### Template

"I want to share some positive feedback with you. When you [specific situation], you [specific behavior], and the impact was [concrete outcome]. I really appreciated that because [why it matters]."

#### Examples

"During yesterday's client meeting (Situation), you noticed the client's hesitation about the timeline and proactively offered to create a phased approach (Behavior). The impact was that the client immediately relaxed and agreed to move forward (Impact). I really appreciated that because it showed you were listening beyond just the words they were saying (Why it matters)."

"When we lost that key team member last month (Situation), you stepped in to document all their work and created handover materials without being asked (Behavior). The impact was that the team barely missed a beat during the transition (Impact). I appreciated this because it showed your commitment to team stability (Why it matters)."

### Public vs. Private Recognition

#### When to Give Public Feedback

✅ Use for:
- Behaviors you want replicated across the team
- Achievements that benefit the whole group
- Examples of living team values
- Milestones and accomplishments

#### Script for Public Recognition

"I want to take a moment to acknowledge [Name]. [Specific behavior] and [specific impact]. This exemplifies our value of [team value]. Thank you for setting this example."

**IMPORTANT:** Always ask permission before giving public feedback. Some team members prefer private recognition.

"Hi [Name], I'd love to share some positive feedback about [project/work] in our team meeting. Would you be comfortable with that?"

#### When to Give Private Feedback

✅ Use for:
- Sensitive or personal topics
- Feedback that might embarrass them publicly
- Detailed developmental feedback
- When you're unsure of their preference

### The "Appreciation vs. Praise" Distinction

Most leaders give praise when they should give appreciation. There's a difference:

#### Praise (Generic, Evaluative)

"You're so smart!"
"You're a rock star!"
"Great job!"

**Why it fails:** It's vague, evaluative, and creates pressure to maintain an image.

#### Appreciation (Specific, Impact-Oriented)

"I appreciated how you asked questions until you truly understood the problem."
"I value how you always consider the customer perspective in your decisions."
"Thank you for staying calm during that crisis. Your steadiness helped the whole team."

**Why it works:** It's specific, actionable, and focuses on behavior rather than identity.

## Constructive Feedback Structure

### The CED Model

**Context → Experience → Dialogue**

This model replaces the problematic "sandwich method" (positive-negative-positive), which creates confusion and erodes trust.

#### Context

Set the stage clearly and directly.

**Template:** "I want to give you some feedback about [specific topic/behavior]. When is a good time to have that conversation?"

**Why:** Avoids blindsiding them and gives them agency.

#### Experience

Share your observation and its impact using "I" statements.

**Template:** "When you [specific behavior], I experienced [your reaction/emotion]. The impact was [concrete consequence]."

**Examples:**

❌ **Bad:** "You're always late to meetings."
✅ **Good:** "When you arrived 15 minutes late to the last three team meetings (Context), I felt frustrated and the team had to restart discussions (Experience). The impact was that we ran over time and didn't cover everything (Impact)."

❌ **Bad:** "You need to communicate better."
✅ **Good:** "When the project status wasn't updated in the tracker for two weeks (Context), I felt anxious about our timeline (Experience). The impact was that I had to manually check in with you multiple times, which took time away from other work (Impact)."

#### Dialogue

Ask for their perspective and co-create next steps.

**Template:** "That's my experience. I want to hear your perspective. What was happening from your point of view?"

### The AID Model (Alternative)

**Action → Impact → Desired Outcome**

This model works well for performance-focused conversations.

#### Action

What you observed (specific, recent, factual)

"During yesterday's presentation, you read directly from your slides for the entire 30 minutes."

#### Impact

The consequence of that action

"The audience disengaged—people were checking phones and not asking questions. We missed an opportunity to showcase our expertise and build client confidence."

#### Desired Outcome

What you want to see instead

"Going forward, I'd like you to use slides as visual supports, not scripts. I can help you practice speaking more naturally if that would be helpful."

### Handling Defensive Reactions

**When they say:** "That's not what happened!" or "You're wrong!"

**Response:** "Thank you for that correction. My perception was [share what you observed]. Can you help me understand what I missed?"

**When they say:** "Well, what about when YOU did X?"

**Response:** "I hear that you have feedback for me too, and I'm open to that conversation. I want to finish discussing your feedback first. Can we circle back to my feedback after?"

**When they say:** "I didn't mean to..." or "It wasn't my intention..."

**Response:** "I believe that your intention was good. At the same time, impact matters more than intention. Even though you didn't mean to [negative outcome], the result was still [impact]. How can we avoid that impact in the future?"

### Common Feedback Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Better Approach |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Waiting for formal reviews | Too late, loses impact | Give feedback immediately (within 24-48 hours) |
| Being vague ("do better") | Not actionable | Be specific about what to change |
| Focusing on personality | Feels personal, hard to change | Focus on behavior, not character |
| Using "always" or "never" | Triggers defensiveness | Use specific examples, not generalizations |
| Giving too much at once | Overwhelming, nothing sticks | Pick 1-2 key points per conversation |
| Not asking for their perspective | Feels one-way | Make it a dialogue, not a monologue |

### During the Feedback Conversation

#### Active Listening Checklist

- [ ] Listen fully before responding
- [ ] Take notes (don't rely on memory)
- [ ] Ask clarifying questions
- [ ] Paraphrase back to ensure understanding
- [ ] Manage my emotional response
- [ ] Thank them for the feedback

#### Clarifying Questions to Use

- "Can you give me a specific example?"
- "What impact did that have?"
- "What would you like to see differently?"
- "How often do you notice this happening?"
- "Is this a pattern or a one-time thing?"
- "What's the most important thing for me to work on?"

#### Paraphrasing Template

"Let me make sure I understand. You're saying that [paraphrase what you heard]. Is that accurate?"

### Follow-Up Conversation Script

Use this 2-4 weeks after giving feedback:

"Remember when we talked about [topic from previous conversation]? I wanted to check in on how it's going.

What I've noticed since then is [specific observations of change or lack of change].

How do you think it's going?

What support do you need to continue improving?"

### When Improvement Happens

Acknowledge it specifically:

"I want to acknowledge the progress you've made on [specific change]. I've noticed [specific examples of improvement], and the impact is [positive outcome]. Thank you for taking this feedback seriously."

### When Improvement Doesn't Happen

Have a direct conversation:

"We talked about [feedback topic] on [date], and I haven't seen the changes we discussed. The impact is still [negative consequence].

Help me understand what's getting in the way.

What do you need to make this change?"

## Creating a Feedback Culture

Feedback shouldn't just be top-down. Here's how to build a culture where feedback flows in all directions.

### Peer Feedback Framework

Create structure for peer-to-peer feedback:

#### Monthly Feedback Swap

Pair team members and have them exchange feedback using this structure:

1. **What I appreciate about your work** (specific examples)
2. **One area where I've seen you grow** (recent observation)
3. **One suggestion for continued growth** (specific, actionable)

#### Team Retrospective Prompt

At the end of each sprint/project, ask the team:

- What feedback do you have for me as the leader?
- What feedback do you have for each other?
- What feedback do you have for our processes?
- What should we start/stop/continue doing?

### Upward Feedback Framework

Create channels for team to give feedback to leadership:

#### Anonymous Option

Some feedback needs safety of anonymity. Use tools like:
- Anonymous surveys
- Suggestion box (physical or digital)
- Third-party facilitated sessions

#### Direct Option

Encourage direct feedback by modeling receptiveness:

"I know I'm not perfect. I want to hear where I can improve. What's one thing I could do differently to better support you?"

**Then:** When they give feedback, respond with "Thank you," not defensiveness.

## Quick Reference: Feedback Scenarios

### Scenario: Missed Deadline

❌ **Bad:** "You're always late. This is unacceptable."

✅ **Good:** "When the project deliverable was submitted three days past deadline (Context), I felt frustrated and concerned about our client relationship (Experience). The impact was that I had to personally intervene with the client to manage expectations (Impact). What was happening from your perspective?"

### Scenario: Disruptive Meeting Behavior

❌ **Bad:** "You talk too much in meetings."

✅ **Good:** "In today's meeting, you spoke for most of the time while several team members didn't speak at all (Context). I'm concerned that we're not getting diverse perspectives (Experience). The impact is that we might be missing important ideas or concerns (Impact). Can we talk about how to create more balanced participation?"

### Scenario: Poor Written Communication

❌ **Bad:** "Your emails are confusing."

✅ **Good:** "I've noticed that your project updates often require follow-up questions to clarify what you mean (Context). This creates extra communication overhead and delays decision-making (Impact). I'd like to see more structured updates that include: [specific format]. Can we work on that?"

### Scenario: Not Taking Ownership

❌ **Bad:** "You're too passive."

✅ **Good:** "When issues arise on your project, I often see you waiting for direction rather than proposing solutions (Context). I'd like to see more proactive problem-solving (Experience). The impact is that bottlenecks form and decisions get delayed (Impact). What would help you feel more confident taking ownership?"

## Final Thoughts

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**

Every feedback conversation is an opportunity to strengthen or weaken the relationship. Approach feedback not as something you do TO people, but as something you do WITH people.

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

When you give feedback from a place of care and commitment to their growth, people can feel it. When you give feedback from a place of frustration or judgment, they can feel that too.

Start with the relationship. The feedback will follow.
