# Difficult Conversation Script Examples

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

These examples show how the scripts from the main toolkit translate to real conversations. Each is drawn from actual leadership situations, with lessons learned.

---

## Example 1: Performance Discussion

### Context

Team member "Alex" has been missing deadlines and the quality of their work has declined over the past two months. This is the first formal performance conversation.

### Opening Script

**LEADER:** "Hi Alex, thanks for meeting with me today. I want to have an open conversation about how things are going in your role. I've noticed some gaps between expectations and outcomes over the past couple of months, and I want to understand your perspective before we discuss next steps. This conversation is about supporting your success, not about criticism. Can you start by sharing how you think things have been going lately?"

**ALEX:** *[Defensive]* "I think things are going fine. I'm working hard."

**LEADER:** *[Using de-escalation]* "I can hear that you feel like you're putting in a lot of effort. Your hard work is not in question. At the same time, I want to talk about some specific concerns because I care about your success and the team's success. Can we talk about the last two project deliverables?"

### Addressing Specific Concerns

**LEADER:** "When the Q4 report was submitted three days late without any prior communication, the client team was left scrambling and had to delay their own presentation. This matters because our reputation depends on reliability. I also noticed the report had several data errors that had to be corrected after submission. Help me understand what was happening from your perspective."

**ALEX:** *[Getting emotional]* "I've been going through a really hard time personally. My mom is sick and I've been traveling to see her on weekends. I didn't think I could talk about that at work."

**LEADER:** *[Empathetic but clear]* "Alex, I'm so sorry to hear about your mom. That's incredibly difficult, and you should absolutely be able to talk about that at work. We have support resources and we can work with you on accommodations. At the same time, communication is essential. If you had told me about the situation, we could have adjusted deadlines or redistributed work. Going forward, I need you to communicate proactively when you're struggling. Does that make sense?"

### Closing with Clarity

**LEADER:** "Let me recap what we've discussed to make sure I understand:

- You shared that you're dealing with a family health situation that's affecting your work
- We agreed that you'll communicate proactively when you need support
- You'll connect with HR about available resources and leave options
- I'll adjust the next project deadline by one week to give you breathing room
- We'll check in weekly instead of biweekly until things stabilize
- We'll revisit the situation in 30 days to see if further adjustments are needed

Does that capture it accurately? What did I miss?"

**ALEX:** "That sounds right. Thank you for understanding."

### What Worked

1. **Started with their perspective** before sharing concerns
2. **Acknowledged effort** while addressing performance
3. **Responded to emotions** with empathy, not dismissal
4. **Maintained clarity** about expectations despite circumstances
5. **Recapped agreements** explicitly

### What Could Be Better

- Should have noticed signs earlier and initiated conversation sooner
- Could have asked more open-ended questions about personal challenges
- Should follow up within a week to check on both work and personal wellbeing

## Example 2: Behavioral Concern - Team Conflict

### Context

Two senior team members, "Jordan" and "Taylor," have been having conflicts that are affecting the whole team. Their disagreements have become personal and hostile.

### Joint Session Opening

**LEADER:** "I've asked to meet with both of you because there's tension that's affecting the team. My goal isn't to assign blame or determine who's right—my goal is to help you find a way to work together effectively. This conversation is confidential, and I expect both of you to speak respectfully and listen openly. Can we agree to that?"

**JORDAN & TAYLOR:** *[Both nod]*

**LEADER:** "Before we dive in, let's establish some ground rules:

- Each person will have uninterrupted time to share their perspective
- We'll use 'I' statements and speak from our own experience
- We'll focus on the issue, not personal attacks
- We'll seek to understand before seeking to be understood

Does this work for everyone?"

### Structured Sharing

**LEADER:** "Jordan, I'd like you to share your perspective on the situation. Start with 'I felt...' or 'I experienced...' and focus on specific behaviors, not character judgments. Taylor, your role is to listen without responding. You'll have your turn next."

**JORDAN:** "I felt frustrated in yesterday's meeting when Taylor interrupted me three times. I've been working on this project for months and have deep expertise, but I couldn't finish a thought. I felt disrespected and like my contributions weren't valued."

**LEADER:** "Taylor, can you repeat back what you heard Jordan say before you respond?"

**TAYLOR:** *[Reluctantly]* "Jordan felt frustrated because I interrupted them three times in the meeting. They felt disrespected and undervalued."

**LEADER:** "Is that accurate, Jordan?"

**JORDAN:** "Yes."

**LEADER:** "Taylor, your turn. Share your perspective using 'I' statements. Jordan, listen without responding."

**TAYLOR:** "I felt anxious about the timeline. We're behind schedule and I've been getting pressure from leadership. When Jordan was going into detailed explanations, I tried to move us along. I didn't intend to be disrespectful—I was stressed about time."

**LEADER:** "Jordan, can you repeat back what you heard Taylor say?"

**JORDAN:** "Taylor was anxious about the timeline and feeling pressure. They interrupted to keep us moving, not to be disrespectful."

**LEADER:** "I want to identify where you both agree. From what I've heard:

- You both agree that the meeting didn't go well
- You both care about the project's success
- You both felt stressed during the meeting
- Neither of you intended to create personal conflict

Is that accurate?"

**JORDAN & TAYLOR:** *[Both nod]*

### Finding Solutions

**LEADER:** "Let's build from this common ground. What could we do differently in future meetings to respect both Jordan's need to share expertise and Taylor's need to keep things moving?"

**JORDAN:** "Maybe I could prepare a summary document beforehand, so I don't need to explain everything in the meeting?"

**TAYLOR:** "And maybe I could wait until Jordan finishes speaking, then ask clarifying questions instead of jumping in."

**LEADER:** "Those sound like great ideas. Let's agree:

1. Jordan will prepare a one-page brief before the next meeting
2. Taylor will use a 'raised hand' signal when wanting to speak, not interrupt
3. We'll add a 5-minute buffer to meetings to allow for deeper discussion
4. We'll check in after the next meeting to see how this feels

Does that work for both of you?"

**JORDAN & TAYLOR:** "Yes."

### What Worked

1. **Set clear ground rules** before the conversation got heated
2. **Used structured turn-taking** to prevent interruption
3. **Required paraphrasing** to ensure listening
4. **Found common ground** before seeking solutions
5. **Co-created agreements** rather than imposing solutions

### What Could Be Better

- Should have intervened earlier when tensions first arose
- Could have had individual conversations before the joint session
- Should follow up more frequently to ensure agreements stick
- Need to address the broader team dynamic (others were affected)

## Example 3: Role Change Conversation

### Context

Team member "Sam" was hired as a generalist but the role has evolved to require deep specialization in an area where Sam struggles. Need to discuss role change.

### Opening

**LEADER:** "Sam, I want to have an important conversation with you about your role. I've been thinking about how your strengths could be better utilized, and I want to explore a potential change with you. This conversation is about finding the right fit, not about performance concerns. Does that make sense?"

**SAM:** *[Concerned]* "Am I being fired?"

**LEADER:** "No, not at all. This is about fit, not performance. Let me explain. When we hired you, we needed a generalist who could do a bit of everything. Over the past year, the role has evolved. We now need deep specialization in data analysis, and while you've worked hard to develop those skills, it's clear your strengths lie elsewhere—specifically in client communication and project coordination. I want to explore shifting your role to focus more on those areas."

### Presenting Options

**LEADER:** "I see a few possible paths forward, and I want to get your input on which feels right:

**Option 1:** Shift your role to focus 80% on client communication and project coordination, with only 20% data analysis. We'd hire a specialist to handle the deep analysis work.

**Option 2:** Move into a dedicated client success role on another team. That role is 100% focused on client relationships, which I know you excel at.

**Option 3:** Stay in the current role but we invest in intensive training to build your data analysis skills over the next 6 months.

What's your initial reaction to these options?"

**SAM:** *[Relieved but uncertain]* "Honestly, Option 1 sounds most appealing. I love working with clients and managing projects, but I find the data analysis really draining. But would that be seen as a demotion?"

**LEADER:** "Not at all. It's a role evolution based on strengths, not a demotion. The title and compensation would stay the same. In fact, I think you could have more impact and be more successful in this evolved role. Does that help?"

**SAM:** "Yes. So what would this look like practically?"

### Closing with Action Plan

**LEADER:** "Here's what I propose:

- Take this week to think about it and ask me any questions
- Let's meet again on Friday to discuss your decision
- If you choose Option 1, we'll create a 30-day transition plan together
- I'll provide job shadowing opportunities with the client team
- We'll have weekly check-ins during the transition

What questions do you have before we wrap up?"

**SAM:** "What if I try Option 1 and it doesn't work out?"

**LEADER:** "Great question. If after 90 days it's not the right fit, we'll revisit and explore other options. My commitment is to find a role where you can thrive and contribute your best work. That might take some iteration."

### What Worked

1. **Addressed fear directly** (the "am I being fired" question)
2. **Presented multiple options** for agency and choice
3. **Framed as strength-based** not deficit-focused
4. **Provided reassurance** about title, compensation, and exit plan
5. **Created safety** for trying and adjusting

### What Could Be Better

- Should have had this conversation earlier when role evolution first became clear
- Could have involved Sam in the role evolution process before it became a problem
- Need to ensure the transition doesn't feel like a punishment to the rest of the team

## Key Patterns Across Examples

### What Consistently Works

1. **Start with their perspective** before sharing your concerns
2. **Acknowledge emotions** before addressing problems
3. **Be specific** with examples, not generalizations
4. **Recap agreements** explicitly
5. **Follow through** on commitments made

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Waiting too long** to have difficult conversations
2. **Being vague** about concerns
3. **Focusing on character** instead of behavior
4. **Not checking for understanding** before moving on
5. **Making agreements without follow-up**

---

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

These examples show that perfect scripts don't exist. What matters is showing up with care, clarity, and commitment to the relationship. The conversation won't go perfectly—and that's okay.

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**
