## How to Use Each Document

### Document 1: Delegation Decision Matrix
**When to use:** Monthly audit of your workload

**Quick process:**
1. List all tasks from last week
2. Categorize into 4 quadrants:
   - Must delegate (others can do better)
   - Should delegate (growth opportunities)
   - Can't delegate (your core leadership work)
   - Shouldn't do (eliminate or automate)
3. Choose 3-5 tasks to delegate this month

**Time investment:** 30 minutes monthly

---

### Document 2: Delegation Brief Template
**When to use:** Every time you delegate an important task

**Quick process:**
1. Fill out all 5 sections:
   - Context (why it matters)
   - Outcome (what done looks like)
   - Resources (what they can access)
   - Checkpoints (when to check in)
   - Learning (skills development)
2. Schedule 30+ minute handoff meeting
3. Walk through brief together
4. Answer questions and confirm understanding

**Time investment:** 15 minutes to write + 30 minutes to discuss

---

### Document 3: Delegation Readiness Checklist
**When to use:** Before delegating (pre-flight check)

**Quick process:**
1. Run through all 8 items
2. If all checked → proceed with delegation
3. If 6-7 checked → fix gaps before delegating
4. If <6 checked → not ready, consider alternatives

**Time investment:** 5 minutes per delegation

---

### Document 4: Delegation Tracking Log
**When to use:** Ongoing, for all active delegations

**Quick process:**
1. Add task to master log when delegating
2. Update status at each checkpoint
3. Note issues and blockers as they arise
4. Complete lessons learned after task done
5. Review patterns quarterly

**Time investment:** 5 minutes daily + 15 minutes weekly review

## Typical Delegation Workflow

### Step 1: Identify (Decision Matrix)
- Use Decision Matrix to audit workload
- Identify tasks in "Must delegate" and "Should delegate" quadrants
- Prioritize 3-5 tasks for this month

### Step 2: Validate (Readiness Checklist)
- Run Readiness Checklist for each prioritized task
- Confirm team member has capacity and skills
- Fix any gaps before proceeding

### Step 3: Brief (Brief Template)
- Create Delegation Brief for the task
- Be specific about outcomes and success criteria
- Schedule checkpoints and confirm resources

### Step 4: Handoff (Brief + Conversation)
- Meet for 30+ minutes to discuss brief
- Answer all questions
- Confirm understanding (ask them to summarize back)

### Step 5: Monitor (Tracking Log)
- Add to Tracking Log
- Check in at scheduled checkpoints
- Note blockers and provide support
- Update status regularly

### Step 6: Learn (Tracking Log + Debrief)
- Complete task review when done
- Capture lessons learned
- Discuss what worked and what didn't
- Apply insights to future delegations

## Quick Start Scenarios

### Scenario 1: You're Overwhelmed (1 hour intervention)
1. Use Decision Matrix to audit current work (20 min)
2. Identify 3 tasks to delegate immediately (10 min)
3. Run Readiness Checklist for each (10 min)
4. Create Delegation Briefs for each (15 min)
5. Schedule handoff meetings for tomorrow (5 min)

### Scenario 2: Building Delegation Habits (Weekly practice)
1. Weekly: Review Decision Matrix, choose 1-2 tasks to delegate (15 min)
2. Each delegation: Run checklist + create brief (20 min)
3. Daily: Update Tracking Log (5 min)
4. Weekly: Review progress and plan next week (15 min)

### Scenario 3: New Team Member Onboarding (First month)
1. Use Decision Matrix to identify good starter delegations
2. Use Readiness Checklist to ensure appropriate task selection
3. Create detailed Delegation Briefs with extra context
4. Schedule frequent checkpoints (daily → every other day → weekly)
5. Use Tracking Log to monitor progress and build capability
6. Gradually increase complexity and autonomy

## Common Situations

### "I don't have time to delegate"
**Reality:** You don't have time NOT to delegate.
**Approach:** Start with 1 task this week. Invest 1 hour upfront. Save 5+ hours every week thereafter.

### "I've tried delegation and it didn't work"
**Reality:** You probably dumped, not delegated.
**Approach:** Use the Brief Template and Readiness Checklist. Most failed delegations fail because of unclear expectations or insufficient setup.

### "My team is already overloaded"
**Reality:** This is a capacity problem, not a delegation problem.
**Approach:** Use Decision Matrix to eliminate "Shouldn't do" work. Hire if needed. Delegate to build capacity, not add to overload.

### "It's faster to do it myself"
**Reality:** True in the short term, false in the long term.
**Approach:** Calculate the cost. If you delegate once with proper brief, that's 1 hour. If you do the task yourself every week for a year, that's 52 hours. Delegation pays for itself quickly.

### "I'm the only one who can do this right"
**Reality:** Maybe true now, but that's a failure to build team capability.
**Approach:** Document your process. Create templates. Train someone. Be patient through the learning curve. That's leadership.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### The "Inbox Zero" Delegator
- Delegate via email without conversation
- No brief, no checkpoints, no follow-up
- Result: Misalignment, rework, frustration

### The "Set and Forget" Delegator
- Delegate and disappear
- No checkpoints until deadline
- Result: Surprise failures, missed deadlines

### The "Micromanager"
- Delegate but constantly check in
- Rewrite work, question every decision
- Result: No team growth, you still do the work

### The "Grunt Work Only" Delegator
- Only delegate boring, low-visibility tasks
- Keep all interesting work for yourself
- Result: Team can't grow, high turnover

### The "Abdicator"
- Delegate everything including core responsibilities
- Blame team when things go wrong
- Result: Leadership vacuum, team resentment

## Success Patterns to Emulate

### The Development-Focused Delegator
- View delegation as team development, not offloading work
- Choose "Should delegate" tasks for growth
- Connect work to team member's development goals
- Result: Strong team, rising leaders

### The Clear Communicator
- Always writes a brief before delegating
- Schedules proper handoff conversations
- Confirms understanding explicitly
- Result: Clear expectations, fewer rework cycles

### The Available (Not Hovering) Leader
- Sets checkpoints and trusts between them
- Available for questions, doesn't force status updates
- Focused on outcomes, not methods
- Result: Team autonomy, leader visibility

### The Continuous Learner
- Captures lessons learned after every delegation
- Reviews patterns quarterly
- Adapts approach based on what works
- Result: Delegation skills improve over time

## Measuring Delegation Success

**Good signs:**
- Team member delivers work on time
- Quality meets or exceeds expectations
- Team member develops new skills
- You reclaim time for high-value work
- Delegation gets easier over time

**Warning signs:**
- Frequent rework needed
- Missed deadlines or quality issues
- Team member seems overwhelmed or confused
- You're still heavily involved in execution
- Same mistakes repeated across delegations

**Fix warning signs by:**
- Improving your briefs (clearer expectations)
- Adjusting checkpoints (more frequent check-ins)
- Confirming resources and access (remove blockers)
- Training before delegating (build capability)
- Capturing and applying lessons learned (continuous improvement)
