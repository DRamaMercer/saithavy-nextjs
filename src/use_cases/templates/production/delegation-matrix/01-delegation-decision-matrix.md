# Delegation Decision Matrix

> **You don't have to do it all yourself. That's not leadership, that's burnout.**

---

## The 4 Quadrants of Delegation

Use this matrix to decide what to delegate, what to keep, and what to eliminate altogether.

### 1️⃣ MUST DELEGATE (High skill + Low leadership value)

**Characteristics:**
- Others can do it better (or just as well)
- Not the best use of your time as a leader
- Routine tasks that don't require your unique perspective
- Good development opportunities for team members

**Examples:**
- Social media graphics and content scheduling
- Basic research and data gathering
- Data entry and report formatting
- Meeting scheduling and logistics
- Basic customer support inquiries
- Expense report processing
- Document formatting and proofreading

**Action:** Delegate immediately. If no one has capacity, this is a hiring signal.

---

### 2️⃣ SHOULD DELEGATE (Medium skill + Medium leadership value)

**Characteristics:**
- Others can learn to do this
- Good development opportunities
- Requires some judgment but not your unique expertise
- Frees you for higher-impact work

**Examples:**
- Report writing (first drafts)
- Meeting facilitation (team standups, project updates)
- Client updates and status reports
- Presentations (with clear guidance)
- Project coordination and follow-up
- Vendor management (routine relationships)
- Interviewing (initial screening rounds)

**Action:** Delegate with proper briefing and checkpoints. This is your growth engine.

---

### 3️⃣ CAN'T DELEGATE (High skill + High leadership value)

**Characteristics:**
- Your unique contribution as a leader
- Core leadership responsibilities
- Requires your positional authority or expertise
- High-stakes decisions with long-term impact

**Examples:**
- Strategic direction and vision-setting
- Culture-building and team development
- Tough decisions (layoffs, strategy pivots, major investments)
- Performance conversations (direct reports)
- Hiring and firing decisions
- Crisis leadership
- Building key relationships (investors, major clients, partners)

**Action:** Keep these. They're why you're in leadership. Protect time for them ruthlessly.

---

### 4️⃣ SHOULDN'T DO (Low skill + Low leadership value)

**Characteristics:**
- Not worth anyone's time
- Manual processes that could be automated
- Redundant or unnecessary work
- "We've always done it" tasks

**Examples:**
- Manual data entry that could be automated
- Redundant approval processes
- Unnecessary meetings ("could have been an email")
- Outdated reports nobody reads
- Double-handling work (re-doing what others did)
- Work that doesn't advance strategic goals

**Action:** Eliminate or automate. Don't delegate garbage work.

---

## How to Use This Matrix

### Step 1: Audit Your Current Work
List everything you spent time on last week. Be honest.

### Step 2: Categorize Each Task
Place each task in one of the 4 quadrants based on:
- **Skill required**: Does it need YOUR unique skills?
- **Leadership value**: Does it advance your core leadership responsibilities?

### Step 3: Take Action
- **Must delegate**: Identify who can take it
- **Should delegate**: Plan development handoffs
- **Can't delegate**: Protect and schedule this work
- **Shouldn't do**: Eliminate or automate

### Step 4: Review Monthly
Your matrix will shift as your team grows and your role evolves.

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Treating everything as "Can't delegate"**
- This is control, not leadership
- You become the bottleneck
- Your team can't grow

❌ **Only delegating "Must delegate" tasks**
- You miss development opportunities
- "Should delegate" is where team growth happens

❌ **Delegating "Shouldn't do" tasks**
- Don't delegate bad processes to your team
- Fix or eliminate them first

---

## Your Turn

Take 15 minutes to list your current tasks and categorize them.

**Hard truth:** If more than 50% of your time is in "Can't delegate," you're either not delegating enough or you haven't built a team you trust. Both are leadership failures.

**Better approach:** Aim for 20-30% "Can't delegate," 40-50% delegated, and the rest eliminated or automated.
