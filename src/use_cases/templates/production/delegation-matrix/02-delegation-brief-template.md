# Delegation Brief Template

> **"I need you to handle this" is not a delegation. It's abdication.**

Use this brief template whenever you delegate an important task. A clear brief prevents confusion, reduces rework, and sets both you and your team member up for success.

---

## Template Structure

### Section 1: Context
*Why this task matters and how it connects to the bigger picture*

### Section 2: Outcome Definition
*What "done" looks like with clear success criteria*

### Section 3: Resources & Authority
*What they can access and decisions they can make*

### Section 4: Checkpoints
*When and how to check in*

### Section 5: Learning
*Skills they'll develop and why they were chosen*

## Delegation Brief Template

**Task Name:** [Clear, specific task title]

**Delegated to:** [Name]

**Date:** [Delegation date]

**Deadline:** [Final due date]

---

### Section 1: Context

**Why this task matters:**
[Explain the strategic importance. Who will use this? What decision depends on it? What's at stake?]

**How it connects to bigger picture:**
[How does this support team goals? Company objectives? Customer value?]

**Background information needed:**
[Context, history, previous attempts, key players, constraints]

**Example:**
> "This Q3 sales report will inform our budget planning for next year. The finance team needs accurate numbers by August 15th to model different scenarios. Last year's report was late, which delayed our hiring decisions. Your work directly impacts our ability to grow the team next quarter."

---

### Section 2: Outcome Definition

**What "done" looks like:**
[Deliverable specifics - format, length, key elements]

**Success criteria:**
[How will we know this was successful? Be specific. Metrics? Stakeholder approval? Decision made?]

**Quality standards:**
[Accuracy requirements, style guidelines, brand voice, data sources, approval levels]

**Timeline/Milestones:**
- [ ] Draft due: [Date]
- [ ] Review cycle: [Date]
- [ ] Final version: [Date]

**Example:**
> **Done looks like:** A 12-page slide deck with executive summary, KPI dashboard, and 3 strategic recommendations
>
> **Success criteria:** CFO reviews and approves; no major errors in data; clear recommendations that inform budget decisions
>
> **Quality standards:** Use Q2 report template; cite data sources; brand guidelines for charts; get approval from sales director before final
>
> **Timeline:** Draft by Aug 1, review by Aug 8, final by Aug 12

---

### Section 3: Resources & Authority

**Resources available:**
- Budget: [What budget can they access?]
- Tools: [Software, platforms, data sources]
- People: [Who can they collaborate with? Interview? Ask for help?]
- Examples: [Previous work, templates, reference materials]

**Decisions they can make without checking:**
- [Clear authority boundaries - up to $X, up to Y level of commitment, certain types of choices]

**Decisions requiring approval:**
- [What needs to come back to you? What requires stakeholder sign-off?]

**Support available:**
- Office hours: [When can they reach you?]
- Escalation path: [Who to contact if blocked? How to reach you urgently?]

**Example:**
> **Resources:** Access to sales CRM, $500 budget for data tools, can interview sales leads for input, use Q2 report as template
>
> **Authority:** Can make decisions on report format, data visualization choices, and which KPIs to highlight
>
> **Needs approval:** Strategic recommendations (review together), any data assumptions, anything over $500 budget
>
> **Support:** I'm available M/W/F 2-4pm for questions. Slack me anytime if blocked. Sales director can help with data access.

---

### Section 4: Checkpoints

**Check-in schedule:**
- [Date/time]: What to prepare - [Specifics for each checkpoint]
- [Date/time]: What to prepare - [Specifics for each checkpoint]
- [Date/time]: Final review - [Specifics for final sign-off]

**What updates to provide:**
[Status, progress against milestones, blockers, decisions made, questions]

**How to handle roadblocks:**
[Immediate escalation vs. wait for checkpoint; what constitutes a blocker]

**Final handoff:**
[How will they deliver the work? What does final approval look like?]

**Example:**
> **Checkpoints:**
> - **July 25 (30-min)**: Bring outline + data sources identified. We'll review approach.
> - **Aug 1 (45-min)**: Bring draft report. We'll review content together.
> - **Aug 8 (30-min)**: Bring revised version after sales director feedback.
> - **Aug 12 (final)**: Submit final version for CFO review.
>
> **Updates:** Email me brief status every Friday. Slack me immediately if blocked on data access.
>
> **Roadblocks:** If you can't access data within 24 hours, escalate to me. If unsure about strategic direction, don't guess - bring questions to checkpoint.

---

### Section 5: Learning

**Skills they'll develop:**
[What capabilities will they build? Data analysis? Stakeholder management? Strategic thinking?]

**Why I chose you for this:**
[Be specific about their strengths, growth areas, or development goals]

**Feedback approach:**
[How will you provide feedback? When? What's the process?]

**Long-term development:**
[How does this fit their growth path? What opportunities might this unlock?]

**Example:**
> **Skills you'll develop:** Strategic analysis (connecting data to business decisions), executive communication (clear recommendations for CFO), cross-functional collaboration (working with sales and finance)
>
> **Why I chose you:** You've shown strong analytical skills in client reports. This is your chance to apply that thinking internally and build executive presence. You mentioned wanting more strategic projects - this is one.
>
> **Feedback approach:** We'll discuss feedback at each checkpoint. After final delivery, we'll do a 30-min review of what went well and what to improve for next time.
>
> **Long-term:** If this goes well, you'll own quarterly reporting going forward. It's a path toward more strategic planning work.

## Real Example: Delegating a Client Proposal

### Task: Acme Corp Proposal Revision

**Context:**
"Acme Corp is a $500k opportunity we've been chasing for 6 months. They liked our first proposal but want more detail on implementation timeline and risk mitigation. This is our shot to close them - they're deciding this month. Your work directly impacts whether we hit Q3 revenue targets."

**Outcome:**
- Done looks like: 20-page proposal with implementation timeline, risk matrix, and case studies
- Success criteria: Client approves; VP of Sales signs off; addresses all 5 questions from their email
- Quality: Use proposal v3 template; include 2 case studies; get technical review before final
- Timeline: Draft by Wednesday, review Thursday, final Friday

**Resources & Authority:**
- Resources: Can interview implementation lead for details; use proposal template library; 2 hours of my time for questions
- Authority: Can choose which case studies to include; can propose timeline structure; can offer up to 10% discount without approval
- Needs approval: Any discount over 10%, timeline commitments over 6 months, any scope changes
- Support: I'm available 10am-12pm daily. Implementation lead (Sarah) is expecting your call.

**Checkpoints:**
- Monday 10am: Bring outline with risk categories and timeline structure
- Wednesday 2pm: Bring full draft. We'll edit together.
- Thursday 10am: Bring revised draft after technical review.
- Friday: Final version to client.

**Learning:**
- Skills: Client relationship management, risk analysis, persuasive writing for executive audiences
- Why you: You wrote great case studies last month. You understand this client's industry. You're ready for more client-facing work.
- Feedback: We'll debrief after client response. Win or lose, there's learning here.
- Long-term: Leading small proposals is the path to owning major opportunities. This is your next step.

## Common Delegation Brief Mistakes

### ❌ Vague outcome definition
**Bad:** "Write a report on sales performance"
**Good:** "Create a 12-page Q3 sales report with KPI dashboard, variance analysis, and 3 strategic recommendations for CFO review by August 15"

### ❌ No context about why it matters
**Bad:** "I need you to handle the Acme proposal"
**Good:** "Acme is a $500k opportunity and our decision month. This proposal is make-or-break for Q3 targets."

### ❌ Unclear authority boundaries
**Bad:** "Make decisions as needed" (too vague) or "Check with me on everything" (micromanagement)
**Good:** "You can decide on timeline structure and case studies. Check with me on any discount over 10% or scope changes."

### ❌ No checkpoint schedule
**Bad:** "Let me know when you're done"
**Good:** "We'll review outline Monday, draft Wednesday, and have final by Friday"

### ❌ Missing learning connection
**Bad:** [No mention of why they were chosen or what they'll learn]
**Good:** "You're ready for more strategic work. This builds executive communication and strategic thinking - both are on your development plan."

---

## Quick Checklist Before Delegating

□ Context is clear (why it matters, how it connects to bigger picture)
□ Outcome is specific (what done looks like, success criteria, quality standards)
□ Resources are identified (budget, tools, people, examples)
□ Authority is clear (what they can decide vs. what needs approval)
□ Checkpoints are scheduled (specific dates/times with preparation expectations)
□ Learning connection is made (skills, why them, feedback approach)

**If any box is unchecked, fill it in before the handoff meeting.**

---

*A delegation brief takes 15-20 minutes to write and saves hours of confusion, rework, and frustration. It's the difference between "I need you to handle this" (abdication) and "Here's the mission, here are the resources, here's how we'll stay aligned" (leadership).*
