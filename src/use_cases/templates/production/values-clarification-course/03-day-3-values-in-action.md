**Values aren't what you believe. Values are what you do.**

I can value "honesty" but lie in every difficult conversation. I don't value honesty. I value comfort or approval or avoiding conflict.

**Values are lived, not thought.**

## How Values Show Up in Daily Life

Every value shows up as behaviors.

**If you value "Freedom":**
- You might avoid long-term commitments
- You might quit jobs that feel restrictive
- You might prioritize flexibility over stability
- You might resist being told what to do

**If you value "Security":**
- You might save aggressively
- You might stay in safe jobs even if you're unhappy
- You might avoid risks
- You might prioritize stability over growth

**If you value "Connection":**
- You might prioritize time with friends
- You might struggle when relationships feel distant
- You might make decisions based on impact on others
- You might feel energized by deep conversations

**Same value, different behaviors for different people.**

## Values Conflicts: When Values Compete

Here's where it gets tricky: Your values aren't always aligned.

Sometimes they compete.

**Example: Freedom vs. Security**
- Freedom says: Quit your job and start something new
- Security says: Stay in your stable job with benefits

Both are your values. Which one wins?

**Example: Growth vs. Comfort**
- Growth says: Have that hard conversation
- Comfort says: Avoid conflict and keep the peace

Both are your values. How do you choose?

**Example: Achievement vs. Balance**
- Achievement says: Work late and hit your goals
- Balance says: Go home and spend time with family

Both are your values. What do you do?

## The Values Hierarchy

Not all values are equal.

You have a values hierarchy. Some values trump others.

For me: **Learning > Approval** (usually)
- I'll risk disapproval if I'm learning something

But sometimes: **Security > Growth** (historically)
- I'll stay in a safe situation even if I'm not growing

Your values hierarchy explains why you make the choices you do.

It also explains why some decisions feel so hard—two high-priority values are in conflict.

## How to Live Your Values

Living your values isn't complicated. It just requires intentionality.

**For each of your core values, you need to know:**

1. **What does living this value look like daily?**
   - Specific behaviors
   - Concrete actions
   - Real examples

2. **What boundaries protect this value?**
   - What you'll say no to
   - What you won't compromise
   - Lines you won't cross

3. **What relationships support this value?**
   - People who share this value
   - Environments that honor this value
   - Communities that reinforce this value

4. **What conflicts with this value?**
   - Situations that violate it
   - People who don't respect it
   - Choices that compromise it

## My "Learning" Value in Action

**What it looks like daily:**
- Reading for 30 minutes every morning
- Taking notes on what I learn
- Trying to apply one thing per week

**Boundaries that protect it:**
- "No meetings before 11am" (protects learning time)
- "I won't work on projects where I'm not learning"
- "I'll pay for courses/books even when money is tight"

**Relationships that support it:**
- Friends who share interesting ideas
- Mentors who teach me
- Online communities of learners

**What conflicts with it:**
- Jobs that are repetitive and unchallenging
- People who mock my curiosity
- Environments that don't value growth

See how specific this is?

**Vague values = no action.**
**Specific values = clear action.**

## The Trap: Philosophical Values

Most people keep their values philosophical.

"I value growth." (But what does that mean?)

"I value connection." (But how do you live it?)

"I value excellence." (But what does that look like?)

**Philosophical values are useless.**

Values need to become behaviors. They need to become choices. They need to become actions.
