I want to start with something uncomfortable: Most of us don't know our values.

We know what we *wish* our values were. We know what values *sound good*. We know what values *successful people are supposed to have*.

But our actual values—the ones driving our decisions, shaping our relationships, determining our satisfaction?

Those remain invisible.

## What Are Values (Really)?

Values aren't beliefs. They aren't goals. They aren't what you think you should care about.

Values are what you *already* care about.

They're your internal compass. They're how you judge if something is "good" or "bad," "worth it" or "waste of time," "meaningful" or "empty."

**Values are what you use to evaluate your life.**

The problem? Most of us never examine this compass. We assume it's pointing true north. Meanwhile, we're wandering in circles, wondering why we keep ending up places we didn't intend to go.

## The Values Mistake I Made For Years

I used to think my values were: Achievement, Excellence, Impact, Growth, Leadership.

These sounded good. These were what successful people valued. These were what I *should* value.

But when I looked at my life—how I spent my time, what decisions I made, what brought me genuine satisfaction—I discovered something different.

**My actual values were:** Security, Approval, Control, Independence, Learning.

Not what I wanted them to be. What they *actually were*.

This mismatch explained everything:
- Why I stayed in jobs I hated (Security > Growth)
- Why I couldn't say no to requests (Approval > Boundaries)
- Why I micromanaged everything (Control > Trust)
- Why I struggled with authority (Independence > Collaboration)
- Why I loved reading business books but implementing nothing (Learning > Action)

I wasn't living my values. I wasn't violating my values. I was living someone else's values while pretending they were mine.

## Why This Matters

Your values determine:
- What career path will satisfy you vs. drain you
- Which relationships will fulfill you vs. frustrate you
- What "success" means for you (not society's definition)
- Why some decisions feel easy and others feel impossible
- Where your guilt and dissatisfaction come from

**When you don't know your values, you can't make good decisions.**

You're using someone else's compass to navigate your life.

## Today's Exercise: Values Card Sort

I've included a list of 50 common values in the workbook. Here's what to do:

**Round 1: Choose your top 20**
- Don't overthink this
- Go with your gut reaction
- These should feel like "yes, that's me"

**Round 2: Choose your top 10**
- Harder now
- Look at your top 20
- Ask: "Which of these are non-negotiable?"

**Round 3: Choose your top 5**
- Hardest round
- These are your core values
- These define you

**Important Rule:** You're not choosing what you *wish* your values were. You're choosing what they *actually are*.

If "Approval" is in your top 5, own it. If "Comfort" is there, accept it. If "Achievement" is missing, acknowledge it.

This is about truth, not aspiration.

## What You'll Discover

Most people find this exercise revealing. Sometimes uncomfortable. Occasionally painful.

You'll find values you didn't know you had. You'll find "values" you thought were yours aren't. You'll find explanations for choices that never made sense before.

This is the foundation.

Everything else this week builds on what you discover today.
