# Non-Verbal Communication Awareness

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

Research suggests that 60-93% of communication is non-verbal. Yet most leadership communication training focuses almost entirely on words. This guide fills that gap.

I've learned the hard way that what I don't say often communicates more than what I do. This guide shares those lessons.

## Body Language Observation Guide

### Reading Others' Body Language

**Important Note:** Body language is context-dependent. These signals are clues, not definitive conclusions. Always consider the situation, culture, and individual baseline.

#### Signals of Engagement

**Open and Receptive:**
- Leaning forward slightly
- Nodding while listening
- Maintaining appropriate eye contact
- Arms uncrossed, hands visible
- Feet pointed toward the speaker
- Mirroring the other person's body language

**What it means:** They're interested, open, and actively listening.

**How to respond:** Continue your approach. You have engagement.

#### Signals of Disengagement

**Checked Out:**
- Leaning back or away
- Little or no nodding
- Looking at phone, watch, or around the room
- Arms crossed, body turned away
- Blank or neutral expression
- Feet pointed toward exit

**What it means:** They're bored, distracted, or closed off.

**How to respond:**
- "I want to make sure I'm not losing you. What are your thoughts?"
- Check if they need a break
- Ask them a direct question to re-engage

#### Signals of Discomfort or Anxiety

**Uncomfortable:**
- Fidgeting (tapping, touching face, shifting weight)
- Avoiding eye contact or staring too intensely
- Forced smile or no smile at all
- Rigid posture
- Blushing or pale complexion
- Shallow or rapid breathing

**What it means:** They're stressed, anxious, or hiding something.

**How to respond:**
- "You seem uncomfortable. Is there something I'm missing?"
- "I want this to be a safe conversation. What do you need?"
- Slow down, create more psychological safety

#### Signals of Agreement

**Aligned:**
- Nodding while listening
- Smiling genuinely (eyes crinkle)
- Open posture
- Leaning in
- Mirroring your expressions
- Relaxed muscles

**What it means:** They're on board, engaged, and aligned.

**How to respond:**
- Move to action
- Confirm the agreement explicitly
- Document the alignment

#### Signals of Disagreement or Skepticism

**Not Convinced:**
- Furrowed brow or squinting
- Arms crossed tightly
- Leaning back with chin tucked
- pursed lips or tight jaw
- Looking away or breaking eye contact frequently
- No nodding or head shaking slightly

**What it means:** They have doubts or disagree.

**How to respond:**
- "I'm sensing some skepticism. What concerns do you have?"
- "Help me understand what doesn't sit right with you."
- Don't push harder—ask for their perspective

### Managing Your Own Body Language

#### Authenticity Over Performance

**Rule:** Your body tells the truth even when your words don't. Trying to "perform" confident body language often backfires because it looks inauthentic.

**Better approach:** Actually feel what you're communicating. If you're nervous, acknowledge it. If you're uncertain, say so. Authenticity builds trust; performance destroys it.

#### Leadership Presence Basics

**Posture:**
- Sit or stand tall (not rigid, just aligned)
- Lean forward slightly to show engagement
- Keep your torso open (not crossed arms)
- Face your torso toward the person you're speaking with

**Eyes:**
- Maintain eye contact, but don't stare (60-70% of the time while listening, 40-50% while speaking)
- Look at one person at a time in groups (not scanning)
- Break eye contact occasionally to avoid intensity
- Notice your own patterns—do you look away when nervous?

**Hands:**
- Keep hands visible (not hidden in pockets or under the table)
- Use gestures to emphasize points, but not constantly
- Open palms signal honesty and openness
- Avoid pointing (accusatory) or chopping (aggressive)

**Face:**
- Express emotions appropriate to the content
- Smile when it's genuine, not as a default
- Furrow your brow when something is serious
- Relax your jaw and shoulders

**Movement:**
- Move with purpose, not fidgeting
- Stand or sit still when making important points
- Use movement to transition between topics
- Avoid swaying, bouncing, or constant shifting

### Environmental Non-Verbals

**Physical Space:**

| Setting | Optimal Distance | What It Signals |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| Casual conversation | 4-8 feet | Friendship, approachability |
| Work discussion | 3-5 feet | Collaboration |
| Difficult conversation | 4-6 feet | Respect, not intimidation |
| Presentation | 8+ feet from audience | Authority, clarity |

**Seating Arrangements:**

**Collaborative (Best for teamwork):**
- Round table or no table
- Everyone at same level
- No head of table

**Authoritative (Best for decisions):**
- Rectangular table
- Leader at head
- Clear hierarchy

**Confrontational (Avoid if possible):**
- Directly across from each other
- Creates oppositional dynamic

**Better approach:** Sit at an angle to the person, not directly across

**Room Setup:**
- Remove physical barriers between you and them
- Ensure good lighting (you want your face visible)
- Control temperature (too hot/cold creates discomfort)
- Minimize distractions (noise, movement, visual clutter)

## Tone and Word Choice Checklist

### Tone Awareness

Your tone includes volume, pitch, speed, and intensity. It communicates emotion, certainty, and relationship.

#### Tone Self-Audit

**Before important conversations, ask yourself:**

1. **What emotion am I feeling right now?**
   - [ ] Calm
   - [ ] Frustrated
   - [ ] Anxious
   - [ ] Excited
   - [ ] Angry
   - [ ] Sad
   - [ ] Uncertain

2. **What tone do I want to convey?**
   - [ ] Confident but not arrogant
   - [ ] Warm but professional
   - [ ] Direct but kind
   - [ ] Certain but open to input
   - [ ] Serious but hopeful

3. **What might my default tone be?**
   - [ ] Too fast when nervous
   - [ ] Too loud when excited
   - [ ] Too quiet when uncertain
   - [ ] Too flat when stressed
   - [ ] Too sharp when frustrated

#### Matching Tone to Context

**Delivering Difficult Feedback:**
- **Tone:** Calm, clear, compassionate
- **Volume:** Moderate, steady
- **Speed:** Slower than normal
- **Pitch:** Natural, not elevated

**Sharing Exciting News:**
- **Tone:** Enthusiastic, genuine, warm
- **Volume:** Slightly elevated
- **Speed:** Energized but not rushed
- **Pitch:** Natural variation

**Making a Hard Decision:**
- **Tone:** Serious, resolute, empathetic
- **Volume:** Moderate, steady
- **Speed:** Deliberate, not rushed
- **Pitch:** Lower, grounded

**Uncertain Situation:**
- **Tone:** Honest, transparent, reassuring
- **Volume:** Moderate
- **Speed:** Deliberate
- **Pitch:** Natural, not too high (which signals anxiety)

### Word Choice Awareness

The words you choose shape how your message is received. Here's how to choose well.

#### Inclusivity Checklist

**Before speaking, ask:**

- [ ] **Am I using gendered language unnecessarily?**
  - Instead of "guys" → "folks," "team," "everyone"
  - Instead of "manpower" → "workforce," "team members"
  - Instead of "maternity leave" → "parental leave"

- [ ] **Am I using idioms or metaphors that not everyone understands?**
  - Sports metaphors (not everyone follows sports)
  - Cultural references (not everyone shares your background)
  - Industry jargon (not everyone has your expertise)

- [ ] **Am I making assumptions?**
  - "Obviously..." → Nothing is obvious
  - "As you know..." → They might not
  - "Everyone agrees..." → They probably don't

- [ ] **Am I using unnecessarily complex language?**
  - Instead of "utilize" → "use"
  - Instead of "conceptualize" → "think about"
  - Instead of "operationalize" → "implement"

### Word Choice Patterns

#### Certainty vs. Curiosity

**Too Certain (Closes Discussion):**
- "This is the only way."
- "We have to do this."
- "There's no other option."

**Balanced Certainty (Confident but Open):**
- "Based on what we know, this seems like the best approach."
- "I'm leaning toward this option. What do you think?"
- "This is my recommendation, but I'm open to other perspectives."

**Too Uncertain (Undermines Confidence):**
- "I think maybe we could..."
- "I'm not sure, but..."
- "This might be a good idea..."

**Balanced Uncertainty (Honest but Reassuring):**
- "I don't have all the answers yet, but here's what I'm thinking."
- "There's some uncertainty, and here's how we're approaching it."
- "I'm still forming my view. What's your take?"

#### Directness vs. Softness

**Too Direct (Can Feel Harsh):**
- "You're wrong."
- "This won't work."
- "That's a bad idea."

**Direct but Kind:**
- "I see it differently."
- "I have concerns about this approach."
- "I don't think this will work because..."

**Too Soft (Unclear):**
- "I was wondering if perhaps we might consider..."
- "I sort of feel like maybe..."
- "I'm not sure if this is right, but..."

**Balanced (Clear and Compassionate):**
- "I have a different perspective."
- "I'd like to suggest an alternative."
- "Here's what I'm thinking."

## Cross-Cultural Communication Tips

### Universal Principles

**What's Universal:**
- Smiling (in most cultures) signals warmth
- Direct eye contact (duration varies by culture)
- Open posture signals receptiveness
- Nodding signals agreement (mostly)

**What Varies by Culture:**
- How much eye contact is appropriate
- Personal space preferences
- Directness vs. indirectness
- Emotional expression
- Hierarchy and formality
- Time orientation

---

### Cultural Dimension Awareness

#### Direct vs. Indirect Communication

**Direct Cultures** (e.g., US, Germany, Netherlands):
- Say what they mean
- Value explicit communication
- "Yes" means yes, "no" means no
- Feedback is straightforward

**Indirect Cultures** (e.g., Japan, China, Arab cultures):
- Meaning is between the lines
- Value harmony and face-saving
- "Yes" might mean "I hear you," not agreement
- Feedback is subtle

**Tip:** Learn the communication style of those you work with. When in doubt, ask: "I want to make sure I understand. Can you help me see what you're really saying?"

#### Individual vs. Collective Orientation

**Individualist Cultures** (e.g., US, UK, Australia):
- Personal goals over group goals
- Individual recognition
- Direct confrontation is acceptable
- "I" statements

**Collectivist Cultures** (e.g., Japan, China, Latin America):
- Group goals over individual goals
- Group recognition
- Harmony over confrontation
- "We" statements

**Tip:** In mixed settings, use both "I" and "we." Recognize individual contributions within the context of team effort.

#### Power Distance

**Low Power Distance** (e.g., US, Australia, Scandinavia):
- Hierarchies are informal
- Subordinates expect to be consulted
- Approachable leadership
- First names are common

**High Power Distance** (e.g., China, Mexico, Philippines):
- Hierarchies are respected
- Subordinates expect direction
- Formal leadership
- Titles are important

**Tip:** Match the formality level of the person you're communicating with. When in doubt, start more formal and let them invite informality.

#### Time Orientation

**Monochronic (Time as Line)** (e.g., US, Germany, Switzerland):
- Time is limited and valuable
- Punctuality is important
- One thing at a time
- Schedules are rigid

**Polychronic (Time as Fluid)** (e.g., Latin America, Mediterranean, Middle East):
- Relationships over schedules
- Punctuality is flexible
- Multiple things at once
- Schedules are guidelines

**Tip:** Be explicit about time expectations. "We have 30 minutes for this meeting" creates clarity regardless of cultural orientation.

### Inclusive Language Practices

#### Pronouns and Identity

**Best Practices:**
- Share your pronouns: "My name is [Name], my pronouns are she/her."
- Ask others' pronouns: "What pronouns do you use?"
- Use they/them as singular when unknown: "Someone left their bag here."
- Apologize and move on if you make a mistake: "Sorry, I meant [correct pronoun]."

#### Disability-Inclusive Language

**Avoid:**
- "Confined to a wheelchair" → "Uses a wheelchair"
- "Suffering from..." → "Has..." or "Living with..."
- "Handicapped" → "Person with a disability"
- "Mental patient" → "Person with mental health condition"

**Principle:** Person-first language. The person is not their condition.

#### Age-Inclusive Language

**Avoid:**
- "Senior moment" (ageist)
- "Okay, boomer" (dismissive)
- "Fresh/new/young" as inherently positive

**Better:**
- Focus on experience level, not age
- Value diverse generational perspectives
- Avoid assumptions about tech skills based on age

## Virtual Presence Guidelines

### Video Communication

**Camera On/Off:**

**When to keep camera ON:**
- One-on-one conversations
- Team meetings under 10 people
- Difficult conversations
- Brainstorming or collaborative work
- When you're presenting

**When camera OFF is okay:**
- Large meetings (15+ people) where bandwidth is an issue
- Status updates where you're mostly listening
- When you're having a bad body-image day (be honest about this)
- When your environment is distracting to others

**Tip:** If you need camera off, say why: "I'm turning my camera off so I can move around a bit. I'm still fully present."

**Virtual Eye Contact:**

- Look at the camera lens when speaking, not the screen
- Look at faces on screen when listening
- If you forget (everyone does), just shift back to camera
- Don't stare—break "eye contact" periodically

**Lighting and Background:**

- Light source should be in front of you, not behind
- Face should be clearly visible
- Background should be uncluttered (blur or virtual background if needed)
- Eye level should be aligned with camera (not looking down or up)

**Audio Quality:**

- Use headphones/earbuds to reduce echo
- Mute when not speaking
- Find a quiet space if possible
- Test audio before important meetings

---

### Virtual Meeting Presence

**Engagement Cues:**

- Nod while listening (visible on camera)
- Use reactions (thumbs up, clap)
- Use chat for questions and comments
- Verbalize non-verbal responses: "I'm nodding in agreement"
- Take notes visibly (shows engagement)

**Managing Virtual Fatigue:**

- Take breaks between meetings (5 minutes minimum)
- Schedule shorter meetings (45 min instead of 60)
- Mix video and non-video communication
- Turn off self-view (distraction)
- Move, stretch, or change environments between meetings

**Virtual Norms to Establish:**

- "Cameras on unless..."
- "Mute when not speaking"
- "Use reactions to show agreement without interrupting"
- "Use chat for side questions"
- "Take breaks every 60 minutes"

---

### Virtual vs. In-Person Differences

| Aspect | In-Person | Virtual |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Reading reactions** | Immediate, full body | Mostly face, harder to read |
| **Side conversations** | Easy, spontaneous | Hard, disruptive |
| **Breaks** | Natural, informal | Need to be explicit |
| **Participation** | Easier to jump in | Harder, requires structure |
| **Focus** | Natural, but distractions exist | Effortful, more distractions |
| **Connection** | Easier, more natural | Harder, requires intention |

**Virtual-Specific Challenges:**

- **Delayed reactions:** Audio lag makes conversation awkward
- **No body language:** You only see the face
- **Self-consciousness:** Seeing yourself is distracting
- **Home intrusions:** Kids, pets, partners, doorbells
- **Tech failures:** Connection issues, software problems

**Mitigation:**

- Build in pause time for reactions
- Verbalize what you're feeling
- Hide self-view
- Set boundaries with family/housemates
- Have backup tech plans

---

### Asynchronous Communication

**When to use async:**
- Information sharing (announcements, updates)
- Status reports (can be written)
- Feedback that doesn't need real-time discussion
- Work across time zones
- Deep work that shouldn't be interrupted

**Best practices:**
- Be more explicit than in person (no tone or body language)
- Use emoji to convey tone (but not excessively)
- Over-communicate context
- Assume positive intent
- Respond within agreed-upon timeframes

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

Non-verbal communication isn't performance—it's authenticity. The goal isn't to perfectly control every gesture and expression. The goal is to be present enough that your non-verbals align with your intent.

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**

People don't remember what you said as much as they remember how you made them feel. Your presence—your body, your tone, your attention—communicates more than your words ever could.

Show up fully. That's the best leadership communication you can offer.
