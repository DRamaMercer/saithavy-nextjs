# Feedback Framework Examples

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**

These examples show how feedback frameworks translate to real conversations. Each includes what was said, what happened next, and lessons learned.

---

## Example 1: Positive Reinforcement Using SBI

### Situation

Team member "Morgan" proactively stepped in during a crisis when a key team member had an emergency.

### The Feedback

**LEADER:** "Morgan, I want to share some positive feedback with you. When Alex had that family emergency last week and had to leave immediately during the client presentation (Situation), you stepped in without missing a beat, picked up their talking points, and seamlessly continued the presentation (Behavior). The impact was that the client had no idea anything was wrong, the team stayed calm, and we closed the deal (Impact). I really appreciated that because it showed your commitment to the team and ability to perform under pressure (Why it matters)."

**MORGAN:** "Thanks! I was nervous, but Alex and I had practiced together, so I knew the material."

**LEADER:** "That preparation showed. It's a great example for the team of stepping up when needed."

### What Happened Next

- Morgan's confidence increased in client-facing situations
- Team asked Morgan to share their approach in a team meeting
- Morgan became the designated backup for all client presentations
- Morgan eventually moved into a client lead role

### Why It Worked

1. **Specific situation** (not generic "good job")
2. **Observable behavior** (exactly what Morgan did)
3. **Clear impact** (tangible results)
4. **Personal meaning** (why it mattered to the leader)
5. **Link to values** (teamwork, preparation)

### What Could Be Better

- **Ask permission before giving feedback publicly**
- **Connect to Morgan's goals** (does she want more client-facing work?)
- **Make it a dialogue** ("How did that feel for you?")

## Example 2: Constructive Feedback Using CED

### Situation

Team member "Riley" has been dominating meetings, interrupting others, and not letting people finish their thoughts.

### Context Setting

**LEADER (via email):** "Riley, I want to give you some feedback about team dynamics in meetings. When is a good time to have that conversation?"

**RILEY:** "How about tomorrow at 2pm?"

**LEADER:** "That works. See you then."

### The Feedback Conversation

**LEADER:** "Thanks for meeting with me. I want to talk about meeting dynamics.

When you interrupt other team members and finish their thoughts before they've finished speaking (Experience), I've noticed that several people have stopped speaking up in meetings (Impact). This matters because we need diverse perspectives to make good decisions, and I'm concerned we're losing important input (Why it matters).

That's my experience. I want to hear your perspective. What was happening from your point of view?"

**RILEY:** *[Defensive]* "I'm just trying to be helpful! When I know what someone is going to say, I want to move things along. I don't see why that's a problem."

**LEADER:** *[Acknowledging then redirecting]* "I believe that your intention is to be helpful and keep things moving. At the same time, impact matters more than intention. Even though you mean well, the impact is that people feel unheard and are shutting down.

Have you noticed that in recent meetings, the same 2-3 people do most of the talking?"

**RILEY:** *[Pausing to think]* "Now that you mention it, yeah. Jordan hardly speaks anymore."

**LEADER:** "That's what I'm seeing too. I value your enthusiasm and ideas. At the same time, I need you to create space for others. Can we talk about what that could look like?"

**RILEY:** "I guess I could wait until people finish speaking before I respond?"

**LEADER:** "That would be great. Even better—what if you intentionally paused for 3 seconds before responding? That gives others time to jump in."

**RILEY:** "I can try that."

**LEADER:** "I'll also give you a signal in meetings if you're dominating—if I hold up my hand, that's a cue to pause and create space. Does that work?"

**RILEY:** "Yeah, I appreciate that. I really don't mean to shut people down."

**LEADER:** "I know that. Let's check in after next week's meetings to see how it's going."

### What Happened Next

- Riley was conscious about interrupting but still struggled
- Leader used the hand signal in meetings (subtle but effective)
- Riley asked for a reminder before the next meeting
- After two weeks, meeting participation became more balanced
- Jordan and others started speaking up more

### Why It Worked

1. **Set up the conversation in advance** (didn't blindside)
2. **Shared the experience clearly** (specific behavior)
3. **Explained the impact** (why it matters)
4. **Asked for their perspective** (dialogue, not monologue)
5. **Acknowledged good intentions** while addressing impact
6. **Co-created the solution** (not imposed)
7. **Created ongoing support** (the hand signal)

### What Could Be Better

- **Could have asked Riley more questions** about why they feel compelled to jump in
- **Should acknowledge this pattern earlier** (waited too long)
- **Need to reinforce positive change** when Riley creates space for others

## Example 3: Receiving Feedback (Leader as Recipient)

### Situation

Team member "Jordan" gives feedback to their leader about how meetings are run.

### The Feedback

**JORDAN:** "Can I give you some feedback about how you're running meetings?"

**LEADER:** *[Managing internal defensiveness]* "Absolutely. I value your perspective. Tell me what you're seeing."

**JORDAN:** "When you share your opinion early in meetings, I've noticed that the team often aligns with you instead of sharing their own perspectives. It feels like you're anchoring the conversation, and we lose diverse thinking."

**LEADER:** *[Pausing to process]* "Thank you for sharing that. Can you give me a specific example?"

**JORDAN:** "In yesterday's meeting, you said 'I think we should go with Option B' before anyone else had spoken. After that, everyone just agreed with you."

**LEADER:** "I see. And what impact did that have?"

**JORDAN:** "I had concerns about Option B but didn't feel comfortable bringing them up after you'd taken a position. Later, I realized Option A was actually better for the timeline."

**LEADER:** "That's really helpful feedback, and I appreciate you being direct. I didn't realize I was doing that, and I can see how it shuts down other perspectives. What would you suggest I do differently?"

**JORDAN:** "Maybe hold your opinion until everyone else has shared? Or frame your thoughts as 'one perspective' rather than a position?"

**LEADER:** "Both of those are great suggestions. I'll try that in the next meeting. Can you give me a signal if you notice me slipping back into old patterns?"

**JORDAN:** "Sure."

### What Happened Next

- Leader was conscious about not sharing opinions early
- Team meetings became more participatory
- Jordan gave a subtle signal in a later meeting when leader started anchoring
- Leader adjusted in the moment
- Team reported higher satisfaction with meetings

### Why It Worked

1. **Leader managed their reaction** (didn't get defensive)
2. **Asked for clarification** (specific example)
3. **Acknowledged the impact** (validated Jordan's experience)
4. **Asked for suggestions** (didn't just accept, asked for solutions)
5. **Created accountability** (asked for signals)
6. **Actually changed behavior** (not just lip service)

### What Could Be Better

- **Could have thanked Jordan publicly** for the feedback (with permission)
- **Should check in with the whole team** about meeting dynamics
- **Could share this learning** with other leaders

## Example 4: Giving Feedback Upward (to Your Boss)

### Situation

Team member "Taylor" needs to give feedback to their leader about communication style.

### Preparation (Internal Work)

**Taylor's self-reflection before the conversation:**

1. **What's my goal?** Improve team communication, not complain
2. **What am I afraid of?** Retaliation, being seen as difficult
3. **What's true?** The communication style is causing real problems
4. **What support do I need?** None, but need to be prepared for defensiveness

### The Feedback Conversation

**TAYLOR:** "Do you have a few minutes? I'd like to share some feedback about team communication."

**LEADER:** "Sure."

**TAYLOR:** "When you make decisions without consulting the team and then announce them as fait accompli, I feel frustrated and demoralized (Experience). The impact is that the team feels unheard and motivation is suffering (Impact). I want to understand what's driving this approach (Dialogue)."

**LEADER:** *[Defensive]* "I'm trying to be decisive! Consultation takes forever and slows everything down."

**TAYLOR:** *[Staying calm]* "I understand the need for decisiveness. At the same time, there's a middle ground between no consultation and consensus-seeking. What if you consulted with a small group of key people before deciding? You'd still be decisive, but people would feel heard."

**LEADER:** *[Considering]* "That's a good point. I hadn't thought of that approach."

**TAYLOR:** "There's also a pattern of sending important announcements late at night or on weekends. It creates an expectation that we're always on. Could we keep important announcements to work hours?"

**LEADER:** "I can see that. I'll be more mindful of timing going forward."

**TAYLOR:** "Thank you for hearing me. I know you care about the team, and I want us to work well together."

### What Happened Next

- Leader started consulting with 2-3 team members before decisions
- Team felt more heard and engaged
- Leader stopped sending late-night announcements
- Taylor's relationship with the leader strengthened
- Team culture improved

### Why It Worked

1. **Was direct but respectful** (didn't soften the message)
2. **Used "I" statements** (owned their experience)
3. **Focused on impact** (why it matters)
4. **Made a suggestion** (not just complaint)
5. **Ended positively** (reaffirmed relationship)
6. **Was prepared for defensiveness** (didn't react to it)

### What Could Be Better

- **Could have given this feedback earlier** (waited until frustration was high)
- **Could involve other team members** (if Taylor wasn't alone in experiencing this)
- **Should document the conversation** (for follow-up)

## Example 5: Peer-to-Peer Feedback

### Situation**

Two team members at the same level need to give each other feedback about collaboration.

### Context

**ALEX:** "Hey Jordan, do you have 15 minutes to sync on our collaboration?"

**JORDAN:** "Sure."

**ALEX:** "I want to talk about how we're working together on the client project. I value our partnership and want to make sure it's working well for both of us. Is that okay?"

**JORDAN:** "Of course."

**ALEX:** "When you commit to deadlines and then miss them without telling me (Experience), I find myself scrambling to cover your work and feeling stressed (Impact). This happened three times in the past month. I want to understand what's getting in the way (Dialogue)."

**JORDAN:** *[Defensive]* "I've been overwhelmed! You're not the only one with a lot on your plate."

**ALEX:** *[Acknowledging then redirecting]* "I know you're overwhelmed. That doesn't change the impact on me. When you don't communicate about delays, I can't plan my own work. What if you communicated earlier when you're running behind? That way I could adjust."

**JORDAN:** *[Calmer]* "That's fair. I've been avoiding admitting I'm behind because I don't want to let you down."

**ALEX:** "Letting me know is actually more helpful than trying to do it all and missing deadlines. What would help you manage your workload better?"

**JORDAN:** "Maybe we could review the workload together weekly and adjust deadlines before they become crises?"

**ALEX:** "That sounds great. Let's start that this week. And if you're running behind on anything, just tell me as soon as you know. I'll do the same for you."

**JORDAN:** "Deal. And thanks for bringing this up directly."

### What Happened Next

- Weekly workload reviews became a standing practice
- Both Alex and Jordan communicated proactively about delays
- Trust increased
- Project ran more smoothly
- They became a stronger collaborative team

### Why It Worked

1. **Set a positive frame** ("I value our partnership")
2. **Used CED framework** (Context, Experience, Dialogue)
3. **Acknowledged their challenge** while maintaining boundaries
4. **Focused on solutions** not blame
5. **Created mutual accountability** ("I'll do the same for you")
6. **Co-created the fix** (weekly reviews)

### What Could Be Better

- **Could have raised this earlier** (after first or second time, not third)
- **Should check in regularly** on how the collaboration is working
- **Could involve the leader** if workload issues are systemic

## Cross-Example Patterns

### What Consistently Works

1. **Preparation matters** - Don't wing it, especially for difficult feedback
2. **Ask for their perspective** - Make it a dialogue, not a monologue
3. **Focus on impact** - Why it matters, not just what happened
4. **Co-create solutions** - Don't impose fixes
5. **Follow through** - Check that changes actually happen

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Waiting too long** - Feedback is most effective when timely
2. **Being vague** - Specific examples are essential
3. **Focusing on character** - Behavior, not identity
4. **Ignoring emotions** - Acknowledge feelings, then move to solutions
5. **Not following up** - Feedback without follow-through is noise

### What Makes Feedback Stick

1. **Specificity** - Clear examples, not generalizations
2. **Dialogue** - Two-way conversation, not lecture
3. **Action** - Clear commitments, not vague intentions
4. **Support** - Help, not just expectations
5. **Accountability** - Check-ins, not hope

---

**Communication is relationship, not transmission.**

Every feedback conversation is an opportunity to strengthen or weaken the relationship. Approach feedback not as something you do TO people, but as something you do WITH people.

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

When you give feedback from a place of care and commitment to their growth, people can feel it. When you give feedback from a place of frustration or judgment, they can feel that too.

Start with the relationship. The feedback will follow.
