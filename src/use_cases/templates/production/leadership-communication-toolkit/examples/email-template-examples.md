# Email Template Examples

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool.**

These examples show how the written communication templates translate to real emails. Each includes context, the actual email, and results.

---

## Example 1: Delivering Difficult Feedback (Pre-Meeting Email)

### Context

Team member "Casey" has been consistently missing deadlines and not communicating about delays. Need to schedule a feedback conversation.

### The Email

**Subject:** Conversation about project deadlines

Hi Casey,

I want to schedule time to discuss your recent project work. This is an important conversation about your performance and our expectations, and I want to make sure we have dedicated time to talk through it properly.

Are you available on:
- Tuesday, 2/15 at 2:00pm
- Wednesday, 2/16 at 10:00am
- Thursday, 2/17 at 3:00pm

Please let me know which time works for you.

Thank you,

Alex

### What Worked

1. **Clear subject line** - Recipient knows what this is about
2. **Direct language** - Not vague, uses words "performance" and "expectations"
3. **Multiple time options** - Makes scheduling easy
4. **Professional tone** - Serious but not harsh

### What Could Be Better

- **Could be more specific** about the concerns (missed deadline for X project)
- **Should mention that support is available** (not just a problem conversation)
- **Consider a warm sentence** to humanize it ("I value your contributions and want to see you succeed")

## Example 2: Follow-Up After Difficult Conversation

### Context

After the feedback meeting with Casey (above), need to document the conversation and next steps.

### The Email

**Subject:** Recap of our conversation - 2/15

Hi Casey,

Thank you for the conversation earlier today. I want to recap what we discussed to make sure we're aligned.

**Key points we discussed:**
- You shared that personal challenges have been affecting your work
- I acknowledged the personal challenges while emphasizing the need for proactive communication
- We identified that three project deadlines were missed without prior communication
- You recognized the impact this has on the team and client relationships

**Next steps we agreed to:**
- You'll update project status every Friday by 5pm, even if there's no change
- You'll communicate any potential delays at least 24 hours before deadline
- I'll check in with you weekly on Fridays at 11am for the next month
- You'll connect with HR about available support resources
- We'll review progress together on 3/15

**My commitment to you:**
I want to see you succeed in this role. I'm committed to supporting you through this challenging period. If you're struggling, please reach out before it affects your work.

Let me know if I missed anything or if you have questions.

Best,

Alex

### What Worked

1. **Sent same day** - While conversation is fresh
2. **Captured both perspectives** - Not just leader's view
3. **Clear action items** - Specific commitments with dates
4. **Expressed support** - Not just demands
5. **Invited clarification** - "Let me know if I missed anything"

### What Could Be Better

- **Could ask Casey to confirm** their understanding (reply requested)
- **Should set calendar invites** for the check-ins (makes them real)
- **Could share resources** proactively (not just "connect with HR")

## Example 3: Recognizing Excellent Work

### Context

Team member "Morgan" led a successful client presentation that resulted in a major contract renewal.

### The Email

**Subject:** Appreciation: Client presentation success

Hi Morgan,

I want to recognize and appreciate your outstanding work on the client presentation this week.

**What you did:**
You spent extra time preparing the demo, anticipated the client's concerns before they raised them, and handled difficult questions with confidence and poise. When the technical demo failed, you didn't miss a beat and seamlessly pivoted to the backup plan.

**The impact:**
The client commented specifically on your preparation and professionalism. We secured the contract renewal ($180K/year) and the client mentioned your presentation as a key factor in their decision. Your teammates also mentioned how much they learned from watching you handle the pressure.

**Why it matters:**
This exemplifies our values of preparation, client focus, and grace under pressure. You set an example for the whole team.

Thank you for your dedication and excellence. Your contribution made a real difference for our team and our business.

With appreciation,

Alex

**CC:** Morgan's manager (for their awareness)

### What Worked

1. **Specific examples** - Exactly what Morgan did
2. **Concrete impact** - Real numbers and client feedback
3. **Connected to values** - Why it matters culturally
4. **Team recognition** - Acknowledged that others noticed too
5. **Genuine warmth** - Not robotic or formulaic

### What Could Be Better

- **Ask permission before CC-ing manager** (Morgan might prefer privacy)
- **Consider public recognition** (with Morgan's permission)
- **Link to Morgan's goals** (is she interested in more client-facing work?)

## Example 4: Team Announcement - Difficult News

### Context

Need to announce that a beloved team member is leaving due to relocation.

### The Email

**Subject:** Important update: Jordan's departure

Hi Team,

I have some bittersweet news to share. Jordan will be leaving our team at the end of this month to relocate to be closer to family.

**Jordan's contributions:**
Jordan has been an essential part of our team for the past three years. They've led key initiatives including [Project X], [Project Y], and our client onboarding process. Beyond their work, Jordan has been a mentor to newer team members and a voice for our team culture. They will be deeply missed.

**Timeline:**
- Jordan's last day will be Friday, 2/28
- This week and next: Jordan will document all work and hand off responsibilities
- Week of 2/24: Transition period with team members taking over Jordan's work
- Friday, 2/28: Team farewell lunch (details to follow)

**Transition plan:**
- Taylor will take over Jordan's client relationships
- Casey will pick up the onboarding process work
- Alex (me) will handle any gaps during transition

**What's next:**
We will be hiring for Jordan's position and will share the job posting with the team before it goes public. I value your input on potential candidates.

I know Jordan's departure is a loss for the team. Jordan, thank you for your contributions and for the way you've shown up for this team every day. You'll always be part of our team's story.

Please join me in wishing Jordan well.

Best,

Alex

### What Worked

1. **Direct and clear** - Not vague about the news
2. **Acknowledges the loss** - Validates feelings
3. **Concrete timeline** - Everyone knows what happens when
4. **Clear transition plan** - Reduces anxiety about who's doing what
5. **Honors the person** - Recognition of contributions
6. **Forward-looking** - Hiring plan addresses the gap

### What Could Be Better

- **Could include Jordan's contact info** (with permission)
- **Should address team questions** (how to ask them)
- **Consider a meeting** (not just email) for such significant news

## Example 5: Requesting Feedback (Leader Asking Team)

### Context

Leader wants honest feedback from the team about their leadership style.

### The Email

**Subject:** Feedback request: Your input on my leadership

Hi Team,

I'm writing to ask for your feedback on my leadership. I value your perspectives and want to ensure I'm showing up effectively for each of you and the team as a whole.

**Why I'm asking:**
I want to grow as a leader and serve this team well. The best way to do that is to hear directly from you about what's working and what could be better.

**What I'm asking:**
I'd appreciate your honest feedback on these questions:

1. What's one thing I do that helps you be successful?
2. What's one thing I do that gets in the way of your success?
3. What's one thing you wish I would start doing?
4. What's one thing you wish I would stop doing?
5. Any other feedback you want to share?

**How to share your feedback:**
- You can respond directly to this email (I'm the only one who will see it)
- Or, if you prefer anonymity, you can use this anonymous form: [Link]
- Or, we can schedule a 1:1 conversation if you'd prefer to talk in person

**Timeline:**
No rush—take some time to think about it. I'd appreciate your response by Friday, 3/1, but earlier or later is fine too.

**What I'll do with your feedback:**
I'll read everything carefully, look for patterns, and share themes with the team (not individual responses) along with changes I plan to make. Your feedback will directly shape how I lead this team going forward.

**One request:**
Please be honest. I can't grow if I don't know what's really working or not working. I promise to receive your feedback with gratitude and without defensiveness.

Thank you for your time and for being part of this team.

Best,

Alex

### What Worked

1. **Clear why** - Leader explained the motivation
2. **Specific questions** - Not just "any feedback"
3. **Multiple channels** - Email, form, or in person
4. **Anonymity option** - Creates safety
5. **Commitment to action** - Feedback won't disappear into void
6. **Request for honesty** - Explicit permission to be direct

### What Could Be Better

- **Could share past examples** of feedback received and acted on (builds trust)
- **Should acknowledge power dynamic** (hard to give feedback to your boss)
- **Consider a third party** (anonymous collection by someone else)

## Example 6: Project Delay Communication

### Context

Need to inform stakeholders that a major project will be delayed by 3 weeks.

### The Email

**Subject:** Project timeline adjustment: CRM implementation

Hi [Stakeholder Name],

I want to provide an update on the CRM implementation project and share that we're adjusting the timeline.

**Original timeline:** Go-live March 15
**New timeline:** Go-live April 5

**Reason for delay:**
During data migration testing, we discovered quality issues with the legacy data that weren't apparent in initial analysis. Specifically, we found duplicate records (15% of database) and incomplete client histories (8% of records). We need to clean this data before migration to avoid propagating these issues into the new system.

**Impact:**
- Sales team training will shift from March 25-26 to April 8-9
- Legacy system sunset will shift from April 1 to April 19
- Project budget remains unchanged (we're reallocating within existing budget)

**Mitigation:**
We're bringing in additional data specialists to accelerate the cleanup work and are extending work hours with overtime budget. We're also adding daily data quality checkpoints to catch issues earlier in the process.

**Risk assessment:**
The go-live date of April 5 remains realistic. We're building in a 1-week buffer before the legacy system sunset to allow for any final issues.

**Updated milestones:**
- Data cleanup complete: March 22
- Final migration: March 29
- User acceptance testing: April 1-3
- Go-live: April 5
- Legacy system sunset: April 19

I apologize for this delay and any disruption it causes. I believe it's the right call to ensure we launch with clean data rather than create larger problems downstream.

Please let me know if you have questions or concerns. I'm happy to discuss further on a call if that would be helpful.

Best,

Alex

### What Worked

1. **Subject line is clear** - Recipient knows immediately what this is about
2. **Up-front timeline change** - Doesn't bury the lead
3. **Specific reason** - Not vague, explains exactly what happened
4. **Addresses impact** - What this means for stakeholders
5. **Shares mitigation** - What's being done about it
6. **Provides updated milestones** - New dates for everything
7. **Apologizes without over-apologizing** - Takes responsibility, doesn't grovel

### What Could Be Better

- **Could have raised this earlier** - Don't wait until it's a crisis
- **Should offer a call** - Not just "happy to discuss," schedule something
- **Consider stakeholder questions** - Pre-empt likely concerns

## Example 7: Introducing a New Process

### Context

Introducing a new meeting format to replace daily standups.

### The Email

**Subject:** New process: Async standup - starts Monday, 3/4

Hi Team,

I'm writing to introduce a new process for our daily standup meetings.

**Why we're making this change:**
Based on your feedback and my observations, our current 30-minute daily standups are:
- Taking too much time (10+ hours/week in meetings)
- Covering information that could be shared async
- Creating meeting fatigue
- Reducing focus time for deep work

**What's changing:**
**Old way:** Daily 30-minute in-person standup at 9am
**New way:** Daily async standup in Slack by 10am

**How it works:**
Each day by 10am, share in #standup channel:

**Yesterday:**
- What you completed
- What you learned

**Today:**
- What you're working on
- What you need help with

**Blockers:**
- What's blocking you
- Who can help unblock you

**Try it for 2 weeks:**
We'll test this from 3/4-3/15. On 3/15, we'll vote as a team on whether to continue with async, return to in-person, or try a hybrid approach.

**What I'm excited about:**
- More focus time for everyone
- Better documentation of daily work
- Flexibility for different working schedules
- Still get the benefit of team coordination

**What I'm nervous about:**
- Losing the face-to-face connection
- Missing serendipitous conversations
- Some people might struggle with written communication

**What we'll keep:**
- Weekly in-person team meeting (Fridays 10-11am)
- Biweekly 1:1s
- Ad-hoc conversations whenever helpful

**Questions or concerns?**
Please share them! I'm happy to discuss this more in our team meeting tomorrow, or you can Slack me directly.

I know learning new processes takes effort. I appreciate your adaptability as we try this experiment.

Best,

Alex

### What Worked

1. **Clear before/after** - Exactly what's changing
2. **Explained the why** - Not just change for change's sake
3. **Limited timeframe** - Try it for 2 weeks, not permanent commitment
4. **Honest about concerns** - Leader isn't blindly positive
5. **Kept what works** - Not eliminating all meetings
6. **Invited feedback** - Open to concerns

### What Could Be Better

- **Could have asked the team** to help design the new format (co-creation)
- **Should provide examples** of what a good async standup looks like
- **Consider a template** to make it easier for people to participate

## Cross-Example Patterns

### What Makes Emails Effective

1. **Clear subject lines** - Recipient knows what the email is about
2. **Bottom line up front** - Most important info first
3. **Specific details** - Not vague, concrete examples and dates
4. **Human tone** - Professional but not robotic
5. **Action items clear** - Who does what by when
6. **Appropriate length** - As long as necessary, as short as possible

### Common Email Mistakes

1. **Vague subject lines** - "Update" or "Question"
2. **Burying the lead** - Important info at the end
3. **Too long** - Over 300 words usually needs a meeting
4. **Too brief** - Lacks context, creates questions
5. **Emotional overload** - Too many exclamation points, all caps, or overly emotional
6. **One email, many topics** - Should be separate emails

### Tone by Email Type

| Email Type | Tone |
|------------|-------|
| Difficult feedback | Serious, direct, not harsh |
| Recognition | Warm, specific, genuine |
| Difficult news | Clear, empathetic, not dramatic |
| Feedback request | Humble, open, not defensive |
| Project delay | Apologetic but confident, not panicked |
| Process change | Excited but honest about concerns |
