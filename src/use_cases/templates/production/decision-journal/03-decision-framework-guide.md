# Decision Framework Guide

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool**

---

## Overview

This guide provides practical decision frameworks you can apply to any leadership decision. No single framework works for every situation - the key is having multiple tools in your toolkit and knowing when to use each one.

**Quick rule of thumb:** Use the simplest framework that gives you enough clarity to move forward with confidence.

---

## Framework Selection Guide

### Decision Complexity Assessment

**Simple Decisions (Low stakes, reversible):**
- Examples: What to prioritize today, which meeting to attend
- Framework: Quick pros/cons list, trust your gut
- Time investment: 2-5 minutes

**Moderate Decisions (Medium stakes, some reversibility):**
- Examples: Hiring decisions, budget allocations, project prioritization
- Framework: Expected value + 10-10-10 rule
- Time investment: 15-30 minutes

**Complex Decisions (High stakes, hard to reverse):**
- Examples: Strategy pivots, major hires, product launches
- Framework: Multiple frameworks + outside perspective
- Time investment: 1-2 hours, potentially spread over days

## Framework 1: Pros/Cons List

### When to Use
- Simple decisions with clear tradeoffs
- When you need to quickly compare 2-3 options
- When you're feeling stuck and need clarity

### How to Apply

1. **List all pros** for each option (aim for 5+ per option)
2. **List all cons** for each option (aim for 5+ per option)
3. **Weight each item** as High (3), Medium (2), or Low (1) impact
4. **Calculate scores** - sum weighted pros minus cons
5. **Compare totals** - but also look at pattern, not just score

### Example

**Decision:** Should we implement a 4-day work week?

**Option 1: Implement 4-day week**

**Pros:**
- Better work-life balance for team (3)
- Attract talent in competitive market (3)
- Forced prioritization and efficiency (2)
- Reduced burnout risk (3)
- Progressive employer brand (2)
*Total: 13*

**Cons:**
- Client coverage challenges (3)
- Potential productivity dip during transition (2)
- Coordination complexity with 5-day partners (2)
- Some roles harder to adapt (2)
- Risk of same work in less time (2)
*Total: 11*

**Net Score:** +2

**Qualitative check:** The high-weight items (client coverage, talent attraction) suggest the decision hinges on whether we can solve the client coverage challenge. If we can, it's a go. If not, it's a no.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Incomplete lists:** Forcing equal pros/cons when one side is clearly stronger
- **Ignoring weights:** Treating small and large impacts equally
- **Analysis paralysis:** Creating 50-item lists when 10 would suffice

## Framework 2: Expected Value Calculation

### When to Use
- Decisions with uncertain outcomes
- When you need to quantify risk vs. reward
- When comparing options with different risk profiles

### How to Apply

1. **Identify possible outcomes** for each option (usually 3-5)
2. **Estimate probability** of each outcome (must sum to 100%)
3. **Assign value** to each outcome (positive or negative, on consistent scale)
4. **Calculate expected value:** Probability × Value for each outcome
5. **Compare totals** across options

### Example

**Decision:** Should we launch the new feature now or wait?

**Option 1: Launch Now**

| Outcome | Probability | Value | Expected Value |
|---------|-------------|-------|----------------|
| Immediate success, capture market | 30% | +100 | +30 |
| Minor bugs, quick fixes, good launch | 40% | +50 | +20 |
| Significant issues, delayed recovery | 20% | -50 | -10 |
| Launch failure, recall needed | 10% | -100 | -10 |
| **Total Expected Value** | | | **+30** |

**Option 2: Wait 2 Months**

| Outcome | Probability | Value | Expected Value |
|---------|-------------|-------|----------------|
| Competitor launches first, we lose advantage | 50% | -50 | -25 |
| We launch with better quality, capture market | 30% | +80 | +24 |
| Both launch simultaneously, split market | 20% | +20 | +4 |
| **Total Expected Value** | | | **+3** |

**Conclusion:** Launch now (+30) has higher expected value than waiting (+3), assuming probabilities are reasonable.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Overconfidence bias:** Overestimating probabilities of favorable outcomes
- **Neglecting base rates:** Ignoring historical data about similar situations
- **Failing to update:** Not revisiting estimates when new information emerges

## Framework 3: Worst-Case Scenario Test

### When to Use
- High-stakes decisions where failure has significant consequences
- When you're risk-averse or feeling anxious about a decision
- When evaluating whether you can handle the downside

### How to Apply

1. **Imagine the worst possible outcome** for each option
2. **Assess likelihood** - not just "could it happen" but "how likely is it"
3. **Evaluate recoverability** - if this happens, can you recover?
4. **Test your tolerance** - could you live with this outcome?
5. **Plan mitigation** - what would reduce likelihood or impact?

### Example

**Decision:** Should I leave my stable job to start my own business?

**Option 1: Start business**

**Worst-case scenario:**
- Business fails within 18 months
- Lose $50,000 in savings
- Out of work for 6 months
- Feel like a failure

**Likelihood:** 40% (based on small business statistics)

**Recoverability:**
- Yes, can return to corporate role
- Savings would cover 12 months, not just 6
- Skills and experience would be attractive to employers

**Tolerance check:**
- Can I live with this? Yes. The experience and learning would be valuable regardless of outcome.
- What's the real cost? 18 months and $50,000
- What's the potential upside? Uncapped, both financially and in terms of fulfillment

**Option 2: Stay in current job**

**Worst-case scenario:**
- Continue feeling unfulfilled
- Regret not trying
- Stagnant growth
- Company layoffs in 2 years

**Likelihood:** 70% (already feeling unfulfilled)

**Recoverability:**
- Harder to recover lost time and regret
- Could still start business later, but with more life constraints

**Tolerance check:**
- Can I live with this? This is harder - it's a slow fade vs. acute failure

**Conclusion:** Starting business has clearer worst case (with known recovery path) vs. staying job has emotional worst case that's harder to quantify but highly likely.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Catastrophizing:** Imagining worst cases that are extremely unlikely
- **Ignoring probability:** Treating 1% risks as if they're 50% risks
- **Survivorship bias:** Only imagining the upside of the path not taken

## Framework 4: 10-10-10 Rule

### When to Use
- When you're caught in short-term thinking
- When you're struggling with values alignment
- When you need perspective on immediate pressure

### How to Apply

Ask yourself: How will I feel about this decision in:

**10 minutes:**
- What's my immediate reaction?
- What emotions am I experiencing right now?
- What's the short-term impact?

**10 months:**
- How will this look in the medium term?
- What will the consequences be?
- What opportunities will be created or lost?

**10 years:**
- Will this matter in a decade?
- How will this fit into my life story?
- What kind of person do I want to be, and does this align?

### Example

**Decision:** Should I confront my direct report about their pattern of lateness?

**10 minutes:**
- I feel nervous and uncomfortable
- Immediate impact: awkward conversation
- Short-term: tension in relationship

**10 months:**
- If I don't address it: team morale suffers, others become resentful, pattern worsens
- If I do address it: either they improve (team benefit) or we part ways (team benefit long-term)
- Consequence: short-term discomfort for long-term team health

**10 years:**
- I won't remember this specific conversation
- I will remember whether I was the kind of leader who held standards or avoided hard things
- This decision is about who I am as a leader, not just one employee's lateness

**Conclusion:** The 10-minute and 10-month perspectives push toward avoidance, but the 10-year perspective makes it clear - this is about leadership identity, not just comfort.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Over-weighting the present:** 10 minutes feels more real than 10 years
- **Underestimating compounding:** Small decisions compound over 10 months
- **Neglecting identity:** Focusing on outcomes rather than who you want to become

## Framework 5: Opportunity Cost Assessment

### When to Use
- When you have limited resources (time, money, attention)
- When you're choosing between multiple good options
- When you need to be explicit about what you're NOT doing

### How to Apply

1. **Identify all options** including "do nothing" or "delay"
2. **List what you're giving up** for each option (the opportunity cost)
3. **Estimate value** of what you're giving up
4. **Compare benefits chosen vs. benefits forgone**
5. **Check alignment** with priorities and values

### Example

**Decision:** Which of three product features should we build this quarter?

**Option 1: Build Feature A (AI integration)**
- Benefits: High customer demand, marketable, modern
- Opportunity cost: Not building B or C
- Value of B (forgone): Moderate customer demand, moderate value
- Value of C (forgone): Low customer demand, but high strategic importance
- Assessment: A gives us short-term wins, but we're sacrificing strategic positioning

**Option 2: Build Feature B (Dashboard improvements)**
- Benefits: Moderate customer demand, high usage increase
- Opportunity cost: Not building A or C
- Value of A (forgone): High demand, marketable
- Value of C (forgone): Low demand but strategic
- Assessment: Safe choice, but misses both short-term marketing (A) and long-term strategy (C)

**Option 3: Build Feature C (Infrastructure upgrade)**
- Benefits: Strategic foundation, enables future features
- Opportunity cost: Not building A or B
- Value of A (forgone): High demand, marketable
- Value of B (forgone): Moderate demand, usage increase
- Assessment: Strongest long-term play, but visible short-term sacrifice

**Conclusion:** If we have to choose one, we're sacrificing something significant. The real question: What are we optimizing for? Short-term revenue (A) or long-term capability (C)?

**Alternative insight:** Can we build a scaled version of A while laying groundwork for C?

### Common Pitfalls
- **Ignoring "do nothing":** Forgetting that not deciding is also a decision
- **Sunk cost fallacy:** Continuing investment because of past spending
- **Resource blindness:** Forgetting that time and attention are also limited resources

## Framework 6: Values Alignment Check

### When to Use
- When you're torn between head and heart
- When you need to make decisions you'll feel proud of
- When the "right" choice isn't the "easy" choice

### How to Apply

1. **Clarify your core values** (write them down if you haven't)
2. **Test each option** against each value
3. **Ask:** Would I be proud to explain this decision publicly?
4. **Check your body:** What does each option feel like physically?
5. **Consider your legacy:** What kind of person do I want to be?

### Example

**Core Values:**
1. Growth
2. Integrity
3. Courage
4. Compassion
5. Excellence

**Decision:** Should I admit to my team that I made a mistake in the budget planning?

**Option 1: Admit the mistake**
- Growth: ✓ Shows learning, models vulnerability
- Integrity: ✓ Honest, transparent
- Courage: ✓ Hard conversation, shows strength
- Compassion: ✓ Builds trust through vulnerability
- Excellence: ✓ Addresses issue, leads to improvement
- Assessment: Strong alignment across all values

**Option 2: Fix it silently and hope no one notices**
- Growth: ✗ Misses learning opportunity
- Integrity: ✗ Withholds information
- Courage: ✗ Avoids difficult conversation
- Compassion: ✗ Denies team transparency
- Excellence: ✓ Fixes the problem
- Assessment: Weak alignment, only hits "excellence" superficially

**Body check:**
- Option 1: Nervous but relieved when imagining it
- Option 2: Tight chest, anxious when imagining it

**Legacy check:** In 10 years, do I want to be known as the leader who owned mistakes or hid them?

**Conclusion:** Values alignment strongly points toward admitting the mistake, even though it's uncomfortable.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Values ambiguity:** Having vague values that can justify anything
- **Values collision:** When values conflict without clear hierarchy
- **Rationalization:** Conforming values to preference rather than preference to values

## Combining Frameworks

### When to Use Multiple Frameworks
- High-stakes, hard-to-reverse decisions
- When you're feeling uncertain about your conclusion
- When you need to build confidence in your choice

### Recommended Combinations

**For strategic decisions:**
1. Pros/Cons (for structure)
2. Expected Value (for quantification)
3. Values Alignment (for wisdom)

**For ethical decisions:**
1. Values Alignment (for clarity)
2. 10-10-10 Rule (for perspective)
3. Worst-Case Scenario (for risk assessment)

**For personnel decisions:**
1. Pros/Cons (for comparison)
2. Worst-Case Scenario (for risk)
3. Opportunity Cost (for resource reality)

**For financial decisions:**
1. Expected Value (for quantification)
2. Opportunity Cost (for tradeoffs)
3. 10-10-10 Rule (for perspective)

## Framework Quick Reference

| Framework | Best For | Time Required | Key Question |
|-----------|----------|---------------|--------------|
| Pros/Cons | Clear tradeoffs, 2-3 options | 5-10 min | What are the权衡? |
| Expected Value | Uncertain outcomes, risk/reward | 15-20 min | What's the math? |
| Worst-Case | High-stakes, risk-averse | 10-15 min | Can I handle the downside? |
| 10-10-10 | Short-term pressure, values | 5-10 min | What will I think later? |
| Opportunity Cost | Resource constraints, tradeoffs | 10-15 min | What am I not doing? |
| Values Alignment | Head vs. heart, ethical | 10-15 min | Who do I want to be? |

## Sample Decision: Using All Frameworks

**Decision:** Should we accept a large client that doesn't align with our values but would provide significant revenue?

**Pros/Cons:**
+ Financial stability
+ Growth opportunity
+ Team development
- Values misalignment
- Potential team burnout
- Reputation risk
- May attract more misaligned clients

**Expected Value:**
- 40% chance: Client relationship works, we make good money but feel conflicted
- 30% chance: Client relationship deteriorates, we exit but keep some money
- 20% chance: Client relationship is disaster, we lose money and reputation
- 10% chance: Client relationship transforms positively, we learn and grow

**Worst-Case Scenario:**
- Worst case: We lose $50K, reputation takes hit, team morale plummets, we exit client after 6 months
- Likelihood: 20%
- Recoverability: Yes, but painful
- Tolerance: Could we live with this? Yes, but would regret not listening to instincts

**10-10-10 Rule:**
- 10 minutes: Excited about revenue, nervous about fit
- 10 months: Team is burned out, regretting the decision, but cash is helpful
- 10 years: Won't remember this specific client, but will remember whether we stayed true to our values

**Opportunity Cost:**
- What we're giving up: Time to find aligned clients, team energy for better work, reputation positioning
- Could we use this time to build marketing systems that attract aligned clients?

**Values Alignment:**
- Values: Excellence, Integrity, Compassion, Growth
- Client alignment: Low on Integrity and Compassion (how they treat their team)
- Assessment: Accepting this client would violate core values

**Conclusion:** While the financial Pros and Expected Value suggest consideration, the Values Alignment, 10-10-10 Rule, and Opportunity Cost all point to declining. The revenue isn't worth the values violation and opportunity cost.

**Final decision:** Decline the client, invest in finding aligned clients.

---

## Notes
- No framework replaces judgment - they illuminate, they don't decide
- Different frameworks may point to different conclusions - that's information, not confusion
- The best framework is the one that helps you move forward with confidence
- Your intuition and values should always have the final say

**Remember: Good decisions come from good processes, not perfect frameworks.**

**Your presence is your primary leadership tool**
