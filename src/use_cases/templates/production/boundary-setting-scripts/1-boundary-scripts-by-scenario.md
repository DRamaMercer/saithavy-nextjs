# Boundary Scripts by Scenario

*Real scripts you can use right now. I'm still learning this stuff too - these have come from actual conversations, some that went well and some I'm still working on.*

---

## Workplace Boundaries

### Scenario 1: Saying No to Additional Work

"It means a lot that you thought of me for this. I'm at capacity with my current commitments and can't take this on without dropping the ball on [project X]. I want to be realistic about what I can deliver well."

**Why this works:** You're acknowledging the opportunity while being honest about your capacity. You're protecting your existing work quality.

**Variation:** "I appreciate you thinking of me. My plate is full right now and I don't have the bandwidth to do this well. Let's reconnect in [timeframe] if you still need support."

---

### Scenario 2: Pushing Back on Unrealistic Deadlines

"I want to make sure we deliver quality work on this. Given the scope, I'd need [more realistic timeline] to do this right. If we need it faster, let's discuss what we can de-prioritize or simplify."

**Why this works:** You're positioning it as commitment to quality, not resistance. You're offering a path forward.

**Variation:** "I want to set us up for success here. To do this thoroughly, I need [timeline]. If we're locked into [current deadline], let's talk about what a realistic scope looks like for that timeframe."

---

### Scenario 3: Protecting Focus Time

"I'm blocking focus time on [day/time] to make progress on [key project]. I'll be unavailable for meetings then, but I'll respond to messages after [time]."

**Why this works:** You're clear about when you're unavailable AND when you'll be back. This builds trust.

**Variation:** "I've found I do my best work with uninterrupted focus time. I'm blocking [mornings/afternoons] for deep work. I'll be available for calls and messages after [time]."

---

### Scenario 4: Leaving Work on Time

"I make it a practice to wrap up at [time] to maintain my energy and effectiveness. I can pick this up first thing tomorrow morning."

**Why this works:** You're framing it as sustainability, not checking out. You're offering next steps.

**Variation:** "I'm heading out at [time] today. What's the most important thing to know before tomorrow? Otherwise, I'll catch up on this first thing in the morning."

---

### Scenario 5: Weekend Boundaries

"I don't check work messages on weekends so I can recharge and be fully present when I'm back on Monday. If anything urgent comes up, please call me at [number]. Otherwise, I'll respond Monday."

**Why this works:** You're setting expectations while providing an emergency channel. You're explaining the why.

**Variation:** "I keep weekends work-free to maintain my energy for the week ahead. I won't be checking messages, but I'll get back to anything that comes in on Monday morning."

## Relationship Boundaries

### Scenario 6: Setting Emotional Boundaries

"I care about you and I want to support you. I'm also feeling [overwhelmed/drained/at capacity] right now. Can we find a different time to talk about this, or can you also talk to [other support person]?"

**Why this works:** You're affirming care while being honest about limits. You're offering alternatives.

**Variation:** "I want to be here for you, and I'm running on empty right now. Can we have this conversation [later date] when I can be more present?"

---

### Scenario 7: Family Expectations

"I love you and I want to show up for our family. I also need [time/space/boundaries] to take care of myself. Let's find a balance that works for both of us."

**Why this works:** You're reaffirming connection while advocating for needs. You're inviting collaboration.

**Variation:** "Our family is important to me, and I'm struggling to balance [expectation] with my own wellbeing. Can we talk about what this looks like going forward?"

---

### Scenario 8: Saying No to Social Plans

"Thank you for thinking of me! I'm prioritizing rest right now and won't be able to make it. Hope you have a wonderful time."

**Why this works:** No over-explaining. You're clear about your need and wishing them well.

**Variation:** "I really appreciate the invite. I'm feeling pretty drained and need some downtime this weekend, so I'll pass. Thanks for understanding!"

---

## Digital Boundaries

### Scenario 9: Phone/Notification Boundaries

"I'm trying to be more present and less reactive to my phone. I won't be checking notifications [timeframe]. If it's urgent, please call me."

**Why this works:** You're framing it as intentionality, not avoidance. You're providing an alternative for urgent needs.

**Variation:** "I'm setting some boundaries around my phone use to be more present. I might be slower to respond to texts, but I'll get back to you within [timeframe]."

---

### Scenario 10: Email Boundaries

"I check email [specific times] to stay focused on deep work the rest of the day. I'll respond to non-urgent messages within [timeframe]."

**Why this works:** Clear expectations about when you'll respond. You're protecting focus time.

**Variation:** "I'm batching my email to protect my focus time. I check messages at [times] and will respond within [timeframe]. If it's urgent, a call or text works better."

---

## Self-Boundaries

### Scenario 11: Perfectionism

"Done is better than perfect. This is good enough for now. I can always iterate later."

**Why this works:** You're reframing completion vs. perfection. You're giving yourself permission to ship.

**Variation:** "This doesn't need to be perfect - it needs to be done. Good enough is actually the goal here. I'll learn from feedback and improve."

---

### Scenario 12: Overcommitment

"I'm excited about all these opportunities. I need to be realistic about my capacity. I'll choose [top priority] and politely decline the rest for now."

**Why this works:** You're acknowledging the excitement while being realistic. You're making an intentional choice.

**Variation:** "Everything here looks great, but I can't do it all well. I'm going to focus on [priority] and say no to the rest so I can show up fully."

---

### Scenario 13: People-Pleasing

"I want to help, and I also need to honor my own needs. Let me think about this and get back to you."

**Why this works:** You're buying time to check in with yourself. You're not committing out of guilt.

**Variation:** "I appreciate you asking, but I need to check my capacity first. Let me get back to you by [time]."

---

## Quick Tips for Using These Scripts

1. **Make them yours**: Adjust the wording to sound like you. Authenticity matters more than perfect scripts.

2. **Start small**: Practice with low-stakes situations before the big conversations.

3. **Expect awkwardness**: First few times will feel weird. That's normal. It gets easier.

4. **Some will land well, some won't**: That's about them, not you. You're not responsible for others' reactions.

5. **Celebrate the attempt**: Even if it comes out messy, you're still building the muscle.

---

**Remember:** You're allowed to have boundaries. That's not selfish, that's sustainable. And your needs matter too.

*These scripts are tools, not rules. Take what works, leave what doesn't, and keep practicing.*
