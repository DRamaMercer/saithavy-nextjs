## [0:15-4:00] Section 1: Shock and Dissociation in Crisis

**[Music fades to very subtle background]**

Crisis hits different than everyday stress. It's not just overwhelming—it's shattering. When crisis strikes—a death, a diagnosis, an accident, a sudden loss, violence, trauma—your world can crack open in an instant. Everything you thought was solid suddenly isn't. The future you were planning for vanishes. The ground beneath you disappears.

And your brain responds by... leaving.

This is called dissociation, and it's one of the most disorienting experiences you can have. You feel unreal, like you're watching your life from the outside. Everything looks strange, dreamlike, through thick glass. Sounds are muffled. Time warps—minutes stretch into hours, days blur together. You might feel numb, cut off from your emotions. Or you might feel everything at once, waves of panic and grief crashing over you until you shut down completely.

You might dissociate in other ways too. Losing time—hours or days pass in a blur. Finding yourself somewhere and not knowing how you got there. Feeling like parts of you are split off—there's the you that's going through the motions, functioning, talking to people, making decisions. And there's the you that's somewhere else, frozen, shattered, unreachable.

I know this feeling. I've lived it.

A few years ago, I experienced a crisis that shattered my world. In a single moment, everything changed. Someone I loved was gone. Just gone. One minute they were there, and the next they weren't. No warning. No preparation. No goodbye.

For the first week, I functioned on autopilot. I made arrangements. I answered questions. I talked to people. I ate because people made me. I slept because my body eventually shut down. But I wasn't really there. I was floating somewhere above my life, watching this person who looked like me doing things that looked like living but felt nothing like living.

Everything felt unreal. I'd wake up and forget for a second that anything had happened. Then reality would crash back in and it would feel like hearing it for the first time, over and over again. I'd find myself standing in rooms with no idea why I was there. I'd have conversations and later not remember a word. I felt disconnected from my body, from time, from reality itself.

This is shock. This is trauma. This is your brain trying to protect you from pain that's too big to process all at once. And it's terrifying.

Here's what I wish someone had told me then: **This is not going crazy. This is not permanent. This is your nervous system's response to overwhelm.** And grounding is how you stay connected to reality even when reality has become unbearable.

Grounding in crisis is different from grounding for everyday anxiety. It's not about calming down—it's about staying present. It's not about feeling better—it's about not disappearing completely. It's about keeping one thread of connection to reality so you can find your way back, piece by piece.

During that crisis time, grounding was my lifeline. When I felt myself dissociating—floating away, going numb, checking out—I'd use emergency grounding to come back. Just enough to function. Just enough to get through the next hour. Just enough to stay tethered to reality even when reality felt unbearable.

I didn't feel better. I was still shattered. I was still devastated. But I was here. I was present. I could make decisions. I could take care of myself. I could survive.

## [4:00-7:00] Section 2: Emergency Grounding Protocol

When you're in crisis, you don't have time for long, complicated techniques. You need something quick, powerful, effective. You need an emergency protocol.

Here's the emergency grounding protocol I used through the hardest days of my life. It's simple, it's fast, and it works.

**STEP 1: FEET ON THE FLOOR**

This is always step one. Always. Feel your feet. Notice where they make contact with the ground. Press down. Feel the floor pressing back up. *I am here. My feet are on the ground. The ground is holding me up.*

If you can, stand up. The act of standing engages your core muscles, which sends a signal of strength and stability to your nervous system. But even sitting or lying down, you can feel your feet. This physical sensation of contact with the ground is your anchor. It's proof that you exist, you're here, you're real.

**STEP 2: COLD WATER**

Go to the sink and splash cold water on your face. Or hold an ice cube in your hand until it melts. Cold temperature shocks your nervous system into the present moment. It's impossible to dissociate when freezing water is hitting your face. Your body responds instantly—your heart rate slows, your breathing regulates, you come back.

I can't tell you how many times I stood over my kitchen sink, splashing cold water on my face, trying to come back to myself. It works. Every time.

**STEP 3: NAME 5 THINGS QUICKLY**

Look around and rapidly name five things you can see. Don't worry about details. Just name them. *Wall. Chair. Window. Table. Door.* Do it fast. The speed keeps you from spiraling back into your thoughts. *Lamp. Floor. Ceiling. Phone. Plant.* Keep going until you feel more present.

**STEP 4: STRONG SENSATION**

Your nervous system is overwhelmed by emotional pain. Counteract it with strong physical sensation. Squeeze your arms tight against your chest. Dig your heels into the floor. Clench your fists and release. Bite into a lemon. Sniff a strong smell like peppermint or coffee.

I used to keep a small bottle of peppermint essential oil with me. When I felt myself dissociating, I'd take a deep breath of that sharp, strong scent. It would jerk me back into reality instantly. *I'm here. I smell peppermint. I'm real.*

**STEP 5: TAP YOUR BODY**

Tap your legs firmly with your hands. Tap your chest. Tap your face. The sensation of touch—especially firm, rhythmic touch—helps you reconnect with your body. You're telling your nervous system: *This is my body. I'm in it. I'm here.*

You can do all five steps in under two minutes. That's it. That's the emergency protocol. It's not about feeling calm—let go of that expectation right now. In crisis, calm is not realistic. The goal is presence. The goal is staying connected to reality enough to function, to make decisions, to take care of yourself, to get through.

I used this protocol countless times through my crisis. Waking up in the middle of the night, heart racing, dissociating, feeling like I was disappearing. I'd get up, feel my feet on the floor, splash cold water on my face, name five things, smell peppermint, tap my chest. *I'm here. I'm real. I can get through this night.*

Sitting in meetings about arrangements, feeling everything go blurry and unreal. I'd press my feet into the floor, clench my fists, take a deep breath, name five things around the room. *I'm here. I can do this. I can make this decision.*

Standing in my kitchen, overwhelmed by grief, feeling myself spiraling. I'd turn on the cold water, splash my face, feel the shock, come back. *I'm here. I'm still here. I'm still breathing.*

This protocol kept me anchored. It didn't fix anything. It didn't make the pain go away. But it kept me present enough to survive.

## [7:00-9:15] Section 3: Self-Regulation When Overwhelmed

In crisis, you're going to get overwhelmed. That's not a failure—it's reality. Crisis is overwhelming by definition. The key is having tools to handle those overwhelm moments without spiraling further.

**RECOGNIZE THE SIGNS**

First, learn your personal overwhelm signals. What happens when you're reaching your limit? For me: my vision gets blurry or tunnel-like. My thoughts start racing so fast I can't catch them. My chest gets tight. My hands shake. I feel hot or cold suddenly. I start shutting down—going numb, losing words, feeling like I'm disappearing.

Your signals might be different. Know them. Recognize them. When you notice them, that's your cue to use your emergency protocol.

**DROP EVERYTHING FOR 60 SECONDS**

When you hit overwhelm, stop. Just stop. Whatever you're doing, whatever crisis is demanding your attention—it can wait 60 seconds. Drop everything and use your emergency protocol. Feet on floor. Cold water. Name 5 things. Strong sensation. Tap your body.

In crisis, you might feel like you can't stop. Like there's too much to do, too many decisions, too many demands. **You can always spare 60 seconds.** Those 60 seconds will actually help you function better. You'll make better decisions, communicate more clearly, handle more effectively when you're somewhat grounded than when you're completely spun out.

**USE YOUR PEOPLE**

Grounding doesn't have to be solitary. Sometimes the most grounding thing is another human voice. When you're overwhelmed, call someone. Text someone. Say: *I'm not okay right now. Can you just talk to me? Can you tell me I'm here? Can you help me ground?*

I had a few people I could call at any hour during my crisis. I didn't need them to fix anything. I just needed their voice to help tether me to reality. They'd say my name. They'd tell me I was there, I was safe, I would get through this. They'd help me count breaths, name things I could see, feel my feet. Their presence was grounding.

**ACCEPT WHERE YOU ARE**

In crisis, there's this pressure to be okay. To handle things. To be strong. But here's the truth: **You don't have to be okay.** You just have to be present. You can be shattered and present. You can be devastated and present. You can be absolutely not okay and still be here, grounded in reality, getting through.

Drop the expectation that you should feel better. Crisis is awful. Of course you feel awful. Grounding isn't about fixing that—it's about not disappearing. It's about staying connected to reality even when reality is unbearable. That's enough.

## [9:15-11:15] Section 4: Practice - Crisis Grounding Simulation

**[Music becomes very minimal - almost no melody, just slow, deep tones]**

Now I want to guide you through a crisis grounding simulation. We're going to practice the emergency protocol so it becomes automatic—like muscle memory. When you're actually in crisis, you won't have the capacity to think clearly. You need this protocol to be something your body knows by heart.

If you're driving or operating machinery, please skip this until later. Otherwise, let's begin.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

I want you to imagine a crisis situation. Not your worst fear—just something overwhelming. An accident. A sudden loss. Bad news. Imagine that moment when everything changes in an instant. Let yourself feel a bit of that overwhelm.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Notice what happens in your body. Does your heart race? Does your breath catch? Do you feel tense, shaky, hot, cold? Don't judge it—just notice. This is what crisis feels like. It's overwhelming, but you're safe right now. You're just practicing.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Now, let's do the emergency protocol. Quickly.

**[Pause 3 seconds]**

Step 1: FEET. Feel your feet. Press them into the floor. Notice the contact. *I am here.*

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

Step 2: COLD. Imagine splashing cold water on your face. Feel the shock. Or if you have actual water nearby, go do it. It's okay to pause this.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

Step 3: NAME 5 THINGS. Quickly. Look around. *Wall. Chair. Window. Table. Door.* Do it again if you need to. *Floor. Lamp. Phone. Cup. Hand.* Fast. Keep your eyes on the physical world.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Step 4: STRONG SENSATION. Squeeze your arms tight against your chest. Hard. Feel the pressure. Or dig your heels into the floor. Or clench your fists. Something strong.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Step 5: TAP. Tap your legs. Tap your chest. Firm. Rhythmic. *This is my body. I'm in it.*

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Take a deep breath. Notice how you feel. You might still feel stressed, but are you more present? Are you more in your body? Did the dissociation ease even a little?

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

This works. Even in actual crisis, this works. Practice it. Make it automatic. So when crisis hits, your body knows what to do.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

Take one more deep breath... and when you're ready, open your eyes.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

You handled that simulation. You can handle real crisis too.

## [11:15-end] Encouragement + What's Next

**[Music becomes supportive but not uplifting - serious acknowledgment of difficulty]**

I know this is heavy stuff. Crisis is hard to talk about, hard to think about, hard to prepare for. But here's what I need you to hear: **You are stronger than you know.** You've survived every single difficult day of your life so far. You've survived 100% of your crises. You will survive this too.

Grounding won't fix crisis. It won't make the pain go away. It won't solve the situation. But it will keep you present. It will keep you connected. It will help you make it through, one moment at a time, until you reach the other side.

And there is another side. Crisis doesn't last forever, even though it feels like it will. You will process. You will heal. You will integrate. Grounding is part of that process—staying present enough to do the hard work of healing.

In Part 4, we're going to talk about building these practices into daily life. How to ground not just in crisis, but every day—preventatively, proactively, as a way of living. Because the more you practice grounding when things are calm, the more automatic it becomes when things aren't.

Until then, remember: **If you're overwhelmed, that's not a character flaw. It's a signal.** And in crisis, that signal is loud. Listen to it. Ground. Stay present. You've got this.
