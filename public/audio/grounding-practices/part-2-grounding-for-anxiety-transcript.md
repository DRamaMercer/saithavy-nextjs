## [0:15-3:30] Section 1: How Anxiety Disconnects Us

**[Music fades to background]**

Anxiety has a way of hijacking our brains and stealing us right out of the present moment. It's like a thief that breaks in and kidnaps your attention, dragging you into a future that hasn't happened yet—and might not ever happen.

When anxiety spikes, our nervous system kicks into fight-or-flight mode. This is brilliant if you're facing an actual threat—like a predator chasing you or a car speeding toward you. Your body mobilizes instantly to save you. Heart rate up. Senses sharpen. Blood flow to major muscle groups. You're ready to fight or run.

But most anxiety today isn't about immediate physical danger. It's about *might* happen. What if I lose my job? What if my child gets sick? What if I make a mistake? What if people judge me? What if I can't handle what's coming?

Your brain can't tell the difference between a real threat and an imagined one. So it responds the same way—by launching you into full-on survival mode. Even though you're sitting safely in your chair, not being chased by anything.

Here's what that feels like: Your heart races. Your breath gets shallow. Your muscles tense up. Your thoughts spin faster and faster, cycling through worst-case scenarios. You feel keyed up, on edge, unable to settle. You might feel shaky or nauseous. You might feel detached from reality, like things aren't quite real.

And here's the cruel irony: **anxiety pulls you out of your body and into your head.** Your body is here, in the present moment, actually safe. But your mind has raced ahead into the future, creating catastrophe. You're physically present but mentally gone. You've disconnected from the reality of *right now*—where you're actually okay—and you're living in an imagined future where everything is going wrong.

This is why grounding is so powerful for anxiety. **Grounding is the antidote to the disconnect that anxiety creates.** It brings you back from that imagined future into the actual present. It gets you out of your racing thoughts and back into your body. It interrupts the spiral by saying: *Not there. Here. Not then. Now.*

I want to share something personal. For years, I struggled with health anxiety. Every headache was a brain tumor. Every chest pain was a heart attack. Every weird sensation was something rare and deadly. I'd spend hours researching symptoms late at night, convincing myself I was dying, my heart racing, my stomach in knots.

The more I thought about it, the worse it got. My thoughts were feeding my anxiety, and my anxiety was feeding my thoughts. I was completely disconnected from reality—because the reality was, I was fine. I was healthy. But I couldn't feel that truth because I was so caught up in catastrophic thinking.

Grounding saved me. Not instantly, not magically. But every time I spiraled into health anxiety, I used grounding to come back. I'd feel my feet on the floor. I'd breathe through the panic. I'd use my senses to reconnect with the present moment where I was actually safe. Over time, my nervous system learned that it didn't need to launch into full emergency mode every time I felt a weird sensation. Grounding rewired my response to anxiety.

And I want you to know: **If anxiety is stealing your present moment, that's not a failure. It's your nervous system trying to protect you, even though it's misguided.** Grounding is how you help your nervous system understand that you're actually safe right now.

## [3:30-6:15] Section 2: The 5 Senses Grounding Technique

Let me teach you one of the most effective grounding techniques for anxiety—the 5-4-3-2-1 method. I mentioned this briefly in Part 1, but now let's go deeper.

This technique works because anxiety lives in your head—in your thoughts, your worries, your what-ifs. Your senses, on the other hand, are rooted in the present moment. What you can see, hear, touch, smell, and taste right now is real. It's happening now. It's not imagined.

By shifting your attention from your thoughts to your senses, you're literally shifting from anxiety to reality. You're interrupting the spiral and coming home to your body.

Here's how it works:

**FIVE things you can SEE**

Look around you. Name five things you can see. But don't just list them—really look at them. Notice the details. The color of the wall. The texture of the fabric on your chair. The way the light hits the table. The pattern on the carpet. Be specific. Instead of "I see a lamp," say "I see a brass lamp with a green shade and a small dent near the base." The more detail you notice, the more grounded you become.

**FOUR things you can TOUCH/FEEL**

Reach out and touch four things—or notice four sensations on your body. Feel the fabric of your shirt. Notice the smooth surface of your phone. Feel the texture of the chair arm. Run your fingers over the table. If you can't reach things, notice sensations: the watch band on your wrist, your hair against your neck, the air on your face, your feet in your shoes. Describe the texture: smooth, rough, cool, warm, soft, hard.

**THREE things you can HEAR**

Close your eyes if that helps and listen. What do you hear? Traffic outside? Birds singing? The hum of the refrigerator? Your own breath? A computer fan? Distant conversation? Don't judge the sounds—just notice them. *I hear a car passing. I hear the clock ticking. I hear my breath.*

**TWO things you can SMELL**

Take a slow breath in through your nose. What do you smell? Coffee? Soap? Laundry detergent? Nothing at all—that's okay. "Nothing" is also a smell. If you can't identify smells, that's fine too. Just notice the act of smelling. *I smell nothing, but I feel the air entering my nose.*

**ONE thing you can TASTE**

Or if you can't taste anything right now, name one thing you're grateful for—or one thing you like about yourself. This ends the practice on a positive note, shifting you away from anxiety's negativity.

The beauty of this technique is that you can do it anywhere. In a meeting. In a doctor's waiting room. In bed at night when anxiety keeps you awake. In the middle of a panic attack. No one has to know you're doing it. You can just look around, notice, breathe, and come back to yourself.

I've used this technique countless times. Sitting in doctors' offices, my heart racing, convinced something was wrong. I'd look around and name five things I could see. The exam table with its crinkly paper. The sink with the soap dispenser. The anatomical charts on the wall. The computer on the desk. The chair where I'd sit waiting. By the time I reached "one thing I can taste," my heart rate had dropped. My breathing had slowed. I was back in the room, back in reality, back in the present where I was safe.

## [6:15-9:30] Section 3: Body Scan for Grounding

Another powerful tool for anxiety is the body scan. Anxiety tends to live in specific areas of our bodies—tight chest, shallow breathing, clenched jaw, tense shoulders, knots in the stomach. A body scan helps you notice where you're holding anxiety and consciously release it.

Here's how to do it:

Start at the top of your head and slowly move down through your body, noticing any sensation without trying to change it. Tension? Tightness? Heat? Cold? Tingling? Numbness? Just notice. *My forehead feels tight. My jaw is clenched.*

As you notice each area, imagine your breath flowing into that space. With each exhale, imagine releasing tension. *Breathing into my jaw. Letting it relax. Breathing into my shoulders. Letting them drop.*

Move all the way down through your body—scalp, forehead, eyes, jaw, neck, shoulders, arms, hands, chest, belly, hips, legs, feet. Spend just a few moments on each area.

The key here is **curiosity, not judgment**. If you notice tension, don't criticize yourself for it. Anxiety causes tension—that's normal. Just notice it and offer it some compassion. *Oh, my shoulders are up around my ears. That makes sense—I'm stressed. It's okay. Let's just breathe here for a moment.*

Sometimes, just noticing where we're holding anxiety is enough to start releasing it. Awareness is the first step to change. And bringing awareness to your body brings you out of your anxious thoughts and back to your physical reality.

I remember during a particularly anxious period, I did a body scan every night before bed. My jaw was always clenched. My shoulders were always up. My chest was always tight. Night after night, I'd notice these patterns and breathe into them. Over time, I started to notice when I was tensing up during the day. I'd catch myself clenching my jaw and think: *Oh, there's that anxiety again.* Then I'd drop my shoulders, relax my jaw, and breathe. The body scan had trained me to recognize my body's anxiety signals—and respond to them with grounding instead of spiraling.

## [9:30-11:48] Section 4: Practice - Grounding Through Panic

**[Music becomes slower, more spacious - very gentle]**

Now I want to guide you through a practice specifically for when anxiety spikes. Think of this as a rehearsal—like a fire drill for your nervous system. We're going to simulate what it feels like when anxiety rises, and practice grounding through it.

If you're driving or operating machinery, please skip this practice until later. Otherwise, find a comfortable position. Let's begin.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

I want you to think of something that makes you mildly anxious—not your worst fear, but something that gives you that fluttery, worried feeling. Maybe a difficult conversation you need to have. Maybe a deadline coming up. Maybe a situation that didn't go well recently. Just let yourself feel a bit of that anxiety.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Notice where you feel it in your body. Is your heart beating faster? Is your breath getting shallow? Are your muscles tensing up? Don't try to stop it—just notice. This is what anxiety feels like. It's uncomfortable, but you're safe.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Now, let's ground. Look around the room and quickly name five things you can see. I'll wait.

**[Pause 15 seconds]**

Good. Now name four things you can feel. The texture of your clothes. The chair beneath you. The temperature of the air.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Now three things you can hear. Listen closely. What sounds can you find?

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

Two things you can smell. Breathe in through your nose.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

One thing you can taste—or one thing you're grateful for.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

Take a deep breath in... and let it out slowly.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

Notice how you feel now. Is your heart rate slower? Is your breathing deeper? Do you feel more present? You just grounded through anxiety. You interrupted the spiral. You came back to yourself.

**[Pause 10 seconds]**

I want you to remember this feeling. The next time anxiety spikes—and it will—you have this tool. You can do this anywhere, anytime. Look around. Notice your senses. Come back to the present moment where you're actually safe.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

Take one more deep breath... and when you're ready, open your eyes.

**[Pause 5 seconds]**

You handled that. You can handle this. Grounding works.

Every time you use these techniques, you're rewiring your brain. You're creating new pathways that say: *When anxiety spikes, I don't have to spiral. I can ground. I can come back to the present. I can handle this.*

It gets easier with practice. The first few times, it might feel like you're trying to hold back a tidal wave with your hands. That's okay. Keep practicing. Keep coming back to your senses, your breath, your body. Over time, the waves get smaller. Your capacity to handle them gets bigger.
